# Part 1 — Core Concepts

## 1.1 ICP vs. Buyer Persona vs. TAM/SAM/SOM

These get conflated constantly. They are not the same thing.

**Ideal Customer Profile (ICP)** describes the **company or account** that is the best-fit customer for your product. It is firmographic, environmental, and situational. It answers: *"What kind of organization should we be selling to?"*

**Buyer Persona** describes the **individual humans** inside those accounts — their roles, goals, pressures, and decision criteria. A single ICP account usually has 3–8 relevant personas: economic buyer, champion, technical evaluator, end user, blocker, influencer, procurement, legal/security. Persona work answers: *"How does this specific human think and decide?"*

**TAM / SAM / SOM** is about market sizing, not fit. TAM = total addressable market (everyone who could theoretically buy). SAM = serviceable addressable market (the slice you can actually sell to given your model). SOM = serviceable obtainable market (what you can realistically capture in a given timeframe). These are denominators; ICP is a selector that operates on top.

The causal chain: **TAM → ICP (filter) → Target Account List → Persona (per account) → Opportunity.**

Common failure: teams write a "persona" doc named "Marketing Mary" that is actually a half-baked ICP. If your "persona" doesn't have a first name, a job title, a reporting structure, and a specific goal, it's an ICP in disguise.

## 1.2 The "Fit" Vocabulary

Borrowing from Brian Balfour and others, "fit" is multi-dimensional. ICP is one axis:

- **Product-Market Fit (PMF):** the product solves a real problem for a definable market.
- **Product-Channel Fit:** the channel you use to reach customers is native to the product (e.g., PLG products need low-friction sign-up; enterprise products need outbound + events).
- **Channel-Model Fit:** the channel economics support your business model (a $50 ARPU product cannot afford field sales).
- **Model-Market Fit:** your pricing and packaging match how the market buys (usage-based, seat-based, platform fee).
- **Customer-Solution Fit:** the *specific customer* will successfully use and realize value — Lincoln Murphy's definition of ICP is essentially this.

Most "we have a sales problem" diagnoses are actually fit problems at one of these layers. ICP work without examining the other fits produces a document that feels rigorous but doesn't move revenue.

## 1.3 Why Most ICP Work Fails

Observed failure modes from the consulting trenches:

1. **Descriptive, not predictive.** The ICP describes existing customers rather than identifying *which* existing customers will be successful and expand. Everyone is "in the ICP." No one is disqualified.
2. **Marketing artifact, never operationalized.** The doc sits in a Notion page. Sales reps don't use it to disqualify. Marketing doesn't use it for targeting criteria.
3. **Firmographic only.** "Mid-market fintech in North America" is not an ICP. It's a market segment. An ICP says *why* those companies succeed with your product and *what triggers* the buying moment.
4. **No "bad fit" definition.** If you can't describe the accounts you should *decline*, you don't have an ICP.
5. **Ignores success potential.** Lincoln Murphy's central point: the criterion isn't "will they buy?" — it's "will they get value, renew, and expand?" Bad-fit customers that buy are worse than no customers.
6. **Built from sales, not customers.** The team interviews its own reps instead of actual customers. You learn what salespeople *think* the market cares about, which is not what the market cares about.
7. **Static.** ICP is revisited annually at best. Meanwhile, the product, market, and competitive landscape have shifted.

Operational rule: an ICP is only real if (a) a rep can disqualify a lead with it, (b) marketing can build a target account list from it, and (c) CS can predict retention risk from it.

# Part 2 — Foundational Thinkers

## 2.1 Lincoln Murphy — The Customer Success–Centric ICP

**Core contribution:** redefined ICP as a *success-potential* filter, not a buying-potential filter. The operative frame is "who will be successful with our product?" — not "who will buy?"

**Primary sources:**
- Blog: Sixteen Ventures (sixteenventures.com) — foundational posts on ICP, customer success, bad-fit customers.
- Book: *Customer Success: How Innovative Companies Are Reducing Churn and Growing Recurring Revenue* (co-authored with Nick Mehta and Dan Steinman).
- Industry talks and Customer Success Summit content.

### Key frameworks

**Ready, Willing, Able to be Successful (sometimes rendered RWA or RWA-Success):**
- **Ready:** the prospect has the trigger, urgency, and preconditions to act. There is a compelling event.
- **Willing:** they want to solve the problem now — not "interesting, maybe someday."
- **Able:** they have the authority, budget, technical prerequisites, and organizational capacity.
- **Successful:** if they buy, they will actually achieve their desired outcome with your product, renew, and expand.

The "Successful" leg is the one most frameworks omit. Murphy's argument: filtering for Ready/Willing/Able produces buyers; adding "Successful" produces customers who make your business work.

**Desired Outcome = Required Outcome + Appropriate Experience.** The customer has a concrete result they need (required outcome) and a way they want to achieve it (appropriate experience). Your product must deliver both to produce success.

**Bad-Fit Customers Drag You Down.** Murphy's argument for aggressive disqualification: customers who buy but cannot succeed generate support load, NPS damage, churn, negative word-of-mouth, and rep demoralization — at direct cost to your ability to serve good-fit customers. Saying no is a revenue strategy.

**The Success Gap.** The distance between what your product does and the desired outcome. Customers don't cross this gap alone; either the product, onboarding, CS, or services must. If your ICP succeeds only when you throw bodies at them, you have a success gap and the ICP is narrower than you think.

### ICP criteria categories (Murphy's taxonomy)

1. **Firmographic** — industry, size, geo, revenue, funding stage.
2. **Demographic** (persona-adjacent) — the humans who buy/use.
3. **Environmental** — market conditions, regulatory, competitive.
4. **Behavioral** — how they operate (data maturity, sales process, tech adoption).
5. **Psychographic** — values, risk tolerance, strategic priorities.
6. **Needs-based** — specific pain they are actively trying to solve.
7. **Timing / Trigger** — the compelling event creating urgency.

An ICP definition that does not include timing/trigger criteria is a segmentation doc, not an ICP.

### When to use

- Early-stage: filter the first 10–30 customers ruthlessly by "Successful" to build a retention-first foundation.
- Scaling: identify which current customers match ICP and which don't; tier the book accordingly.
- Expansion: use to define the *next* ICP as the product moves upmarket or laterally.

### Critiques / limits

Murphy's frameworks assume SaaS recurring revenue mechanics. Translating "success potential" for transactional or one-time purchase businesses requires adaptation. Also: he leans heavily on CS-led logic, which can underweight sales efficiency considerations — some accounts are hard to win but hugely successful once won.

## 2.2 April Dunford — Positioning and ICP as One Problem

**Core contribution:** positioning and ICP are the same exercise viewed from different angles. "Best-fit customers" falls out of positioning work naturally; doing ICP without positioning produces a list of accounts you can't articulate a reason to approach.

**Primary sources:**
- *Obviously Awesome: How to Nail Product Positioning So Customers Get It, Buy It, Love It* (2019).
- *Sales Pitch: How to Craft a Story to Stand Out and Win* (2023).
- Talks and workshops, especially her "Positioning workshop" material.
- Her blog and podcast appearances (e.g., on Lenny's Podcast).

### Key framework — the 5 components of positioning

Dunford's 5 building blocks of positioning:

1. **Competitive alternatives** — what the customer would use if your product didn't exist. *Not* your named competitors; the real alternatives, including "do nothing," spreadsheets, in-house tools, a consultant.
2. **Unique attributes** — features or capabilities you have that the alternatives lack.
3. **Value** — the benefit the customer gets because of those unique attributes. "Feature → Value" translation.
4. **Who cares a lot** — the segment of buyers for whom that value is significant. *This is the ICP.*
5. **Market category** — the frame of reference you want customers to evaluate you in.

Sometimes she adds **Relevant Trends** (6th) — the market forces that make your value more urgent now.

The critical move: "Who cares a lot" is derived *from* the value, not asserted upfront. You can't define ICP without knowing what unique value you deliver, which requires knowing what alternatives people use, which requires customer research. Positioning is ICP's upstream dependency.

### The *Sales Pitch* contribution

In *Sales Pitch*, Dunford extends positioning into a sales narrative structure:

1. **Insight** — a point of view about the market or problem that reframes how the buyer thinks. This is the "setup."
2. **Alternatives & their shortcomings** — honestly survey what else exists.
3. **Perfect world / ideal solution** — describe what a solution *should* have.
4. **Introduce your approach** — how you deliver that.
5. **Proof** — evidence it works.
6. **Ask for the business.**

Implication for ICP: the best-fit customer is the one who *resonates with the insight*. If your insight is "modern treasury teams need programmable payments infrastructure," the ICP is modern treasury teams — not all finance orgs.

### When to use

- Any time positioning feels "off" or the sales team uses inconsistent narratives.
- Before ICP refinement — you'll discover your ICP hypothesis was shaped by features, not value.
- When entering a new category or repositioning an existing product.

### Critiques / limits

Dunford's frameworks assume you have paying customers to interview. True pre-PMF startups will need to borrow more from customer development (Blank, Fitzpatrick) before positioning produces reliable ICP inputs. Her work also under-emphasizes pricing-market fit; positioning can be perfect while pricing destroys conversion.

## 2.3 Bob Moesta & Jobs To Be Done (JTBD)

**Core contribution:** reframes customer analysis from *who they are* (demographic/firmographic) to *what progress they're trying to make* (the job). Pairs with ICP work to answer the "why buy now?" question that firmographic filters can't.

**Primary sources:**
- *Demand-Side Sales 101: Stop Selling and Help Your Customers Make Progress* (Moesta, 2020).
- *Competing Against Luck* (Clayton Christensen, Taddy Hall, Karen Dillon, David Duncan — 2016). Moesta is an uncredited contributor to much of the thinking.
- Re-Wired Group content and podcast appearances (The Re-Wired Group, Jobs-to-be-Done Radio).
- The Milkshake/Snickers case studies from Christensen's original work.

### Core principle

*"People don't buy products; they hire them to do a job."*

A "job" is defined as the progress a person is trying to make in a particular circumstance. Same person, different circumstance = different job = different purchase behavior. Demographics predict poorly; circumstance + desired progress predicts well.

### The Four Forces of Progress

Moesta's central diagnostic tool for understanding a switching decision:

1. **Push of the situation** — what about the current state is making the person uncomfortable? (Pain with the status quo.)
2. **Pull of the new solution** — what about the new product attracts them? (Promise of the future.)
3. **Anxiety of the new** — what fears do they have about switching? (Uncertainty, implementation risk, career risk.)
4. **Habit of the present** — what inertia keeps them where they are? (Sunk cost, comfort, relationships.)

Forces 1+2 drive adoption. Forces 3+4 resist it. A purchase happens only when the sum of 1+2 exceeds 3+4. Most ICP definitions ignore 3 and 4 entirely, which is why "perfect ICP" accounts don't convert.

### The JTBD Interview Method

Moesta's forensic interview technique reconstructs the *timeline of the switch*:

- **First thought** — when did they first realize something had to change? (Often months before they took action.)
- **Passive looking** — what prompted them to start noticing alternatives?
- **Active looking** — what made them commit time to evaluation?
- **Decision** — what was the moment of commitment? Who else was involved?
- **First use** — did the product actually deliver progress?

The goal is to reconstruct events, not collect opinions. "Tell me about the day you decided to buy X" produces far better data than "why do you use X?"

### Related: Outcome-Driven Innovation (Tony Ulwick)

A sibling school within JTBD, Ulwick's ODI (*Jobs to be Done: Theory to Practice*, *What Customers Want*) argues for a more quantitative approach: decompose the job into 50–150 measurable outcome statements, survey customers on importance and satisfaction, identify under-served outcomes. More rigorous, more expensive, better suited to product strategy than early-stage sales. Moesta's approach is lighter and better suited to GTM and messaging.

### When to use

- When firmographically identical accounts convert at wildly different rates — JTBD reveals the circumstance that differentiates them.
- When crafting messaging and sales narratives — JTBD produces the "insight" that Dunford's sales pitch needs.
- When prioritizing features or product roadmap against customer reality.
- When your "ICP" feels correct but conversion is poor — usually a Push/Anxiety mismatch.

### Critiques / limits

JTBD interviews are craft-heavy. Done poorly, they produce just-so stories. Moesta himself acknowledges most teams take 10–20 interviews to develop real skill. Also: JTBD under-emphasizes the buying committee in B2B — a single "job" can have five people with five different anxieties.

## 2.4 Rob Fitzpatrick — *The Mom Test*

**Core contribution:** a short, tactical book on how to do customer interviews that produce truth rather than flattery. The foundational reference for anyone doing discovery work.

**Primary source:**
- *The Mom Test: How to talk to customers & learn if your business is a good idea when everyone is lying to you* (Fitzpatrick, 2013).
- Companion: *The Workshop Survival Guide* (2019), *Write Useful Books* (2021) — less relevant but same author.

### The three core rules

1. **Talk about their life, not your idea.** The moment you describe your product, you've poisoned the well — they'll tell you what they think you want to hear.
2. **Ask about specifics in the past, not generics or opinions about the future.** "Would you use X?" is a useless question. "The last time you had problem Y, what did you do?" is gold. Future hypotheticals are fantasy; past behavior is data.
3. **Talk less, listen more.** Aim for 70%+ of the words to come from the interviewee.

### Signal quality taxonomy

Fitzpatrick distinguishes signal from noise rigorously:

**Bad signals (what feels good but isn't):**
- Compliments ("Great idea!")
- Fluff ("I would definitely use that.")
- Generics ("People usually...")
- Opinions about the future ("I'd pay $X for that.")

**Good signals (what actually matters):**
- Specific stories about past behavior.
- Facts and numbers about their current workflow, cost, time spent.
- Emotion expressed about actual lived pain.
- **Commitments** — time, money, reputation, or an introduction. *The strongest signal.*
- **Next steps** — a concrete, scheduled follow-up action.

### The Commitment/Advancement framework

Every meeting should end in either:
- A **commitment** — they've given something of value (money, a formal intro, meaningful time), or
- An **advancement** — a scheduled next step that increases commitment over time.

If the meeting ends in "this was great, let's keep in touch," it failed. You learned nothing about real interest.

### Cold vs warm interviews

Fitzpatrick's practical advice:
- **Warm conversations** are always better. Leverage introductions.
- **Cold interviews** are possible but expensive; frame them as research, not sales.
- **Industry events, conferences, and meetups** are the best cold source.
- **"Can I buy you a coffee to learn about your role?"** outperforms "Can I show you our product?"

### When to use

- Before any ICP work — you need raw customer data, and the Mom Test governs quality.
- At every product/GTM milestone. Teams stop interviewing at their peril.
- When validating a new segment or geography.
- When a sales pattern isn't working and you need to understand why.

### Critiques / limits

The book is short by design and skips some topics (statistical rigor in larger sample studies, survey design, synthesis techniques). Pair with CXL / Laja content for quantitative methods. Also: B2B discovery with buying committees needs adaptation — the "mom test" framing is rooted in solo founder + individual consumer interviews.

## 2.5 Peep Laja & CXL — Research Rigor

**Core contribution:** applies CRO (conversion rate optimization) research discipline to GTM questions. Where Fitzpatrick is qualitative craft, CXL adds quantitative methodology and research synthesis.

**Primary sources:**
- CXL blog and CXL Institute courses (cxl.com).
- Peep Laja's Wynter (B2B message testing platform) content.
- Laja's *The Growth Equation* and CXL's minidegrees (particularly the "Digital Analytics" and "Conversion Optimization" tracks).

### The ResearchXL framework

CXL's conversion research framework, adaptable to ICP/positioning work:

1. **Heuristic analysis** — expert review of messaging, site, product for friction.
2. **Web analytics** — behavioral data on what's actually happening (who visits, who converts).
3. **Qualitative (user testing)** — watch humans try to use/buy the product; note where they get stuck.
4. **Mouse tracking & session recordings** — behavioral micro-evidence.
5. **Surveys** — structured quantitative input from target audience.
6. **Voice of Customer (VoC)** — pull quotes, support tickets, sales calls, reviews — customer language used for messaging.
7. **Technical analysis** — site performance, cross-browser, mobile.

For GTM/ICP work, #3, #5, and #6 are the most load-bearing. VoC in particular is under-used: the language your best customers use to describe the problem *is* your messaging copy, and it's free.

### Message testing (Wynter)

Laja's Wynter platform operationalizes message testing with ICP panels: you write copy, show it to qualified buyers, and get structured feedback on clarity, relevance, and value perception. This is one of the few ways to quantitatively validate positioning before launch.

### Survey design principles (CXL)

- Ask about past behavior, not future intent (echoes Fitzpatrick).
- Open-ended questions for discovery; closed for validation.
- "What almost stopped you from buying?" is the single highest-leverage survey question.
- "What other options did you consider?" maps competitive alternatives (feeds Dunford).
- Segment responses by outcome (churned vs retained, expanded vs flat) to find ICP signal.

### When to use

- When you have enough customers (>100) to run quantitative studies meaningfully.
- When messaging is underperforming and you need rigor beyond anecdote.
- When a qualitative pattern needs validation before you commit to it in positioning.

### Critiques / limits

CXL content is heavily CRO-flavored and can feel conversion-funnel-narrow for strategic ICP work. Most useful as a methodology layer under the strategic frameworks (Murphy, Dunford, Moesta).

## 2.6 Winning by Design (Jacco van der Kooij) — Operationalized Go-to-Market

**Core contribution:** turns GTM into an engineering discipline with named frameworks, scoring rubrics, and process diagrams. Their free public content is higher quality than most paid GTM education.

**Primary sources:**
- Winning by Design blog and YouTube channel (winningbydesign.com).
- *SaaS Sales Method* series (books by Jacco van der Kooij and Dan Smith).
- *Revenue Architecture* framework and associated courses.
- Free public playbooks and frameworks.

### The Bowtie Model

WbD's rethinking of the sales funnel. The traditional funnel ends at purchase; the bowtie continues:

```
AWARE → ENGAGED → EVALUATE → [PURCHASE] → ONBOARD → IMPACT → EXPAND → RENEW
                              |----------------------|
                              Pre-sale (traditional funnel)
                                                     |-----------------------------|
                                                     Post-sale (inverted funnel / expansion)
```

The "tie" is where revenue is actually made long-term: onboarding → impact → expansion → renewal. A narrow mid-funnel conversion metric misses the whole revenue story.

Implication for ICP: the ICP must be defined with the post-sale half in mind. "Accounts that buy but don't reach Impact" are not ICP even if they closed.

### SPICED Framework

WbD's discovery/qualification framework (an evolution of MEDDIC/BANT):

- **S**ituation — what's their current state?
- **P**ain — what's the explicit problem?
- **I**mpact — what's the measurable cost of the pain?
- **C**ritical Event — what forces action by when?
- **D**ecision — who decides, how, and on what criteria?

Good discovery produces all five. ICP fit should be verifiable through SPICED: if a "perfect ICP" account has no Critical Event, it's not buying this cycle no matter how perfect the fit.

### ICP Scoring Framework

WbD advocates weighted scoring across:
- Firmographic fit (industry, size)
- Technographic fit (tech stack match)
- Need fit (specific pain they have)
- Buying posture (in-market signals)
- Success potential (ability to reach Impact)

Each account scored 1–5 on each dimension, weights set by revenue correlation analysis of existing customers. Accounts scoring below threshold are not worked by sales — which is the operational test of whether the ICP is real.

### Revenue Architecture

The broader framework positioning Marketing / Sales / CS as one revenue-generating system with shared metrics. ICP is the *unifying customer definition* across the three functions. When Sales, Marketing, and CS have three different operational ICPs, the system leaks revenue at every handoff.

### When to use

- When the organization is scaling and needs operational definitions of "good lead."
- When post-sale is treated as cost center and churn is hiding real GTM problems.
- When the three functions (Sales/Marketing/CS) are misaligned on who's a good customer.

### Critiques / limits

WbD frameworks can feel over-engineered for early-stage companies with <50 customers. Much of the statistical reasoning requires volume. They also lean heavily on recurring-revenue SaaS mechanics; adaptation required for transactional, marketplace, or fintech-embedded models.

## 2.7 6sense / Latané Conant — Account-Based Revenue & the Dark Funnel

**Core contribution:** reframes ABM around the reality that most B2B research happens anonymously (the "dark funnel"), and that intent data + predictive modeling can identify in-market accounts before they fill out a form.

**Primary sources:**
- *No Forms. No Spam. No Cold Calls.* (Latané Conant, 2020; 2nd ed 2023).
- 6sense blog, research reports, and "Revenue AI" playbooks.
- Forrester/Gartner ABM research cited by 6sense.
- Podcasts: *CMO Coffee Talk*, Conant's speaking appearances.

### Core principle — the Dark Funnel

The claim: ~70% of the B2B buying journey happens before the buyer identifies themselves. They research on G2, read analyst reports, visit your site anonymously, discuss internally — all invisible to traditional marketing. By the time they "convert" (fill a form), the shortlist is often set.

Implication: waiting for leads to self-identify = losing 70% of the market before you engage. You must detect intent signals externally and prioritize accounts that are researching now.

### The Six-Stage Account Journey

6sense's rethinking of the funnel at the account level:

1. **Target** — fits ICP, no activity.
2. **Awareness** — early signals of research (site visits, topic searches).
3. **Consideration** — deeper research, multiple people involved.
4. **Decision** — evaluation behavior, competitive comparison.
5. **Purchase** — in active deal.
6. **Post-Purchase** — expansion, advocacy.

Sales resources concentrate on stages 3–4 (accounts in-market). Marketing nurtures 1–2. This is a coordination map, not just a funnel.

### The Latané Conant arguments

From *No Forms. No Spam. No Cold Calls.*:

- **"Uncovered demand" > "generated demand."** Your job isn't to create demand; it's to find where it already exists and be there first.
- **Account, not lead.** Leads are people; buying is done by accounts. Score, track, and prioritize at the account level.
- **Orchestration over sequence.** Instead of a linear cadence, run coordinated plays across marketing touches, ads, outbound, and direct mail — timed to in-market signals.
- **The "Everyday Admins"** — the marketing ops team that makes this run. Conant is unusually blunt about the operational headcount required.

### When to use

- Target accounts are identifiable and finite (true ABM applicability).
- Deal sizes justify account-level orchestration ($50k+ ACV as a rough floor).
- You have the martech stack to operationalize intent data (or willing to build it).
- Long, considered sales cycles with buying committees.

### Critiques / limits

6sense sells intent data and has obvious commercial interest in the "dark funnel is huge" framing. The 70% stat is directional, not precise. Also: ABM economics require sufficient deal size; applying Conant's playbook to a $5k ACV product destroys margins. For PLG or transactional products, much of this framework is overkill.

## 2.8 Geoffrey Moore — *Crossing the Chasm*

**Core contribution:** the foundational framework for thinking about ICP as a *beachhead* — the small, specific segment you must dominate before expanding. Predates most modern GTM literature but underpins it.

**Primary sources:**
- *Crossing the Chasm: Marketing and Selling High-Tech Products to Mainstream Customers* (Moore, 1991; 3rd ed 2014).
- *Inside the Tornado*, *Zone to Win*.

### Core concepts

**Technology Adoption Lifecycle (from Rogers, popularized by Moore):** Innovators → Early Adopters → Early Majority → Late Majority → Laggards. Moore's addition: there is a **chasm** between Early Adopters and Early Majority that kills most technology companies.

**Why the chasm exists:** Early Adopters are visionaries who buy on promise. Early Majority are pragmatists who buy on references from other pragmatists in their own industry. They don't trust Early Adopter case studies. A product that works for Early Adopters can stall indefinitely trying to reach pragmatists.

**The beachhead strategy:** to cross the chasm, pick *one* narrow vertical or use case where you can dominate so completely that the Early Majority in that niche has no reason to consider alternatives. Win 100% of a small segment rather than 5% of a large one. Use that beachhead to generate the references, integrations, and ecosystem that let you expand.

**Target Customer Characterization:** Moore's pre-ICP ICP exercise — a structured scenario describing the target customer's day, their pain, the compelling event, the buying process, and the value delivered. Predecessor to modern persona + ICP work.

### Implication for ICP work

Most modern ICP frameworks assume the beachhead has been chosen. Moore reminds you the *choice of beachhead* is the strategic decision. A perfectly-defined ICP in a chasm-trapped segment beats nothing.

### When to use

- Early-stage: choosing the beachhead is the core strategic question.
- Scaling products that are stuck — often a chasm problem disguised as a "sales execution" problem.
- Category-defining moves — you need Moore's frameworks for the "tornado" that follows a successful crossing.

### Critiques / limits

Published 1991; examples are dated. The "chasm" is less bimodal in modern software where product-led motion can blur the visionary/pragmatist distinction. Still canonical for category strategy.

## 2.9 Brian Balfour — Four Fits

**Core contribution:** frames growth strategy as the alignment of four interdependent fits. ICP is a necessary but insufficient element of a working growth model.

**Primary source:**
- Reforge essays, particularly "Why Product Market Fit Isn't Enough" and the Four Fits framework.
- Reforge programs (Growth Series, Growth Models, etc.).

### The Four Fits

1. **Market ↔ Product** — the classic PMF: the product solves a real problem for a definable market.
2. **Product ↔ Channel** — the product's characteristics must match the channel's requirements. (Example: a complex enterprise product cannot grow via self-serve social virality.)
3. **Channel ↔ Model** — the economics of the channel must support the business model. (Example: paid social at $200 CAC is incompatible with a $50 ARPU product.)
4. **Model ↔ Market** — the revenue model must match how the market buys. (Example: seat-based pricing fails in markets where buyer counts people differently.)

All four must align. A change to one requires rechecking the others. Many "growth problems" are fit-alignment problems disguised as execution problems.

### Implication for ICP work

An ICP is only valid in the context of specific channels and models. "We're expanding ICP upmarket" is a statement that implies channel and model changes — often overlooked. When ICP expansion fails, it's usually because one of the other three fits broke.

### When to use

- Diagnosing stalled growth.
- Planning a segment expansion (upmarket, downmarket, new geo, new vertical).
- Evaluating acquisition of an adjacent product line.

### Critiques / limits

Highly abstract. Requires translation into operational work. Balfour's frameworks assume growth-stage discipline; early-stage founders often need more tactical tools first.

## 2.10 Supporting Voices — Faster Skim

### Elena Verna (growth / PLG)
- Practitioner, advisor, and writer. Substack and Lenny's Newsletter guest posts.
- Expert on B2B product-led growth, user-led vs marketing-led, self-serve mechanics.
- Use for: how ICP changes in a PLG motion (often "the first user in the account" matters more than the firmographic fit).

# Part 3 — Fintech / Payments / B2B Addendum

Generic B2B ICP frameworks under-serve fintech and payments because these verticals have structural peculiarities most thinkers haven't worked in. Additions and modifications:

## 3.1 The fintech buying committee is larger and weirder

A typical fintech/payments sale has *at minimum*:

- **Economic buyer** — CFO, Head of Payments, VP Treasury, VP Finance Ops.
- **Champion** — often a senior ops/payments manager who owns the pain.
- **Technical evaluator** — Engineering lead, frequently with payments domain expertise.
- **Risk & Compliance** — Risk Officer, Compliance, Fraud Ops. *Frequent deal killer.*
- **Legal & Regulatory** — data residency, PCI scope, BSA/AML implications, state money transmitter exposure.
- **Security** — SOC 2, pen tests, SIG questionnaires, cloud architecture review.
- **Procurement / Vendor Management** — master services agreements, vendor risk, third-party oversight (especially in regulated institutions).
- **Internal Audit** (for enterprise / bank buyers) — control mapping.

ICP work that defines only firmographic + economic buyer misses the people who actually kill deals. A working fintech ICP includes **a map of the likely blocker personas** and their disqualifying criteria.

## 3.2 Regulatory fit is a hard ICP filter

Some accounts are simply not sellable to without a specific compliance posture on your side. Questions to include in the ICP:

- Do they require **SOC 2 Type II**, **PCI-DSS**, **ISO 27001**, **HIPAA**, **HITRUST**?
- Do they require **data residency** (US-only, EU-only, country-specific)?
- Are they a **regulated financial institution** under OCC / FDIC / NCUA / FinCEN / state banking department supervision? (If yes: third-party risk management / vendor oversight requirements apply.)
- What is their **BSA/AML** posture? Do they need a vendor that supports CIP, sanctions screening, SAR integration?
- Are they subject to **Reg E, Reg Z, Reg CC**, the **Durbin amendment**, or **Nacha** operating rules as a purchaser?
- International: **PSD2 / SCA**, **GDPR**, **MiCA**, **Open Banking** variants?

An ICP that doesn't filter on regulatory fit produces deals that die in security review. Filter early.

## 3.3 "Ready" means something specific in fintech

Murphy's "Ready" criterion in fintech usually means one of:

- **A compliance event** (audit finding, regulatory exam, new rule) forcing change by a deadline.
- **A vendor change** (incumbent provider sunset, pricing change, M&A-forced migration).
- **A volume threshold** crossed (new processor needed, new unit economics required).
- **A fraud / loss event** forcing a risk-tool purchase.
- **A strategic program** (new product launch, new geo, new payment rail) requiring capability.

Timing criteria for fintech ICP should name these triggers explicitly. Without a named trigger, the deal is not in-market regardless of fit.

## 3.4 Unique attributes that matter disproportionately in fintech

When applying Dunford's framework, these unique-attribute categories are especially load-bearing in fintech/payments:

- **Licensing / network membership** (money transmitter licenses, card network sponsorship, ACH access, Fedwire/FedNow connectivity, RTP network participant).
- **Certifications** (PCI Level 1, ISO 20022 readiness, Nacha Preferred Partner).
- **Bank partnerships / BaaS posture** (sponsor bank relationships, chartered status).
- **Settlement speed / economics** (same-day ACH, RTP, instant, FX spreads).
- **Programmable primitives** (API completeness, webhook reliability, SDK maturity).
- **Dispute / chargeback handling** (automated, integrated, compliant with Reg E timelines).
- **Reconciliation / reporting** (GL-ready outputs, audit trails, ASC 606 support).

If none of your "unique attributes" live in this list, your positioning in fintech is probably commoditized.

## 4.2 Discovery Interview Question Bank

Blended from The Mom Test (Fitzpatrick), JTBD (Moesta), and CXL research methods. Adapt and pare to 8–12 questions per interview; never ask all of these.

### Opening / context
1. Can you walk me through your role and what your team is accountable for?
2. What are the top 2–3 outcomes you're personally measured on this year?
3. Tell me about a typical week — what's the mix of things you're doing?

### The switching moment (JTBD — only if they've adopted a recent solution)
4. Tell me about the day you first realized you needed to solve [problem X]. What happened?
5. Between that first thought and actually deciding to look for a solution, what changed?
6. When you started actively evaluating options, what were you looking at? In what order did you find them?
7. Who else was involved in the decision? What was each person's role?
8. What almost caused you to not buy? What were you worried about?
9. Can you walk me through the first two weeks of actually using it? What went well? What was harder than expected?

### Current state pain (Mom Test — past specifics)
10. The last time [problem X] happened, what did you do?
11. How often does that happen in a typical month? How long does it take you each time?
12. Roughly, what's the cost — time, money, team morale — of that problem over a quarter?
13. What have you tried before to fix it? What happened?
14. Is anyone else on the team actively working on this? Who?

### Buying process (SPICED mapping)
15. If you decided to solve this, who would need to be involved to approve a purchase?
16. What would cause this to become a must-do-now priority rather than a nice-to-have?
17. What's on your team's roadmap for the next 2 quarters that might force action here?
18. What's the budget process — is there a line item for this already, or would it be carved out?

### Competitive alternatives (Dunford)
19. If you had to solve this today without any new vendor, how would you do it?
20. What vendors or approaches have you heard about in this space?
21. What would be the "boring" default choice if you had to pick one tomorrow?

### Success definition (Murphy)
22. Imagine it's a year from now and this is working perfectly. What's different?
23. How would you know it was working? What would you measure?
24. What would have to be true about the vendor/product for you to be successful?

### Closing / commitment test (Mom Test)
25. Who else should I be talking to about this?
26. Would you be willing to introduce me to [specific person role]?
27. If a product existed that did [specific described capability], would you want to see it? [If yes:] what would be a good next step?

**Notes on use:**
- Budget ~45 min. Expect to cover 10–12 of these.
- Record (with permission) and transcribe — don't rely on notes.
- After 5 interviews, look for patterns; after 10, you should have draft themes; after 15–20 in a segment, themes stabilize.
- Segment interviewees by outcome (successful customer, churned customer, lost deal, current prospect, non-buyer) — each yields different truth.

## 4.4 Disqualification Framework

The underused half of ICP work. Saying no is a revenue strategy.

### Three tiers of "no"

1. **Hard disqualifiers (never sell):**
   - Regulatory posture we can't meet (e.g., they require data residency we don't offer).
   - Technology prerequisite absent (e.g., they run on a system we don't integrate with, won't for 18+ months).
   - Fundamental model mismatch (e.g., they buy via RFP-only procurement and we don't do RFPs).
   - Industry we refuse on risk / reputation grounds.

2. **Soft disqualifiers (not now, not us alone):**
   - Deal size below profitable floor → route to partner or self-serve.
   - Complexity exceeds current services capacity → defer until capacity exists.
   - Timing: no trigger, no budget, no champion → long-cycle nurture, not active sales.

3. **Yellow flags (proceed with eyes open):**
   - Only one champion, no backup → build coach before proposal.
   - Budget exists but not allocated → validate with procurement early.
   - Previously failed with a competitor → understand why before committing.

### Disqualification script for reps

> "Based on what you've shared, I don't think we're the right fit for [specific reason]. Here's what I'd suggest instead: [partner / alternative / revisit in 6 months]. If that situation changes — specifically [trigger condition] — I'd love to reconnect."

This is hard. Reps don't want to kill pipeline. But bad-fit deals consume 2–3× the rep time and 5–10× the CS time of good-fit deals, and they churn. The highest-leverage GTM intervention at many companies is *training reps to disqualify*, not to close harder.

### Disqualification as a leading indicator

Track the disqualification rate. If it's below ~20% at the SDR stage, the ICP isn't doing operational work. If it's above ~70%, the market definition is too broad or messaging is attracting the wrong audience. The sweet spot varies by motion; for most mid-market B2B, 30–50% SDR-stage disqualification is healthy.

# Glossary

- **ABM (Account-Based Marketing):** marketing strategy focused on specific target accounts rather than broad demand generation.
- **ARPU (Average Revenue Per User):** recurring revenue divided by active customers/users.
- **Beachhead:** a narrow initial target segment (Moore); dominate here first, expand later.
- **Bowtie (model):** Winning by Design's extended funnel including post-sale expansion (see 2.6).
- **Champion:** internal advocate at the prospect who drives the deal.
- **Compelling event / trigger:** the time-bound reason a prospect must act now.
- **Dark funnel:** the portion of the buyer journey that occurs anonymously, before lead capture (6sense).
- **Demand-side selling:** Moesta's term for selling framed around the buyer's progress, not the seller's features.
- **Disqualifier:** a criterion that, if present, removes a prospect from active pipeline.
- **Economic buyer:** the person with budget authority to approve the purchase.
- **Firmographic:** company-level attributes (industry, size, geo).
- **Four Fits:** Brian Balfour's framework (Market-Product, Product-Channel, Channel-Model, Model-Market).
- **Four Forces of Progress:** Moesta's JTBD diagnostic (Push, Pull, Anxiety, Habit).
- **ICP (Ideal Customer Profile):** account-level description of the best-fit customer.
- **Jobs To Be Done (JTBD):** framework defining customer behavior by the progress they seek in a circumstance.
- **MEDDIC / MEDDPICC:** sales qualification framework (Metrics, Economic buyer, Decision criteria, Decision process, Identify pain, Champion, Competition).
- **Persona (Buyer Persona):** individual-level description of a decision-maker or user archetype.
- **PLG (Product-Led Growth):** growth motion where the product is the primary acquisition and expansion channel.
- **PMF (Product-Market Fit):** the product meaningfully solves a problem for a defined market.
- **Psychographic:** attitudes, values, priorities of a buyer or organization.
- **RWAS (Ready, Willing, Able, Successful):** Lincoln Murphy's ICP criteria set.
- **SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market):** the portion of TAM your model can actually serve.
- **SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market):** the portion of SAM you can realistically capture.
- **SPICED:** Winning by Design's discovery framework (Situation, Pain, Impact, Critical event, Decision).
- **TAM (Total Addressable Market):** the total theoretical market size.
- **Target Account List (TAL):** operationalized list of named accounts fitting the ICP.
- **Technographic:** technology-stack attributes used as fit criteria.
- **VoC (Voice of Customer):** unfiltered customer language (reviews, transcripts, tickets) used for research and messaging.
