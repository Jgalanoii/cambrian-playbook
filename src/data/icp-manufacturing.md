# ICP — Manufacturing / Industrial Playbook

> Vertical playbook for companies selling to manufacturers, industrial operators, or industrial SaaS buyers. Applies to both software/SaaS sold *into* manufacturing and industrial/product companies selling in B2B manufacturing markets.

## 1. What makes manufacturing distinct

Four structural features set manufacturing apart from software-sold-to-software:

**Long decision cycles, capital-intensive purchases.** Manufacturing buyers have multi-quarter to multi-year evaluation cycles, especially for anything touching production. Capital equipment purchases run 18–36 months. Even software sold into plants often requires 6–12 months and a pilot.

**Operational risk dominates.** A new software tool that takes down a production line is a multi-million-dollar event. Buyers are risk-averse by job description; "moving fast and breaking things" is a non-starter. Reference customers in the exact same plant/process type are disproportionately valuable.

**Decision-makers are distributed.** Plant managers, operations VPs, IT, engineering, quality, safety, supply chain, finance, and sometimes union representation all have a say. Manufacturing org charts are flatter at the plant level than enterprise software buyers expect; the line manager has real influence.

**Digital maturity varies enormously.** "Manufacturing" spans Tier-1 automotive suppliers with smart factories and fully-instrumented lines to mid-market job shops still running paper travelers. The same ICP won't fit both.

## 2. Manufacturing is not one market

Useful subdivisions (ICP depends on which you sell to):

**By production type:**
- **Discrete manufacturing** — distinct units (autos, electronics, appliances, machinery).
- **Process manufacturing** — continuous or batch (chemicals, pharma, food/beverage, pulp/paper).
- **Hybrid** — both, often within one company.

**By customer type:**
- **OEMs** — design and sell the final product.
- **Tier-1 / Tier-2 / Tier-3 suppliers** — supply chain position; Tier-1 sells to OEMs directly.
- **Contract manufacturers (CMs / EMS)** — produce for other companies' brands.
- **Private-label / white-label manufacturers**.

**By scale & market:**
- **Enterprise / Tier-1** — $1B+ revenue, multi-plant, global.
- **Mid-market** — $100M–$1B, 2–10 plants, regional.
- **SMB / job shop** — <$100M, 1–2 facilities, often specialized.

**By vertical:**
Automotive, aerospace & defense, food & beverage, pharma, chemicals, industrial machinery, electronics, consumer goods, medical devices, energy/utilities, metals, building products. Each has distinct compliance, buying patterns, and vocabulary.

## 3. ICP patterns

Slicing dimensions that matter:

- **Firmographic**: revenue, plant count, plant size (employee and volume), geography, vertical.
- **Production type**: discrete/process/hybrid; batch/continuous; make-to-order vs. make-to-stock.
- **Digital maturity**: ERP in place (SAP/Oracle/Infor/Microsoft/Epicor/Plex/IQMS); MES present; IoT/sensor deployment; data historian (PI, Wonderware, Ignition); cloud posture.
- **Supply chain posture**: JIT vs. buffer-heavy, single-source vs. diversified, recent disruption exposure.
- **Regulatory environment**: FDA (pharma/medical/food), ITAR/EAR (aerospace/defense), ISO 9001/14001/45001, IATF 16949 (automotive), FSMA, GFSI, AS9100.
- **Union vs. non-union**: changes implementation dynamics, especially for productivity or monitoring tools.
- **Capital intensity**: high-capex industries move slower but buy bigger.
- **Current vendor landscape**: incumbent software vendors, consulting relationships, SI partnerships.

## 4. The buying committee

Manufacturing committees are larger and more operationally weighted than typical B2B software:

- **Economic buyer** — CFO, COO, VP Operations, VP Supply Chain, GM (at plant level for plant-specific tools).
- **Champion** — Plant Manager, Operations Director, Engineering Lead, Continuous Improvement Manager.
- **End users** — Floor operators, supervisors, maintenance staff, quality personnel. Their buy-in dictates success.
- **IT / OT** — Plant IT, corporate IT, OT (operational technology) team. **Historically separate; convergence is a buying trigger.**
- **Engineering** — Manufacturing engineering, process engineering, controls engineering. Technical eval gate.
- **Quality** — owns ISO/regulatory compliance; frequent blocker.
- **Safety / EHS** — for tools touching safety-critical processes.
- **Supply chain / procurement** — vendor qualification process; long forms (SRM systems, audits).
- **Finance / accounting** — capex approval (often separate from software opex).
- **Union / works council** — where present, must be engaged for workforce-facing tools.
- **Corporate vs. plant decision rights** — often conflicting; multi-plant rollouts require both.

Unique pattern: **"Plants hate being told what to do by corporate."** Successful multi-plant rollouts win plant-by-plant buy-in even if corporate signed the contract. ICP should include "organization has corporate-plant alignment" as a maturity filter.

## 5. Trigger events

Manufacturing-specific compelling events:

- **Supply chain disruption** — shortage, tariff, geopolitics, natural disaster, pandemic aftershock.
- **ERP upgrade or migration** — SAP S/4HANA migration, Oracle Fusion, Epicor Kinetic, Plex, Netsuite ERP. Adjacent tools are reconsidered during ERP projects.
- **Plant expansion or greenfield** — new facility triggers new vendor decisions.
- **M&A integration** — acquirer standardizes tools across acquired plants.
- **Regulatory action** — FDA warning letter, recall, ISO non-conformance, safety incident, environmental issue.
- **Labor shortage** — persistent hiring difficulty triggers automation and digital-work-instructions buying.
- **Quality event** — major recall, customer complaint escalation, OEE (Overall Equipment Effectiveness) below target.
- **Tariff / trade policy shift** — forces sourcing changes and supply-chain visibility investment.
- **Continuous improvement / Lean program** — organizational initiative opens budget.
- **Executive change** — new CEO, COO, VP Operations, or Plant Manager (first 6–12 months).
- **Industry 4.0 / digital transformation mandate** — board- or private-equity-driven initiative.
- **Customer compliance demand** — a major customer requires a new certification, traceability capability, or EDI format.

Manufacturing deals move on operational urgency. Without an operational trigger, they slip indefinitely.

## 6. Positioning — unique-attribute categories

Durable differentiation in industrial/manufacturing markets:

- **Industry / process specificity** — "built for discrete automotive Tier-1" beats "flexible platform" every time.
- **Integration with industrial systems** — PLC, SCADA, historian, MES, ERP. Named integrations with specific vendor versions.
- **Deployment model** — on-prem/edge for production-critical, cloud for analytics. Hybrid is the norm.
- **Regulatory validation** — FDA 21 CFR Part 11, validation packages, IQ/OQ/PQ support.
- **Operational resilience** — offline capability, latency guarantees, failover behavior.
- **Connectivity to legacy** — ability to work with older PLCs, proprietary protocols, Modbus/Profibus/EtherNet/IP.
- **ROI framing in manufacturing KPIs** — OEE, first-pass yield, MTBF, MTTR, scrap rate, cycle time, labor hours per unit.
- **Reference plants** — named, similar-process customers willing to be visited.
- **Implementation partners** — SI partnerships with names plant managers trust (Deloitte, Accenture, Cognizant, regional specialists).

Positioning without named process-type specificity loses to positioning with it. The manufacturer buying DCS or MES tools does not care about "digital transformation" — they care about cycle time on stamping line #7.

## 7. Pricing & model patterns

Manufacturing pricing often hybrid:

- **Capex / perpetual license** — still common for on-prem and embedded software.
- **Subscription / SaaS** — increasingly standard for analytics, dashboards, cloud MES.
- **Per-plant / per-site fees** — common for multi-plant platforms.
- **Per-machine / per-asset / per-line** — common for monitoring, predictive maintenance, OEE tools.
- **Per-user** — for engineering/office tools but rarely for plant-floor tools.
- **Platform + implementation + services** — the services component is large and strategic; it's not an afterthought.
- **Outcome-based pricing** — ascending in predictive maintenance and energy optimization; "share of savings" models.

Implementation services are often 1–3x the first year's license revenue. ICP work must include: "does this buyer have the implementation partner relationships or internal capability to succeed?"

## 8. Compliance / regulatory overlay

By vertical:

- **Automotive**: IATF 16949, PPAP, APQP.
- **Aerospace / defense**: AS9100, ITAR, EAR, CMMC (US DoD), NADCAP.
- **Food & beverage**: FSMA, HACCP, GFSI (SQF, BRC, FSSC 22000), FDA, USDA.
- **Pharma / medical device**: FDA 21 CFR Part 11 / Part 820 / Part 211, EU MDR, ISO 13485, GxP validation.
- **Chemicals**: OSHA PSM, EPA RMP, REACH, RoHS, ISO 14001.
- **Electronics**: RoHS, REACH, IPC standards, conflict minerals reporting.
- **General quality**: ISO 9001.
- **General environmental / safety**: ISO 14001, ISO 45001.

Validation burden: for regulated manufacturing (pharma, medical device, aerospace), software validation is a significant cost and timeline consideration. Vendors who provide validation packages and IQ/OQ/PQ support close faster in those verticals.

## 9. Archetypal ICP slices

**A — "Mid-market discrete manufacturer, digital transformation initiative"**
US-based discrete manufacturer, $200M–$1B revenue, 2–10 plants, has named digital transformation or Industry 4.0 initiative with executive sponsor, recent SAP/Oracle ERP migration or upgrade in last 3 years, OEE visibility is a stated gap, has Continuous Improvement/Lean program. Trigger: labor shortage, OEE below target, or M&A integration.

**B — "Tier-1 automotive supplier, quality-driven"**
Tier-1 automotive supplier, $500M–$5B revenue, serves major OEMs (GM/Ford/Stellantis/Toyota/VW/Tesla), IATF 16949 certified, named Quality Director with decision authority, currently managing PPAP and quality data via spreadsheets or legacy QMS. Trigger: customer complaint escalation, failed audit, or new platform launch.

**C — "Process manufacturer with historian-heavy data"**
Chemical, oil & gas, pulp & paper, or food & beverage process manufacturer, $1B+ revenue, running OSIsoft PI or Wonderware/AVEVA historian, has named Digital Operations leader, wants to move from descriptive to predictive analytics, recent merger or divestiture creating data fragmentation. Trigger: operational loss event, new digital leader, or cost-reduction mandate.

**D — "Contract manufacturer / EMS"**
Electronics or contract manufacturer, $100M–$1B, high product-mix complexity, serves multiple brand-owner customers with different compliance demands, struggles with visibility across customer orders. Trigger: major customer demands new traceability or reporting; capacity planning pressure from growth.

**E — "SMB job shop, modernization"**
$10M–$100M revenue, 1–2 facilities, currently on legacy/homegrown systems or entry-level ERP (QuickBooks + Excel), owner-operator or recently-hired professional management, pressure from OEM customers to adopt specific EDI/reporting standards. Trigger: customer mandate, generational handoff, or private-equity platform play.

## 10. Common failure modes

- **Treating "manufacturing" as one market.** Discrete vs. process, automotive vs. pharma — very different buyers.
- **Pitching software-only ROI.** Manufacturers buy outcomes measured in their KPIs (OEE, yield, throughput, scrap).
- **Underestimating integration complexity.** Legacy PLCs, proprietary protocols, OT networks — integration is the deal.
- **Skipping plant visits.** Manufacturing buyers distrust vendors who've never been on a plant floor.
- **Missing the services reality.** Software is 20–40% of TCO; services, change management, and training dominate. Ignoring this in pricing = unhappy customers.
- **Corporate-only selling.** Deal closes at HQ; plants resist rollout; implementation fails.
- **Ignoring OT security.** Production networks are increasingly attacked; cybersecurity posture is now a gate.
- **Single-reference strategy.** Manufacturing buyers want references in their exact process type; a generic reference doesn't move them.
- **Underestimating sales cycle.** Planning cycles are budget-linked; missing a fiscal window = 12 months lost.
