# ICP — Digital Incentives, Rewards & Promo/Merchandise Playbook

> Vertical playbook for companies selling incentives, rewards, gift cards, loyalty platforms, or promotional products. Reflects the nuances of the space Cambrian Catalyst's founder knows from the inside (Blackhawk / Tango).

## 1. What makes this space distinct

Incentives and promo sit at an unusual intersection: financial product (gift cards are stored-value instruments) + marketing tool + HR/rewards tool + sometimes research incentive. Structural features:

**Multi-buyer reality.** The same product gets sold into marketing (acquisition incentives, research), HR (employee recognition, benefits), CX/research (survey and panel incentives), sales (channel incentives, spiffs), customer service (service recovery, goodwill), and loyalty programs. Each buyer has different criteria, budgets, procurement processes, and success metrics.

**Regulated as financial product.** Gift cards are subject to state escheatment, Reg E (to varying degrees), gift card laws (CARD Act federally, state additions), and IRS tax treatment rules (de minimis, imputed income, 1099 thresholds). Employee rewards have tax implications (compensation vs. de minimis fringe). International programs hit FX, local tax, local gift card regulations, and cross-border reporting.

**Distribution-heavy.** The value is often in the *catalog* (available brands) and *integrations* (HRIS, payroll, survey tool, marketing platform) as much as the platform. ICP must account for where the buyer lives in their tech stack.

**Volume pricing dominates.** Margins are thin (typically 2–15% on gift card face value, much higher on platform/software), so volume and unit economics drive everything. Buyers are cost-sensitive and will negotiate on every line.

**B2B reward ≠ B2C reward.** Consumer loyalty programs (airline miles, credit card rewards) have different mechanics than B2B incentive and rewards programs. This doc focuses on B2B incentive / reward / recognition buyers.

## 2. Use-case segmentation (the real ICP axis)

Segment by use case first, buyer second. Primary use cases:

- **Employee recognition & rewards** — peer-to-peer recognition, service awards, spot bonuses, sales spiffs.
- **Employee benefits & wellness** — wellness program incentives, flex rewards.
- **Customer acquisition incentives** — sign-up rewards, referral bonuses, win-back.
- **Customer loyalty & retention** — ongoing rewards, tier programs, surprise-and-delight.
- **Sales / channel incentives** — SPIFs, channel partner rewards, distributor incentives.
- **Market research incentives** — panel rewards, survey incentives, interview compensation.
- **Rebates** — consumer and B2B rebate fulfillment.
- **Service recovery & goodwill** — customer service compensation, warranty goodwill.
- **Clinical trial / healthcare** — participant compensation (regulated under IRB rules).
- **Public sector / nonprofit** — program incentives, beneficiary disbursement.

These are different products disguised as one category. A working ICP names the use case, not just the buyer title.

## 3. ICP patterns

Useful slicing dimensions:

- **Use case** (per Section 2).
- **Program size**: recipients per month, average reward value, annual disbursement volume. Dictates pricing tier, technical requirements, compliance burden.
- **Integration surface**: HRIS (Workday/SAP SuccessFactors/UKG/BambooHR/ADP), payroll, survey tools (Qualtrics/SurveyMonkey/Medallia), marketing platforms, CRM, expense systems, custom.
- **Geography & currency**: US-only vs. multi-country. International programs add FX, local tax, local gift card availability as major ICP filters.
- **Delivery preference**: digital-only, physical cards, mixed; branded merchandise vs. unbranded.
- **Buyer's program maturity**: first-time buyer vs. replacing incumbent. Very different sales motions.
- **Compliance posture**: SOC 2 requirement, tax reporting needs, 1099 handling, state residency tracking, international compliance.
- **Brand catalog priority**: does the buyer care about specific brands, or is flexibility the value?

## 4. The buying committee

Varies sharply by use case. Composite committees:

**For HR/recognition programs:**
- Economic buyer: CHRO, VP Total Rewards, VP HR.
- Champion: Director of Compensation & Benefits, Head of Recognition, People Ops Manager.
- End users: Managers, employees.
- Tech evaluator: HRIT, HRIS admin (integration owner).
- Procurement + legal + tax + payroll.

**For marketing/acquisition programs:**
- Economic buyer: CMO, VP Growth, VP Lifecycle.
- Champion: Director of Acquisition, Head of Referral, Lifecycle Marketing Manager.
- MarOps for integration.
- Finance/legal for tax, privacy, terms & conditions.

**For research/panel incentives:**
- Economic buyer: VP Research, Head of Insights, VP UX.
- Champion: Research Ops Manager, ResearchOps Lead.
- IT for panel platform integration.
- Legal for participant agreements, privacy (GDPR/CCPA for international panels).

**For sales/channel incentives:**
- Economic buyer: CRO, VP Sales, VP Channel.
- Champion: Sales Ops, Channel Ops, Partner Programs Manager.
- Finance for accrual and payout accounting.

**Universal gates in most programs:**
- **Tax & finance** — IRS reporting, 1099s, state-by-state income inclusion, GL coding.
- **Legal** — terms, sweepstakes/promotions rules (state-by-state), gift card compliance, international regulation.
- **Security / IT** — SSO, data handling, PII exposure.
- **Procurement / vendor management** — especially in regulated or large enterprise buyers.

## 5. Trigger events

Compelling events that make programs in-market:

- **New CHRO, CPO, or VP Total Rewards** — recognition and benefits program audits are common in first 6 months.
- **Engagement / pulse survey drop** — forces investment in recognition and appreciation programs.
- **Labor market tightening** — wage pressure → non-cash reward strategies to supplement comp.
- **M&A integration** — consolidating two or more recognition/reward vendors.
- **Budget cycle planning** — Q3/Q4 planning for next-year HR and marketing programs.
- **Program audit / cost review** — CFO-driven; creates consolidation opportunities.
- **Compliance event** — tax finding, IRS audit, state escheatment issue, incumbent vendor regulatory failure.
- **Research program launch or expansion** — new panel, new geography, new study type.
- **Marketing growth initiative** — new acquisition program, new loyalty tier, surge campaign.
- **Sales re-org or channel program launch** — triggers SPIF/incentive tool buying.
- **Incumbent vendor failure** — outages (e.g., delivery failures, fraud events, tax reporting issues), M&A dislocation, price increase, service deterioration.
- **Regulatory change** — state gift card law, CARD Act interpretation, IRS guidance update, international regulatory shift.
- **Internationalization** — expanding a US-only program globally triggers vendor reassessment.

## 6. Positioning — unique-attribute categories

Durable differentiation in this space:

- **Catalog breadth & quality** — brands available, recipient satisfaction with options, digital vs. physical mix.
- **International coverage** — countries, currencies, local brands, local compliance handled.
- **Integration depth** — named integrations with the buyer's HRIS, payroll, survey, CRM, marketing platform.
- **Compliance handling** — automated tax reporting, 1099 generation, state residency, international tax.
- **Fulfillment speed** — same-day digital, scheduled, batch, on-demand API.
- **Fraud & abuse controls** — reward abuse detection, velocity limits, identity verification.
- **Program management tools** — budget pacing, approvals, reporting, audit trail.
- **API & embedded options** — programmatic disbursement, white-label, embeddable rewards.
- **Recipient experience** — claim flow, customization, self-service, multi-language support.
- **Data & analytics** — recipient behavior, program ROI measurement, engagement metrics.
- **Reporting quality** — GL-ready exports, audit trails, configurable dashboards.

Competitive landscape (for positioning): Blackhawk Network, Tango Card, Tremendous, Runa, Giftbit, Prezzee, Extole (referral), Ambassador (referral), Rybbon (research-focused, now part of Tremendous), Virtual Rewards Center, Sodexo (physical/voucher), Edenred, Awardco, Bonusly (P2P recognition), Motivosity (P2P), Workhuman (enterprise recognition), Achievers (enterprise recognition), Guusto (small-mid market recognition), Snappy (curated gift), Caroo (physical gifts).

Knowing where you sit in this landscape is positioning 101. A new entrant that doesn't name its category position (e.g., "P2P recognition for mid-market" vs. "incentive API for developers") gets filtered out as undifferentiated.

## 7. Pricing & model patterns

Structures commonly seen:

- **Breakage / margin on face value** — historical default; 2–15% margin on gift card face value, often lower for high-volume. Ethically/operationally contested (breakage treatment); disclose.
- **Per-transaction fee** — flat $ or % per reward issued.
- **Platform SaaS fee** — monthly or annual, often with tiering.
- **Platform fee + per-transaction** — hybrid, ascendant.
- **Prepaid / float model** — buyer deposits funds; vendor holds float.
- **Net terms / invoice** — enterprise; tied to credit terms.
- **Volume rebates** — tiered discounts for scale.
- **Branded merchandise** — COGS-plus pricing with customization, often per-unit.
- **Custom enterprise** — negotiated for large or complex programs.

Unit economics sensitivity: at scale, a few basis points of margin matter. Pricing is ICP strategy here more than almost anywhere — if you can't profitably serve a given segment's volume at their expected pricing, they aren't your ICP.

## 8. Compliance / tax overlay

Material considerations, most buyers aren't fully aware of:

- **Gift card laws**: CARD Act (federal) + state variations (expiration, inactivity fees, escheatment).
- **Escheatment**: state unclaimed property laws; vendor must handle or buyer is exposed.
- **IRS treatment**:
  - Employee rewards: generally taxable compensation unless de minimis; non-cash awards >$100 typically taxable; must be reported via payroll.
  - Non-employee (contractor, research participant, customer): 1099-NEC if ≥ $600 aggregate in a year to a single person; vendor often handles collection and generation.
  - De minimis fringe benefit rules ($75 IRS guidance; company-specific thresholds).
- **SOX & audit**: public company buyers need SOX-compliant controls and audit trails.
- **State sales tax**: varies for gift cards vs. merchandise.
- **International**:
  - EU: VAT on vouchers (SPV/MPV), PSD2 for e-money, local gift card regulations.
  - UK: FCA if electronic money, HMRC treatment.
  - Canada: GST/HST, provincial variations.
  - LATAM, APAC: country-specific, often intricate.
- **Research incentives**: IRB requirements for clinical/academic research; Common Rule for federal research; HIPAA if health data.
- **Sweepstakes / promotions**: state rules, prohibited states, registration/bonding in NY/FL/RI for prize values.
- **AML/KYC**: for high-value disbursements, sanctions screening, beneficiary verification.
- **Privacy**: GDPR/CCPA/state for recipient data; data minimization principle.

ICP filter implication: buyers who understand compliance upfront convert faster and retain better. Buyers who don't will discover the complexity post-purchase and blame the vendor. Early compliance education is an ICP qualification move.

## 9. Archetypal ICP slices

**A — "Mid-market HR recognition, replacing spreadsheet-plus-gift-cards"**
US-based, $100M–$1B revenue, 500–5,000 employees, currently runs recognition via manager discretion + physical gift cards or Amazon cards, has named VP/Director of Total Rewards or Employee Experience with 12+ months tenure, recent engagement-survey drop or annual-planning cycle underway, on Workday/UKG/BambooHR/ADP HRIS. Trigger: engagement survey, CHRO directive, or annual planning.

**B — "Enterprise global recognition program replatforming"**
Multinational, $1B+ revenue, 10k+ employees across 10+ countries, existing recognition vendor underdelivering on global coverage or integration, has Global Head of Recognition or Total Rewards, formal RFP process. Trigger: incumbent contract renewal, M&A integration, or global program expansion.

**C — "Research ops scaling panel incentives"**
Mid-market to enterprise with significant research function (UX research, market research, customer insights), running 50+ studies/year, currently fulfilling incentives manually or with a mix of vendors, has Research Ops leader, uses UserTesting/Dscout/Qualtrics. Trigger: research program expansion, failed audit of incentive disbursement, or international study requirements.

**D — "Marketing acquisition program at scale"**
D2C brand or B2B2C company, $50M–$500M revenue, running referral or acquisition incentive programs, has Head of Growth/Lifecycle, needs programmable API-driven disbursement, currently fulfilling via manual processes or an incumbent with poor API. Trigger: growth-marketing relaunch, CAC pressure, or incumbent integration failure.

**E — "Channel / partner incentive program"**
B2B tech or CPG company, $100M+ revenue, multi-tier channel (distributors, resellers, retail partners), has Head of Channel or VP Partnerships, running SPIFs / MDF programs manually. Trigger: new channel program launch, partner-satisfaction issue, or audit/compliance finding.

**F — "Merchandise / promo for branded programs"**
Brand-heavy company, $50M+ revenue, running employee swag programs, event/conference giveaways, client appreciation gifts; looking for branded merchandise with program management, inventory, international shipping. Trigger: new event cycle, rebrand, or consolidation of multiple vendor relationships.

## 10. Common failure modes

- **Treating all use cases as one ICP.** HR recognition and marketing acquisition are different sales.
- **Under-investing in tax & compliance content.** Buyers make a compliance mistake once; they blame the vendor.
- **Ignoring integration depth.** A rewards platform without HRIS/survey/CRM integrations loses to a lesser one that has them.
- **Feature-based pricing wars.** Margin-thin category; competing on price without operational efficiency destroys economics.
- **Underestimating international complexity.** Buyers ask for "global"; what they need varies wildly by country.
- **Ignoring the recipient experience.** Recipients who hate the claim flow complain to HR/marketing, who churn.
- **Missing the switching cost reality.** Once integrated, customers are sticky. But the first year of onboarding is the fragile window. Solve that.
- **Neglecting breakage ethics.** Regulatory and consumer-protection attention to breakage practices is rising. Build positioning that doesn't rely on breakage.
- **Selling "platform" to a buyer who wants "service."** Some buyers want full-service managed programs; selling them a self-serve tool loses.
