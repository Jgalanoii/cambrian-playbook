# ICP — Marketing / MarTech Playbook

> Vertical playbook for companies selling to marketing teams, or selling marketing technology/adtech/revtech to GTM buyers.

## 1. What makes MarTech distinct

MarTech is one of the most crowded categories in B2B software (Scott Brinker's landscape has tracked 10,000+ vendors for years). Structural dynamics shape ICP work here:

**Commoditization is constant.** Each functional subcategory has 20–100 credible vendors. Differentiation must be sharp or price-competition takes over. ICPs built on weak differentiation produce low-conversion pipelines.

**Buyer is often also a user.** Marketing leaders typically buy tools they personally use. This makes ICP work unusually persona-heavy: the economic buyer and end user are often the same person, with implications for both positioning and product UX.

**Stack-dependent value.** Most marketing tools don't stand alone — they interact with CRM, CDP, marketing automation, analytics. ICP must include stack context; "has HubSpot" and "has Salesforce Marketing Cloud + 6sense + Clearbit" are different ICPs even if firmographics match.

**Marketing orgs have been shrinking.** Budgets tightened since 2023 and continue to normalize. Many tools sold to "marketing" are now sold to "revenue ops" or "growth" — different buyer, different criteria.

**Privacy is restructuring the space.** Third-party cookies, iOS ATT, GDPR/CCPA/state privacy laws, evolving ad-platform restrictions have killed entire categories and reshaped ICPs.

## 2. Marketing is six buyers in a trenchcoat

Name the buyer precisely. Common marketing org sub-segments:

- **Demand gen / growth** — quota-adjacent marketers owning pipeline and revenue attribution.
- **Content / product marketing** — narrative, positioning, sales enablement.
- **Brand / communications** — awareness, PR, reputation.
- **Performance / digital / paid media** — channel-specific, ROI-measured.
- **Lifecycle / CRM / email** — existing-customer engagement and retention.
- **Marketing ops / revops** — infrastructure, data, attribution, automation.
- **Web / conversion / SEO** — owned media optimization.
- **Events / field** — in-person, conferences, regional programs.
- **Creative / design** — production.
- **Analytics / insights** — measurement, research, segmentation.

Each sub-function has different pains, budgets, tools, and buying criteria. "We sell to marketing" is not an ICP.

## 3. ICP patterns

Useful slicing dimensions:

- **Firmographic**: B2B vs. B2C vs. B2B2C; industry; company size; funding stage.
- **Technographic**: CRM (HubSpot/Salesforce/Dynamics/Zoho), marketing automation (Marketo/HubSpot/Pardot/Braze/Iterable), CDP (Segment/Rudderstack/mParticle/none), analytics (GA4/Amplitude/Mixpanel), ad platforms, specific adjacent tools.
- **Team structure**: does a given function exist? How big? Reporting to whom?
- **Process maturity**: do they have SLAs between marketing and sales? Is attribution working? Is there a data foundation?
- **Budget posture**: is marketing budget growing, flat, or cut this year? Source: public filings, news, recent hires/layoffs.
- **Buying motion fit**: do they pilot, RFP, or just buy? Are they receptive to outbound?

## 4. The buying committee

Smaller than enterprise IT-heavy committees but can still be layered:

- **Economic buyer** — CMO, VP Marketing, VP Revenue/Growth, CRO, CFO (for large deals).
- **Champion** — Director or Manager in the relevant function who feels the pain.
- **End users** — the team that'll use it; often same person as champion in mid-market.
- **Marketing / RevOps** — integration owner, data steward, often the actual technical gate.
- **IT / Security** — SSO, data access, vendor review. Lighter-weight than IT-led product buying.
- **Legal / Privacy** — especially for data-handling products (CDPs, personalization, identity).
- **Finance / procurement** — above $25k ACV.
- **Sales leadership** — often a veto on revenue-adjacent tools (attribution, enrichment, outbound).

Pattern: if the product spans marketing and sales (ABM, intent, enrichment, attribution), the ICP must include *both* leader personas. Marketing champion without sales alignment = stalled deal.

## 5. Trigger events

Marketing-specific compelling events:

- **New CMO / VP Marketing hire** — first 6 months = audit and new-tool adoption window.
- **New CRO or revenue leader** — forces marketing-sales alignment and often tool changes.
- **Budget cycle (Q4 / fiscal year planning)** — tool consolidation and new spend decisions cluster here.
- **Missed pipeline quarter** — forces demand-gen tool reconsideration.
- **Attribution crisis** — "we can't tell what's working" triggers attribution/measurement tool purchases.
- **Privacy deprecation event** — cookie deprecation, ATT, iOS update, platform policy change that breaks existing workflows.
- **Platform change** — Google Analytics 4 migration, HubSpot tier change, Marketo or Eloqua replacement initiative.
- **Content / brand refresh** — triggers CMS, DAM, creative tool purchases.
- **M&A** — forces stack consolidation.
- **Funding round** — expands marketing budget; new investments in demand gen and brand.
- **Product launch** — new GTM motion creates tool gaps.

## 6. Positioning — unique-attribute categories

Durable differentiation in a crowded category requires sharp positioning:

- **Measurable ROI / attribution story** — not "we improve marketing" but "we improved X metric by Y% for customers like Z."
- **Stack integration depth** — native to the tools the buyer already uses; bi-directional sync, not just connectors.
- **Data completeness / accuracy** — for enrichment, identity, intent: accuracy claims with methodology.
- **Time-to-value** — campaign live in days vs. months.
- **Vertical or segment specificity** — B2B vs. B2C vs. PLG-specific; mid-market vs. enterprise.
- **Privacy posture** — first-party-data-native, cookieless, GDPR-by-design.
- **AI-native workflows** — genuinely AI-first product design vs. retrofitted features.
- **PLG / self-serve motion** — for tools that can be bought without sales touch.
- **Category creation vs. category displacement** — naming a new category vs. unseating an incumbent.

Marketing tool positioning that relies on feature lists loses to positioning that relies on integration depth, specific ICP fit, or a measurable outcome claim.

## 7. Pricing & model patterns

- **Per seat** — common but shrinking category; marketing teams are often small.
- **Per contact / per record** — dominant for marketing automation, CRM, CDP.
- **Per volume** — events, emails sent, API calls, page views, impressions.
- **Tiered packaging** — Good/Better/Best with feature gates, contact limits, or integration depth.
- **Platform fee + usage** — ascending; matches customer growth and value delivered.
- **Custom enterprise** — negotiated; usual above $100k ACV.
- **Freemium → paid** — common for SMB-targeted tools, but hard to graduate to enterprise on the same product.

Contact-based pricing has a pathology: the customer grows their list and gets punished with price increases. Pricing strategy is ICP strategy — if your pricing punishes success, it caps expansion.

## 8. Compliance / privacy overlay

MarTech compliance matters more than many vendors assume:

- **GDPR, CCPA/CPRA, state privacy laws** (Colorado, Virginia, Connecticut, Utah, etc., and growing).
- **DPA / DPF** (EU-US Data Privacy Framework) for cross-border data transfers.
- **CAN-SPAM, CASL** (Canada), **GDPR consent** for email.
- **TCPA** for SMS/voice.
- **COPPA** (children's data).
- **SOC 2 Type II** — near-universal expectation.
- **ISO 27701** (privacy) — emerging differentiator.
- **Ad platform policy alignment** — Google, Meta, LinkedIn, X policies on data collection, targeting, tracking.

A vendor's privacy posture is increasingly an ICP filter for enterprise buyers. "Privacy-first by design" is a real positioning angle, not marketing fluff.

## 9. Archetypal ICP slices

**A — "Mid-market B2B, demand-gen tooling"**
US-based B2B, $25M–$250M revenue, has VP Marketing with demand gen background and 2+ years tenure, running multi-channel programs (paid, content, events, email), on HubSpot or Salesforce + Marketo/Pardot, no dedicated martech admin, revenue attribution is broken or non-existent. Trigger: missed pipeline quarter, new VP hire, or planning cycle.

**B — "Enterprise B2B with RevOps function"**
Enterprise ($1B+), RevOps team of 10+, CRM is Salesforce with heavy customization, has CDP (Segment/Rudderstack/mParticle) or is shopping one, running ABM programs, current intent-data vendor (6sense/Demandbase/ZoomInfo) contract up for renewal. Trigger: renewal, new CRO, or ABM program relaunch.

**C — "Mid-market B2C with lifecycle focus"**
DTC or consumer tech, $10M–$100M revenue, subscriber or customer base of 100k+, Head of Lifecycle or Head of Retention champion, on Braze/Iterable/Klaviyo/Customer.io, needs personalization or next-best-action at scale, evidence of churn pressure. Trigger: LTV/CAC compression, new lifecycle leader, or replatforming.

**D — "PLG SaaS, self-serve marketing stack"**
Product-led SaaS, $5M–$50M ARR, small marketing team (2–8), founder- or PLG-leader-dominated decisions, comfortable with SQL / Amplitude / Mixpanel, prefers API-first tools, low patience for long sales cycles. Trigger: self-discovered via content; buying motion is try → buy in <30 days.

## 10. Common failure modes

- **Defining ICP as "marketing teams."** Too broad; sub-function is the real ICP axis.
- **Ignoring RevOps as decision-maker.** Marketing wants it; RevOps kills it if integration is weak.
- **Feature-list positioning.** Commoditized within 12 months in every marketing category.
- **Underpricing for SMB while pretending to be enterprise.** Pricing tells buyers who you're for.
- **Selling attribution to teams with no data foundation.** They can't use you; they churn.
- **Missing the CMO tenure dynamic.** Most CMOs tenure < 24 months. Deals sold to a CMO who leaves pre-renewal churn at alarming rates. ICP should account for organizational permanence beyond one person.
- **Ignoring the stack consolidation mood.** Post-2023 CFOs push hard on tool rationalization. "We have too many tools" is both opportunity (for platforms) and threat (for point solutions).
