# Cambrian Catalyst — Solution-Fit Cards

> Three cards for three off-the-shelf offers. Follow the Solution-Fit Card template from `offer-fit-mapping.md` Part 6. Numbers and specifics are calibrated to first-draft v1 — refine against actual delivery data after 3–5 engagements.

## How these offers ladder

The three offers are designed to stage a relationship, not compete for the same prospect:

```
Prospect shows up
       |
       v
[Diagnostic needed?]   YES → Offer 1: Revenue Architecture Diagnostic (6 wks)
       |                              |
       NO                             v
       |                     [Proceed to execution?]
       v                              |
[Ongoing capacity]                    YES → Offer 2: Revenue Architecture Program (12 wks)
  needed?]                            NO → Honest exit, referral, or nurture
       |                              |
       YES                            v
       |                     [Ongoing relationship?]
       v                              |
Offer 3: Fractional                   YES → Offer 3 as follow-on
GTM Advisor                            NO → Graceful close, reference request
(6-mo retainer)
```

A typical flow: diagnostic qualifies the relationship and the program, program executes on the diagnostic's priorities, and some percentage of program clients graduate to fractional advisor for ongoing capacity. Not every prospect moves through all three — many stop at one.

# Offer 1 — The Revenue Architecture Diagnostic

```
Name:           The Revenue Architecture Diagnostic
Duration:       6 weeks
Price:          $25,000–$35,000 (fixed, based on scope)
Deliverables:   GTM audit, ICP definition, positioning assessment,
                prioritized 90-day roadmap, final executive readout
Commitment:     ~6 hours/week of CEO or CRO time over 6 weeks;
                introductions to 5–8 internal stakeholders;
                facilitated access to 6–10 customer interviews
Outcome:        Client knows (a) what their GTM problem actually is, 
                (b) what to do about it in priority order, 
                (c) whether they want Cambrian Catalyst to help execute it
Last updated:   2026-04-20
Owner:          Joe / Cambrian Catalyst
```

## Who this offer is for

**Ideal ICP slice:** Series A–C B2B SaaS, $2M–$15M ARR, fintech / payments / martech-adjacent verticals (sometimes broader B2B where the problem type matches). CEO is still founder or is in first 12 months of post-founder-CEO tenure. Usually pre-CRO or early-CRO (first 6 months in role).

**Current state they're in:** Pipeline is inconsistent or declining. Founder is still the top seller. Sales hires aren't ramping. Marketing-to-sales handoff is broken or undefined. Board is pushing for GTM professionalization. At least one of the above is acute.

**Desired future state:** Predictable, diagnosable pipeline motion. Clarity on who they should sell to, with what message, through what motion. A plan that the CEO, CRO, and board can all align on. Reduced founder-dependence on top-of-funnel.

**The gap:** quantified on a case-by-case basis in diagnostic week 2. Common ranges: pipeline coverage 2–3x vs. target of 4x+; ramp to productivity 9–12 months vs. target of 5–6; win rate 15–20% vs. target of 30%+; founder-sourced deals 60–80% of pipeline vs. target of 30–40% post-diagnostic.

**Compelling event:** one of the following typically present:
- New CRO hired in last 6 months
- Series A/B/C funding closed in last 6 months
- Missed a revenue quarter with board visibility
- Prep for upcoming fundraise or board review
- Failed sales hire(s) making founder-led selling obvious bottleneck
- Strategic pivot (new segment, new product, new geo)

## Who this offer is NOT for

Any of the following is disqualifying:

1. **Pre-product-market-fit.** If the product isn't demonstrably solving a problem customers pay for, the answer is product and customer-discovery work, not GTM architecture. Refer to customer-development coaches or product-oriented advisors.
2. **Enterprise (post-Series D, $50M+ ARR, 200+ employees).** The engagement shape and depth required exceeds this offer. Either scope as a larger custom engagement or refer to larger consultancies.
3. **Industries outside our competence.** Medical devices, defense, heavily-regulated pharma manufacturing, construction tech, and other verticals where neither fintech nor broader B2B SaaS patterns apply.
4. **CEO/CRO unwilling to commit the time.** 6 hours/week is the floor. Clients who cannot commit this will not get value; decline honestly.
5. **"We just want execution."** Clients who want us to run sales for them (not advise + architect) are asking for a fractional CRO role, not a diagnostic. Route to Offer 3 or a partner referral.
6. **Incumbent consultant relationship the client hasn't named.** If they're evaluating us while under engagement with a current GTM consultant they haven't told, that's a yellow flag. Ask directly; disqualify if the incumbent is doing the same work.

## The 5–7 discovery questions that prove or disprove fit

1. **"Walk me through how you generate pipeline today — who does what, where are the breakdowns?"** (Tests: is there a defined motion at all? How founder-dependent is it? Where's the actual gap?)
2. **"When did you first realize something had to change about the GTM motion? What happened?"** (Tests: is there a genuine trigger, or generic dissatisfaction? Per Moesta — timeline reconstruction.)
3. **"Who are your best three customers, and what do they have in common?"** (Tests: do they have an implicit ICP they can articulate? If they can't, that's strong diagnostic signal.)
4. **"What's happening on the board that's making this a Q[n] priority?"** (Tests: Critical Event. If no board pressure, the urgency is real but softer.)
5. **"What's the economic consequence if you don't fix this in the next 6 months?"** (Tests: pain quantification. Gap Selling: if they can't quantify, it's not real pain.)
6. **"If you could snap your fingers and have the right ICP, positioning, and motion in place 90 days from now, what would be true that isn't today?"** (Tests: Future State articulation. Need-payoff.)
7. **"Who else is involved in deciding whether to do a diagnostic like this? Have they bought into the problem?"** (Tests: decision process, champion dynamics, economic buyer access.)

## Red flags in the discovery call

- **"We just need leads."** If their framing reduces the problem to lead volume, they're not ready for architecture work. Misaligned expectations will produce a bad engagement.
- **"Our biggest problem is [one very specific tactical thing]."** Tactical problems are tactical; diagnostic is for architectural problems. Refer out or scope a smaller tactical engagement.
- **CEO delegates the diagnostic kickoff call to someone else.** If the CEO won't take the first call, the engagement is under-sponsored. Reschedule or disqualify.
- **Evasive on budget.** Enns's principle: address money early. A prospect who won't discuss a budget range in the first call is unlikely to close; pursue at your own risk.
- **Competitor evaluation signals.** "We're talking to 4 firms." Fine if named and sane; concerning if they're treating this as a vendor bake-off for a commodity engagement. Either reframe the process (see battle card template) or walk.
- **"Can we just skip the diagnostic and do the work?"** Tempting, and occasionally right for a very mature buyer. Default: no. The diagnostic qualifies the program, generates the insight that makes the program effective, and establishes the expert posture (per Enns). Skipping it collapses these into a low-margin execution engagement.

## Adjacent offers / referral routing

- **If they need only positioning/messaging work** → scope a 4-week Positioning Sprint (smaller offer, ~$12–18k) or refer to a positioning specialist (Emily Kramer / MKT1, or Dunford-trained independents).
- **If they need only ICP definition** → 3-week ICP Sprint (~$8–12k) or refer to a practitioner specializing in ICP work.
- **If they need interim sales leadership, not architecture** → Offer 3 (Fractional GTM Advisor) or refer to Pavilion's fractional marketplace or domain-specific fractional CROs.
- **If they need content/demand-gen execution** → refer to MKT1, Exit Five, Refine Labs, or a demand-gen-specific agency.
- **If they're pre-PMF** → refer to Reforge programs, YC-adjacent advisors, or customer-development specialists.
- **If they're larger than our fit** → refer to Pavilion Advisors, GTM Partners, Winning by Design consulting, or boutique mid-tier GTM firms.
- **If they're outside our verticals** → refer to vertical-native consultancies (healthcare-specific, manufacturing-specific, etc.) in our network.

- **2–3 reference contacts** from prior engagements willing to speak to peers (rotate, don't burn).
- **Diagnostic outline** showing the 6-week schedule, meeting cadence, and deliverables so the client can visualize the engagement.

# Offer 2 — The Revenue Architecture Program

```
Name:           The Revenue Architecture Program  
Duration:       12 weeks (following a completed Diagnostic)
Price:          $60,000–$120,000 (based on scope of priorities)
Deliverables:   Execution on top 3 priorities identified in diagnostic;
                typical menu includes ICP operationalization, 
                positioning/messaging rebuild, sales enablement system,
                ABM playbook, pricing/packaging redesign, 
                pipeline/forecasting discipline
Commitment:     ~8 hours/week of CRO + ~4 hours/week of CEO;
                team participation (SDRs, AEs, marketing, CS as scoped);
                access to CRM and sales systems
Outcome:        The priorities from the diagnostic are executed to 
                completion, not just recommended. Sales team is 
                equipped to operate the new architecture post-engagement.
Last updated:   2026-04-20
Owner:          Joe / Cambrian Catalyst
```

## Who this offer is for

**Ideal ICP slice:** Same as Diagnostic, but after the Diagnostic is complete. Typically 60–70% of Diagnostic clients move into Program; the 30–40% who don't split between "too early," "internal-capacity decision," and "wrong fit — we learn this during diagnostic and refer out."

**Current state they're in:** Completed Diagnostic. Has a written roadmap. Has CEO and CRO agreement on top 3 priorities. Has a budget conversation already had (Diagnostic opened it). Ready to execute.

**Desired future state:** The top 3 priorities are operational, not aspirational. Team is trained and tooled. Measurement and reporting exists for each initiative. Next-quarter plan is visible.

**The gap:** typically quantified during the Diagnostic itself. Program operationalizes the closing of the gap.

**Compelling event:** the Diagnostic itself is the compelling event once complete. Client has invested in understanding the problem; momentum drives toward executing the solution.

## Who this offer is NOT for

1. **Prospects who skipped the Diagnostic.** Tempting but harmful. Without diagnosis, the Program is a bespoke execution engagement with no clear success criteria, low margin, and high risk. Decline or re-route to Diagnostic first.
2. **Diagnostic clients whose outcome is "not a fit."** Sometimes the Diagnostic reveals the client needs capabilities we don't have, or reveals they're pre-PMF, or reveals scope larger than we can handle. Be honest; refer out.
3. **Clients unwilling to commit team time during Program.** The Program requires team participation; clients who want us to "just do it in a corner" are buying a dependency, not a capability. Decline.
4. **Clients who want only 1 of the 3 priorities executed.** If they want narrower scope, unbundle into a scope-specific smaller engagement — don't shoehorn into Program frame.

## The discovery questions (mid-engagement, at Diagnostic-to-Program transition)

This is the "second sale" — from Diagnostic client to Program client. Questions are different from first-meeting discovery:

1. **"Based on the Diagnostic, what's your honest read on the top 3 priorities? What did we nail, what did we miss?"** (Tests: alignment. If they disagree materially with the roadmap, Program scope needs rework or the relationship isn't ready.)
2. **"Which of the priorities, if executed well, moves the needle most for your Q[n] board conversation?"** (Tests: CEO/CRO framing for internal selling.)
3. **"Who on your team is accountable for each priority once we're done? What capacity do they have today?"** (Tests: operationalization fit. A Program with no internal owner post-engagement doesn't stick.)
4. **"What's the internal case for continuing with us vs. hiring a full-time CRO (or another firm)?"** (Tests: Why us vs. alternatives. Good answer: "we trust the work we've seen, we want continuity." Bad answer: "we haven't really thought about it.")
5. **"If we don't execute on these priorities in the next 90 days, what happens?"** (Tests: Critical Event urgency. Program needs time-pressure to sustain executive engagement.)

## Red flags in the Diagnostic-to-Program transition

- **CEO disengages during Diagnostic.** Program requires CEO sponsorship. If the CEO pulled back in the back half of Diagnostic, Program will be harder to deliver. Name it.
- **Budget conversations get harder, not easier.** Diagnostic should open the money discussion; if Program pricing is surprising or resisted, we mis-scoped expectations.
- **Team resentment surfacing.** Diagnostic findings that blame a specific team member or function will meet resistance during Program. Surface and address before commitment.
- **"Can we just have you implement one thing?"** See disqualifier above. Re-scope to a tactical offer, not Program.
- **Champion unclear on internal political landscape.** Program touches multiple functions; a champion who can't navigate politics will watch the engagement stall.

## Adjacent offers / referral routing

- **If Program scope is too broad for 12 weeks** → scope as Program + extension (16–20 weeks) or break into sequential Programs. Don't under-scope and fail to deliver.
- **If Program scope is narrower than 3 priorities** → unbundle into a single-priority sprint (e.g., "ICP & Positioning Sprint," "ABM Playbook Sprint," "Sales Enablement Build").
- **If Program reveals need for interim sales leadership** → transition to Offer 3 (Fractional GTM Advisor) during or immediately after.

# Offer 3 — The Fractional GTM Advisor

```
Name:           The Fractional GTM Advisor
Duration:       6-month minimum, month-to-month after
Price:          $10,000–$18,000 / month depending on intensity
Deliverables:   Weekly 1:1 with CEO/CRO (60 min);
                monthly GTM health review with leadership team;
                quarterly strategic planning facilitation;
                ad-hoc deal coaching, hiring support, board-prep assistance
Commitment:     ~8–12 hours/month of CEO/CRO time;
                ~4 hours/month of broader leadership team time;
                inclusion in key GTM decisions
Outcome:        Client has strategic GTM capacity without full-time 
                hire cost. Ongoing relationship continuity across 
                hires, pivots, and funding events.
Last updated:   2026-04-20
Owner:          Joe / Cambrian Catalyst
```

## Who this offer is for

**Ideal ICP slice:** Most commonly, Program graduates who want continuity. Secondarily, prospects who come in directly for ongoing strategic capacity without needing Diagnostic/Program work first — typically more mature buyers who know what they need.

**Current state they're in:** GTM motion exists and works; strategic sparring partner is the need. Common patterns: CEO is still founder-led on sales strategy and wants a peer; CRO is in first 6 months and wants an outside advisor; company is between CROs and needs strategic continuity.

**Desired future state:** Strategic decisions get made with outside counsel. CEO/CRO has a reliable sounding board. Blind spots get surfaced. Hiring, pricing, positioning, pipeline decisions benefit from external perspective.

**The gap:** harder to quantify than Program's operational gap. Usually framed in terms of "decisions made better" or "pitfalls avoided" — less measurable, more relational.

**Compelling event:** transition points. Between CRO hires, post-Program, post-funding, post-pivot, or explicitly "we want someone in the room for Q[n]."

## Who this offer is NOT for

1. **Clients wanting execution capacity dressed as advisory.** If they want us to run outbound or manage reps day-to-day, that's interim leadership, not fractional advisory. Re-scope or decline.
2. **Clients shopping hourly consulting.** Fractional is a relationship, not a punch-card. If they want per-hour billing, refer to marketplaces (Catalant, Pavilion's fractional tier, etc.).
3. **Clients whose problem is a Diagnostic + Program.** Route them to Offer 1 first; fractional doesn't replace architectural work.
4. **Clients without committed CEO sponsorship.** Without CEO engagement, fractional becomes hallway advisory with no influence; disengage.
5. **Clients with existing fractional CRO who haven't disclosed.** Conflict of interest — decline.

## The discovery questions (relationship sale, different dynamic)

Fractional is a relationship sale. Questions reflect this:

1. **"What are the 3–5 decisions you see coming up in the next 6 months where an outside voice would meaningfully change the decision?"** (Tests: concrete value, not vague "advisory.")
2. **"What's the case for us vs. hiring a full-time CRO?"** (Tests: their actual job-to-be-done. If answer is "we can't afford a CRO yet," fractional may be a bridge; if "we want flexibility," fractional is the right shape; if "we don't actually need a CRO," disqualify.)
3. **"Who else is in your strategic advisor pool — board, investors, peers? How does this role fit?"** (Tests: we're not their only voice; good — know the ecosystem.)
4. **"When was the last time you changed your mind about something important in the business because of outside input?"** (Tests: openness to influence. Clients who never change their mind will not benefit from fractional work.)
5. **"What would make you renew us at month 6 vs. not?"** (Tests: success criteria for the relationship. Surface early.)

## Red flags

- **"We need someone in the room for our board meetings."** Only red flag if that's the primary value. Fractional should drive strategic work all month, not just prep for 4 board meetings/year.
- **CEO cancels 2+ of first 4 weekly 1:1s.** The relationship requires cadence. Early cancellation pattern predicts disengagement.
- **"Can you just do [specific execution task]?"** Mission creep. Say no clearly; re-scope to a project engagement if the need is real.
- **Client doesn't take a single recommendation seriously in first 60 days.** They want validation, not advisory. Name it; renegotiate the role or exit.
- **Payment delays or renegotiation requests in month 1–2.** Financial stress. Predicts relationship stress. Address directly.

## Adjacent offers / referral routing

- **If client outgrows fractional and needs full-time CRO** → graceful handoff; support the hire; retain advisory role if useful.
- **If client needs deeper functional expertise we don't have** → co-advisor or referral to specialist fractional (fractional CMO, fractional RevOps lead, etc.).
- **If client situation changes and they need Program-style execution** → re-scope into a Program under the Fractional umbrella.

## Champion enablement (relational)

Different from Program. Fractional enablement is about making the CEO/CRO look smart in their own environment:

- **Board-deck inputs** — prepared slides for board meetings when helpful.
- **Hire briefings** — when the client is hiring a CRO, CMO, Head of Sales, we brief them on market and candidate profile.
- **Investor updates contribution** — we contribute GTM sections to monthly investor updates on request.
- **Warm introductions** — to peers, partners, vendors, potential hires. The network access *is* part of the offer.

# Decision logic: which offer for which prospect?

A routing table for junior reps or referral partners. Given prospect signals, which offer to recommend:

| Prospect signal | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| "We need help figuring out what's broken in GTM." | **Diagnostic** — the archetypal fit. |
| "Our pipeline is a mess and we don't know why." | **Diagnostic** — classic unclear-problem signal. |
| "We just had our Diagnostic with you; what's next?" | **Program** — if scope fits and client is ready. |
| "We did a diagnostic with [another firm] and have a roadmap; we need help executing." | **Program** with a compressed first-week to validate their diagnostic. If their diagnostic is weak, re-do as Diagnostic. |
| "We've worked with you before and want ongoing capacity." | **Fractional** — relationship continuity play. |
| "We just hired a new CRO and want an outside voice during their ramp." | **Fractional** — this is one of the best fits. |
| "We need someone to just run our sales for a few months." | **Not us.** Refer to interim leadership or fractional CRO specialists. |
| "We just need leads." | **Not us.** Refer to demand-gen-specific agency. |
| "We need to build our ABM program." | If broader GTM context is relevant: **Diagnostic** then **Program**. If purely tactical: scope a single-priority sprint or refer. |
| "We want to redo our positioning." | If narrow: Positioning Sprint (smaller). If positioning is actually a symptom of larger GTM confusion: **Diagnostic**. |
| "We're pre-PMF but need GTM." | **Not us.** Route to customer-development advisors or Reforge. |
| "We're enterprise, $100M+ revenue." | **Not us** at current capacity. Refer to Pavilion Advisors, GTM Partners, or boutique mid-tier. Reevaluate as firm scales. |
| "We're in healthcare/construction/defense." | **Not us** if outside our vertical competence. Refer to vertical-native consultancies. |

# Maintenance notes

- **Review quarterly.** Every three months, review each card against actual engagement data. Update disqualifiers based on bad-fit deals that slipped through. Update pricing based on realized margins.
- **Populate references as engagements close.** Every completed engagement should generate a reference story for the relevant card, with client permission.
- **Track disqualification rates.** Monthly: how many prospects were disqualified per offer? Low rates (< 20%) suggest ICP is too loose. High rates (> 70%) suggest targeting is off or market is wrong.
- **Test the cards with new readers.** When someone new joins (hire, partner), have them read the cards cold and explain the offers back. If they can't, the cards aren't sharp enough.
