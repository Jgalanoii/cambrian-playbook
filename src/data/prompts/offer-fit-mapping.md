# Offer-Fit Mapping — Deep Knowledge Base

> Companion to the ICP knowledge base. ICP answers *which companies should we target*. This doc answers *given a qualified prospect, which of our off-the-shelf offers is right for them (including "none, refer out")* — and how a junior rep can reach that answer in a single discovery call without feeling like they're interrogating the buyer.

## How to use this document

**For training new reps:** Parts 1–5 are the intellectual foundation. A new rep should read Gap Selling (§1.1), MEDDPICC (§2.1), the Solution Selling pain-chain (§3.1), and JOLT (§5.2) before their first discovery call. The rest can be absorbed over their first 90 days.

**For building playbooks:** Part 6 is the operational system. Copy the Solution-Fit Card template, the Discovery Question Library, and the Fit Scorecard into your enablement stack and customize per offer.

**For offer design itself:** Part 4 is the upstream work. You cannot map prospects to off-the-shelf offers if the offers themselves are vague.

**For LLM retrieval:** Every H2/H3 is self-describing. Queries like "MEDDPICC champion criteria" or "JOLT four levers" should return the relevant subsection cleanly.

# Part 1 — Diagnostic-First Selling

The premise uniting these three: **you cannot recommend a fit until you have diagnosed the situation.** Junior reps who skip diagnosis default to pitching; pitching is exhausting for buyers and converts poorly. These frameworks give the rep a structure for diagnosis that doesn't feel like an interrogation.

## 1.1 Gap Selling — Keenan

**Source:** *Gap Selling: Getting the Customer to Yes* by Jim Keenan (2018). Keenan runs A Sales Growth Company. The book is short, tactical, and the most junior-rep-friendly of any sales methodology in print.

### Core premise

*The customer doesn't buy a product. They buy a change.* Every purchase is a movement from a Current State the buyer wants to leave to a Future State they want to reach. The Gap between those states — its size, urgency, and business impact — is the thing that determines whether and how they buy. Your product is only relevant insofar as it closes that gap.

Most selling goes wrong because reps jump to product before establishing that the Gap exists, is significant, and is worth closing *now*. "Solution unaware" reps feature-dump; "Gap-aware" reps diagnose and recommend.

### The three things a rep must establish

**Current State (CS):** an honest, quantified picture of where the prospect is today. Not what they wish they had; what they actually have. Processes, tools, metrics, team composition, pain points, workarounds, attempts already made. The rep's job is to understand the CS better than the prospect does — which is possible because prospects are inside the problem and often can't see it clearly.

**Future State (FS):** where the prospect needs to be. Also quantified. "We need to convert more leads" is not an FS. "We need our SQL-to-Opp conversion rate at 35% vs. today's 22% to hit our $X pipeline goal by Q3" is an FS.

**The Gap:** the measurable distance between CS and FS, expressed in the buyer's business terms. Size, urgency, cost of staying in CS, value of reaching FS.

### The PIC — Problem Identification Chart

Keenan's core worksheet for a rep pre- or post-call. Four columns:

1. **Technical problem** — what's literally broken or missing in the CS.
2. **Business problem** — the consequence in business terms (revenue, cost, risk, compliance, time).
3. **Impact** — quantified: how much revenue lost, how much cost incurred, how much risk exposed.
4. **Root cause** — *why* the technical problem exists. Often the most overlooked field. Without root cause, your solution treats a symptom.

If the rep can't fill all four columns after a discovery call, they haven't diagnosed. No recommendation should follow.

### Key Keenan principles (memorize)

- **"People don't buy products; they buy change."** Reframes every pitch.
- **"Discovery is investigation, not interrogation."** The rep's curiosity should be obvious; the prospect should feel understood, not grilled.
- **"You should know the prospect's problem better than they do."** This is the reputation that earns advisor status.
- **"Don't sell to the happy."** If the prospect has no gap, disqualify — don't manufacture urgency.
- **"Pain without impact is just complaining."** Every pain must convert to quantified business impact or it won't drive a purchase.
- **"Feature-dumping is the behavior of a rep who didn't do discovery."**

### Junior-rep applicability

Very high. Gap Selling is the single most transferable framework for new reps. Four reasons:

1. The PIC is a fillable worksheet; reps can literally bring it into calls.
2. The CS/FS/Gap mental model is intuitive and stops reps from pitching.
3. It gives reps a disqualification license: "there's no gap here" is a legitimate outcome.
4. The discovery posture (curiosity, investigation) is what buyers actually want — so it aligns with the CX-first goal.

### Limits / critiques

- Assumes a rational, business-decision-making buyer. Underweights political and emotional dynamics that dominate enterprise buying.
- Less helpful for very complex, multi-stakeholder enterprise deals (use in combination with MEDDPICC).
- Keenan's voice is deliberately blunt and sometimes abrasive; not all cultures will absorb it well.

## 1.2 SPIN Selling — Rackham

**Source:** *SPIN Selling* by Neil Rackham (1988), based on a 12-year Huthwaite Research study of ~35,000 sales calls across 23 countries. It remains the only methodology in widespread use that is grounded in large-sample empirical research.

### Core premise

*What makes a rep successful in small, transactional sales is actively harmful in large, complex sales.* Rackham's research found that closing techniques, trial closes, benefit statements, and other "traditional" sales tactics correlated positively with success in small sales and *negatively* with success in large sales.

The differentiator in large sales was question sequencing — specifically, asking questions in a way that caused the buyer to articulate their own pain and desire for resolution.

### The four question types

SPIN is an acronym for the question hierarchy that top performers in the study used:

**S — Situation questions.** Factual questions about the buyer's current state. ("How many reps do you have? What CRM do you use?") Necessary for context but wear buyers out; top performers ask *fewer* Situation questions than average performers. Do your homework first; don't waste the buyer's time on facts that are Google-able.

**P — Problem questions.** Questions about difficulties, dissatisfactions, and problems. ("Where are you losing deals? What's not working in the current handoff?") Surfaces "implied needs" — problems the buyer acknowledges exist but may not yet feel compelled to act on.

**I — Implication questions.** Questions about the *consequences* of problems. ("If that handoff keeps breaking, what happens to quota attainment next quarter? How does that affect retention in the SDR team?") These are the highest-leverage questions. They expand the perceived cost of the problem and convert implied needs to **explicit needs** (active desire for resolution).

**N — Need-payoff questions.** Questions that get the buyer to articulate the value of a solution. ("If you could cut that handoff time in half, what would that mean for your team? How would that change your planning next quarter?") The buyer sells the solution to themselves — far more persuasive than any pitch.

### Rackham's empirical findings (counterintuitive)

- **"Opens" don't matter.** No statistical correlation between opening techniques and success in large sales.
- **Closing techniques hurt in large sales.** More closing attempts = lower close rate in complex sales. The "always be closing" dogma is empirically wrong above a certain deal size.
- **Objection handling is mostly a symptom of poor discovery.** Top reps get fewer objections because their questions prevented objections from arising.
- **Features, Advantages, Benefits.** Rackham distinguishes these precisely. Features = facts about the product. Advantages = how features help in general. *Benefits = how features meet an explicit need the buyer has stated.* Only Benefits correlate with success in large sales. This is why you cannot benefit-sell before discovery.

### Junior-rep applicability

High but requires discipline. The four question types are memorable, but sequencing them well is a learned skill. Typical failure: a rep reads SPIN and tries to "run the framework" as a script, producing stilted conversations. SPIN is a hierarchy of *question types*, not a script.

Training move: have reps tag their own call questions (S/P/I/N) and review the ratio. Weak reps over-index on Situation; top performers weight Implication and Need-payoff.

### Limits / critiques

- The research is dated (1970s–1980s data). Some specifics (e.g., call-opening rituals) don't translate to modern buyer contexts.
- SPIN assumes a 1:1 selling motion. In committee-based enterprise buying, question sequencing happens across multiple calls and stakeholders.
- Rackham's follow-on books (*Major Account Sales Strategy*, *Rethinking the Sales Force*) are better for multi-stakeholder deals.

## 1.3 Demand-Side Sales 101 — Moesta

**Source:** *Demand-Side Sales 101: Stop Selling and Help Your Customers Make Progress* by Bob Moesta & Greg Engle (2020). Applies Jobs-to-be-Done theory to the sales conversation itself (JTBD's ICP applications are covered in the core ICP knowledge base).

### Core premise

*Stop trying to convince people to buy your product; start helping them make the progress they're already trying to make.* "Supply-side selling" begins with the product ("here's what we do, let me tell you why you need it"). "Demand-side selling" begins with the customer's desired progress ("what are you trying to accomplish, and can I help?").

The reframe isn't soft — it's tactical. A rep who understands the buyer's desired progress can show, with precision, how the product fits or doesn't. A rep who doesn't can only pitch.

### The Four Forces of Progress (applied to the sale)

Covered in the ICP doc; here's how they apply inside the conversation:

- **Push of the current situation** — what's driving them away from status quo. The rep surfaces this with "tell me about the day you first realized this needed to change."
- **Pull of the new solution** — what attracts them about change. The rep surfaces this with "when you imagine this solved, what does that look like?"
- **Anxiety of the new** — what they're worried about. The rep surfaces this *intentionally* because unsurfaced anxieties kill deals silently. "What would have to be true for you to feel comfortable moving forward?"
- **Habit of the present** — what inertia holds them in place. "What would need to change internally to make this happen?"

For a purchase, Push + Pull must exceed Anxiety + Habit. A rep who only pitches Pull (their product's benefits) is fighting with half the equation.

### The timeline interview (adapted for sales)

Moesta's "Switch Interview" reconstructs the decision as a timeline. Adapted for an active sales conversation:

1. **First thought** — "When did you first start thinking something had to change here?"
2. **Passive looking** — "Between then and now, what changed that made you start looking?"
3. **Active looking** — "When you started looking seriously, what did you evaluate? What did you rule out?"
4. **Decision / purchase** — "What would need to happen for you to decide by [date]?"
5. **First use** — "Imagine 60 days after you buy. What would make this feel like the right decision?"

This produces a reconstruction of the buyer's own decision logic that is more persuasive (to them) than any pitch.

### Junior-rep applicability

Medium-high. The Four Forces are memorable. The timeline interview technique is harder to run without practice — it requires the rep to resist the urge to pitch and stay in inquiry mode. But once internalized, it's a powerful instrument for both diagnosing fit and giving the buyer an experience of *being understood* that is rare in B2B sales.

### Limits / critiques

- JTBD interviews are craft-heavy. Bad ones produce just-so stories.
- Less useful for transactional sales where the buyer's progress is obvious (renewals, commodity purchases).
- The philosophical reframe ("stop selling") can read as soft to sales leaders raised on forecast discipline. It's not — it's a different discipline.

## 1.4 Synthesis — diagnostic-first for junior reps

All three frameworks converge on the same operational behavior: **the rep's job in the first meeting is not to present, it is to understand.** Gap Selling gives the rep the *structure* (CS / FS / Gap, PIC). SPIN gives the rep the *question hierarchy* (S → P → I → N). Moesta gives the rep the *posture* (helping progress, not convincing).

For a junior rep learning these, the order to absorb them:

1. **Gap Selling first** — most concrete, fillable worksheet, junior-friendly framing.
2. **SPIN second** — layer question-type discipline onto Gap Selling discovery.
3. **Moesta third** — add the progress-making frame once the rep has the mechanics down; this is the "posture" that makes the first two feel like a conversation instead of a framework.

Practical rule: a rep who leaves a discovery call unable to write a complete PIC *and* say which of the Four Forces is dominant has not diagnosed. No proposal should follow.

# Part 2 — Deal Qualification

If Part 1 is about understanding the prospect, Part 2 is about **whether the deal can be closed.** Qualification frameworks are not discovery frameworks — they sit on top of discovery and give the team a shared language for deciding where to invest rep time.

## 2.1 MEDDPICC

**Source:** Evolved from MEDDIC, invented at PTC (Parametric Technology Corporation) in the mid-1990s by Jack Napoli and Dick Dunkel. Extended to MEDDICC (adding Competition) and then MEDDPICC (adding Paper Process). Darius Lahoutifard's MEDDIC Academy and Andy Whyte's book *MEDDICC: The Ultimate Guide to Staying One Step Ahead of Your Competitors* (2020) are the canonical modern references.

### Core premise

*In complex enterprise deals, missing information kills deals more predictably than any other factor.* MEDDPICC is a checklist of eight things you must know to forecast the deal accurately and close it. Every letter maps to a specific failure mode if it's missing.

### The eight letters

**M — Metrics.** The quantified measure the prospect will use to evaluate success. Not "improve efficiency" — "reduce onboarding time from 14 days to 5 days." Without metrics, the deal will die at pricing because the value isn't quantified.

**E — Economic Buyer.** The single person with discretionary authority to sign the contract. Not the champion, not the committee, not the procurement rep. The specific human who can say yes and mean it. Most junior reps never meet the Economic Buyer; most lost deals are lost because they never did.

**D — Decision Criteria.** The criteria the prospect will use to choose (or not choose) a vendor. Written or unwritten. Technical, business, commercial. If the rep doesn't know the criteria, they can't influence them. If the criteria are written and include something your product doesn't do, you're losing already.

**D — Decision Process.** The specific steps from today to signed contract. Who does what, by when, with what dependencies. Reps who can't diagram the decision process can't predict close dates.

**P — Paper Process.** Added in MEDDPICC. The legal, procurement, and security review process specifically. Often takes longer than the "decision process." Enterprise deals slip 1–2 quarters routinely because the rep treated paper process as an afterthought.

**I — Identify Pain.** The quantified business pain being solved. Related to but distinct from Metrics: Pain is what's wrong today; Metrics are how we'll measure getting it right. Without identified pain, the deal has no compelling event.

**C — Champion.** An internal person who (1) has power or access to power, (2) is actively selling your solution internally when you're not in the room, and (3) has a personal win — something they gain if the deal closes. Champions are rare and precious. "Coach" (someone who gives you information but doesn't sell for you) is common; champion is what you need.

**C — Competition.** Who else is being evaluated, including "do nothing" and "build internally." A deal without named competitors is usually a deal where the champion hasn't engaged the committee yet — meaning the deal is less real than it appears.

### The champion criteria — expanded

Whyte's formalization: a true champion meets three tests. Miss any one and you have a coach, not a champion.

1. **Power** — the ability to influence the decision, either directly or through relationships with the Economic Buyer.
2. **Actively selling** — they advocate for your solution in internal meetings you're not in. Testable: ask them to send an internal email on your behalf. A champion will; a coach won't.
3. **Personal win** — something they gain personally if the deal closes. Career win, political win, reduction in their own workload, ownership of a successful initiative. Without this, they have no skin in the game.

### How to use MEDDPICC operationally

As a shared-language checklist in deal reviews. For each active opportunity, the rep should be able to state each letter in a sentence. Missing or weak letters = action items.

**Risk signals by letter:**

- Missing Metrics → deal will die at pricing.
- Missing Economic Buyer → deal will stall before contract.
- Weak Decision Criteria → deal will be reshaped by a competitor who got there first.
- Missing Decision Process → forecast is fiction.
- Unknown Paper Process → close date will slip 1–2 quarters.
- Missing Identified Pain → no compelling event; deal is informational.
- Coach instead of Champion → deal will stall when you're not in the room.
- Missing Competition → deal is earlier-stage than it appears.

### Junior-rep applicability

Extremely high — this is the single most widely-adopted qualification framework in B2B SaaS for good reason. Junior reps benefit from the letter-by-letter discipline because it's debuggable: instead of "I'm not sure about this deal," they can say "I don't have Paper Process yet, and the Champion test fails on 'personal win.'" Managers can coach specific letters.

### Limits / critiques

- Overkill for SMB and transactional deals; rule-of-thumb is MEDDPICC applies to deals above ~$25k ACV with multi-stakeholder buying.
- The acronym is un-memorable and reps will forget letters under pressure. Many teams create memory aids or reorder.
- Can become performative — reps fill in letters to satisfy the pipeline review without actually knowing the answer. Manager discipline is required to test the letters, not just collect them.

## 2.2 SPICED (Winning by Design)

**Source:** Winning by Design / Jacco van der Kooij. Covered briefly in the core ICP knowledge base; extended here for operational use.

### Core premise

A lighter, recurring-revenue-flavored sibling to MEDDPICC. The insight: **without a Critical Event, there is no deal this quarter.** SPICED is optimized for mid-market and SMB SaaS motions where enterprise committee dynamics don't dominate.

### The five letters

- **S — Situation.** Current state: environment, stack, team, relevant context.
- **P — Pain.** The problem, ideally quantified.
- **I — Impact.** The cost of the pain — expressed in their business terms.
- **C — Critical Event.** The forcing function that makes this a must-do-by-date.
- **D — Decision.** Who decides, how, and on what criteria.

### The Critical Event concept

Unique to SPICED and arguably its most valuable contribution. A Critical Event is an externally-imposed or internally-committed deadline that forces action. Examples:

- A new product launch in Q3 requires this capability by Q2.
- A compliance deadline mandates the control by a specific date.
- A contract with a current vendor expires and requires decision by date X.
- A board commitment was made to deliver by quarter-end.

Without a Critical Event, deals slip indefinitely because there is no cost to waiting. A deal without a Critical Event is forecastable only as "will close someday" — which is to say, not forecastable.

Junior-rep instruction: if you cannot name the Critical Event for a deal, you have an informational meeting, not an opportunity.

### When to use SPICED vs MEDDPICC

- **SPICED** — mid-market and SMB SaaS; shorter cycles; simpler buying committees; 3-to-6-month sales cycles.
- **MEDDPICC** — enterprise; large committees; 6-to-18-month cycles; deals above $50k ACV where Paper Process is a real variable.
- **Both can coexist** — SPICED for early qualification, MEDDPICC for late-stage deal management.

### Limits / critiques

- Underweights Champion dynamics (no explicit Champion letter).
- Critical Event framing can push reps to *manufacture* urgency rather than find genuine events — which corrupts CX.
- Less battle-tested than MEDDPICC in enterprise.

## 2.3 Older / adjacent frameworks

For completeness, because your reps will encounter these in the wild:

**BANT** — Budget, Authority, Need, Timeline. Invented at IBM in the 1960s. Still used widely for SDR qualification (especially inbound lead qualification) because it's simple. Weak for complex deals — doesn't address process, pain quantification, or champion dynamics. Fine as a rough-cut first filter; inadequate as a forecasting framework.

**CHAMP** — Challenges, Authority, Money, Prioritization. A reframe of BANT that leads with challenges (pain) instead of budget. Slightly better than BANT; same structural limitations.

**ANUM** — Authority, Need, Urgency, Money. Another BANT variant prioritizing authority first. Lightweight.

**GPCTBA/C&I** (HubSpot) — Goals, Plans, Challenges, Timeline, Budget, Authority / Consequences & Implications. HubSpot's extension of BANT with consequences/implications appended (a nod to SPIN). Fine for mid-market inbound motions; unwieldy for enterprise.

**FAINT** — Funds, Authority, Interest, Need, Timeline. Optimized for prospecting into accounts without a defined budget — recognizes that enterprise deals often create budget rather than draw from existing budget. Useful for outbound into greenfield accounts.

**Sandler Submarine / Sandler Pain Funnel** — Sandler Training's methodology. Different philosophy: up-front contracts (meeting agreements), the Pain Funnel (a sequence of discovery questions drilling from symptom to root cause), and a "disqualify hard" posture. Strong on CX; demands training to execute.

### Pragmatic recommendation

Don't let your team collect frameworks. Pick one enterprise-grade (MEDDPICC or SPICED) and enforce it as the deal-language standard. Anything else becomes noise.

# Part 3 — Solution-to-Prospect Mapping

Once the prospect is diagnosed (Part 1) and qualified (Part 2), the rep must map them to a specific offer. This is the discipline Part 3 addresses: connecting diagnosed pain to delivered capability to demonstrated outcome. Done well, the prospect hears a *specific* recommendation and feels the rep understands both their problem and the solution space. Done badly, the rep pitches a product.

## 3.1 Solution Selling — Bosworth / Eades

**Source:** *Solution Selling* by Michael Bosworth (1994); *The New Solution Selling* by Keith Eades (2003). Sales Performance International (SPI) commercialized the methodology. Dated in tone, still structurally sound, and the origin of several concepts every enterprise rep uses without attribution.

### Core premise

*Selling is not pitching features; it's helping a buyer create a vision of a solution to a pain they didn't fully understand yet.* The rep's job is to guide the buyer from latent pain → admitted pain → vision of a solution → purchase.

### Key concepts every rep should internalize

**The Pain Chain.** Pain cascades through an organization. An AE's pain (low quota attainment) causes the VP Sales' pain (missed number) which causes the CRO's pain (pipeline coverage) which causes the CFO's pain (revenue forecast). A rep who understands the pain chain can speak to multiple stakeholders in their own terms and show how your solution resolves pain at multiple levels simultaneously.

**Buyer Readiness Levels:**
1. **Latent pain** — buyer has a problem but isn't aware of it or isn't willing to act.
2. **Admitted pain** — buyer acknowledges the problem.
3. **Vision of a solution** — buyer has a mental picture of how the problem could be solved.
4. **Evaluating options** — buyer is comparing approaches.
5. **Purchase** — buyer is making the commitment.

Selling to latent pain requires a different approach than selling to vision-stage buyers. Most sales content treats all buyers identically; this is a major GTM error.

**Vision Creation vs. Vision Reengineering.** If you're the first vendor in, you're creating the buyer's vision of a solution — you can shape it to match your capabilities. If a competitor got there first, you're *reengineering* their vision — harder, but possible if you can introduce new criteria or reframe the problem.

**The "9 Block Vision Processing Model."** Bosworth's discovery scaffold. For each pain, probe three dimensions (reason for the pain, magnitude, who else is affected) across three angles (diagnostic, visionary, proof). Junior reps find it mechanical but it's a useful scaffold until discovery becomes second-nature.

**Reference Stories.** Bosworth's specific format: *"[Company X] in [situation similar to yours] had [pain]. They adopted [solution]. They achieved [quantified outcome]."* This is not a case study — it's a conversational micro-story a rep can deploy in a call to help a buyer envision success. Reps should have 6–10 reference stories rehearsed.

**Sponsor vs. Power Sponsor.** Sponsor = someone who'll introduce you to the right person. Power Sponsor = the Economic Buyer or someone with direct access. The rep's early-stage job is to convert sponsors into power sponsors or get referred up.

**"Letter to the Power" / Evaluation Plan.** A formal document the rep co-authors with the champion, summarizing the business case and proposed success criteria, addressed to the Economic Buyer. Signals the rep is serious and gives the champion a tool to sell internally.

### Junior-rep applicability

Medium. The language is dated and some reps find it stilted. But the concepts — pain chain, readiness levels, reference stories, evaluation plan — are in the water supply of modern enterprise selling. Reps should learn the concepts even if they never read the book.

### Limits / critiques

- The original books read like they were written for 1990s IT sales. Translate the ideas to modern buyer contexts.
- Works best for complex, considered B2B purchases. Overkill for SMB and PLG motions.
- "Vision creation" can shade into manipulation if abused. Use with the CX discipline from Part 5.

## 3.2 Command of the Message — Force Management

**Source:** Force Management's "Command of the Message" methodology. Force Management was founded by John Kaplan and Grant Wilson; John McMahon's book *The Qualified Sales Leader* (2021) is the most accessible public reference. The methodology itself is paid/licensed.

### Core premise

*Sell to business issues, not features. Every sales conversation should be grounded in a prospect business problem, the capabilities required to solve it, and the quantified outcomes you deliver — not in your product catalog.*

### The Value Framework

Command of the Message organizes every customer conversation around a five-layer value structure:

1. **Business issue** — the strategic, executive-level problem the prospect is grappling with. ("We need to scale revenue from $50M to $100M without doubling sales headcount.")
2. **Required capabilities** — the capabilities any solution to that issue would need. ("Automated pipeline generation, reduced CAC, faster ramp to productivity.")
3. **Your differentiated capabilities** — what *you* do, specifically, better than alternatives. ("AI-driven outbound with proven 40% reply rate vs. industry 8%.")
4. **Positive Business Outcomes (PBOs)** — the quantified results the buyer achieves. ("Reduce CAC by 30% in 6 months.")
5. **Metrics and proof** — evidence that the PBOs are real. Customer case data, third-party validation, named references.

The flow runs top-down: you cannot talk about capabilities until you've established the business issue, and you cannot talk about PBOs until you've grounded capabilities in the buyer's context.

### "Why Change? Why Now? Why Us?"

Force Management's three-question framework for any enterprise deal:

- **Why change?** — pain-driven: the status quo is unsustainable.
- **Why now?** — urgency-driven: the critical event forces timing.
- **Why us?** — differentiation-driven: our approach is demonstrably better.

A deal that answers all three closes. A deal missing one answers stalls at that step: weak "why change" = no deal; weak "why now" = deal slips; weak "why us" = competitor wins.

### Champion Enablement

Force Management's emphasis on *enabling the champion to sell internally*. The rep's job is not to close the deal; it's to make the champion into a rep who can close the deal when the rep isn't in the room. Concretely: arm the champion with a deck, a one-pager, a business case template, reference contacts, and a specific story they can tell to the Economic Buyer.

This is one of the most under-implemented practices in junior rep work. Most reps treat the champion as a conduit of information. Top reps treat the champion as a co-seller who requires enablement materials.

### Junior-rep applicability

High, once taught. The Value Framework gives reps a clean structure for discovery and for proposal writing. "Why change, why now, why us" is a memorable diagnostic test applied to any opportunity. Force Management is expensive training ($$$), but the public concepts can be extracted from McMahon's book and applied freely.

### Limits / critiques

- Optimized for enterprise SaaS; heavy for SMB.
- Relies on prospect having a clear business issue at the executive level — not always true for bottoms-up adoption or PLG motions.
- Formal training is cost-prohibitive for smaller teams; self-taught implementations can miss nuances.

## 3.3 Mastering Technical Sales — Care / Bohlig

**Source:** *Mastering Technical Sales: The Sales Engineer's Handbook* by John Care and Aron Bohlig (1st ed. 2002, 4th ed. 2020). Canonical reference for Sales Engineers / Solution Consultants. Relevant here because any B2B offer with a technical dimension will be evaluated against a demo or proof-of-value exercise, and reps must partner with SEs effectively.

### Core premise

*A demo is not a feature tour. It is a targeted response to discovered pain, delivered to move a deal to the next stage.* Most demos fail because they answer questions the buyer didn't ask.

### Key concepts

**"Death by Demo."** The canonical failure mode: a generic, comprehensive demo that shows every feature the product has. Feels comprehensive to the rep; exhausting to the buyer; produces no commitment. The remedy is disciplined discovery before demo and ruthless editing of demo content to what the buyer actually needs to see.

**Discovery before Demo.** Care & Bohlig's central rule. No demo should occur before a rep and SE can complete a pre-demo brief: who will attend, what are their roles, what pain does each care about, what are the specific capabilities to demonstrate, what are the success criteria for the meeting.

**Proof of Value (POV) vs. Proof of Concept (POC).** A POC is a technical test: does it work? A POV is a business test: does it produce the outcome we agreed to measure? POVs have defined success criteria, timeline, evaluator team, and a go/no-go decision at the end. POCs too often become open-ended trials that consume rep and SE time without commitment. Rule: no POC. Only POVs with written success criteria and a named decision date.

**The Technical Win ≠ the Business Win.** A buyer can be convinced the product works (technical win) and still not buy (missing business win). Reps focused only on the technical win lose deals to buyers who can't build the business case. Reps must simultaneously run the technical track (with the SE and technical buyer) and the business track (with the economic buyer and champion).

**Objection Handling at Technical Level.** Most technical objections are symptoms of missing information or misunderstood requirements, not product limitations. Care & Bohlig teach: acknowledge, probe to root cause, address, confirm resolution. Don't answer the surface objection; diagnose why it was raised.

### Junior-rep applicability

High when paired with an SE; essential in any technical sale. The discipline of pre-demo briefing, demo editing, and POV-not-POC prevents the majority of technical-sales failure modes.

### Limits / critiques

- Specific to technical B2B (SaaS, infrastructure, developer tools). Overkill for non-technical consulting or services.
- Older editions' advice on in-person selling is dated; modern remote-first selling has its own demo discipline (screensharing, recording, async review).

---

## 3.4 Synthesis — solution mapping for junior reps

All three frameworks converge on a single principle: **your offer is mapped to the prospect when the prospect can articulate what they'll achieve, how they'll know, and by when — in their own words.** Not when the rep has pitched effectively. Not when the demo went well. When the *buyer* has articulated the outcome.

Practical sequence for a junior rep:

1. **Run Part 1 (diagnosis) to completion.** Don't advance until the PIC is complete and SPICED's Critical Event is named.
2. **Frame with Value Framework (§3.2).** Take the diagnosed pain and map it through the five layers (business issue → capabilities → differentiation → PBOs → metrics).
3. **Deploy reference stories (§3.1).** Have 6–10 rehearsed, specific, situation-matched stories. Use them to help the buyer envision success.
4. **Treat demos as diagnostics, not tours (§3.3).** Every demo moment maps to a specific pain the buyer stated.
5. **Enable the champion to sell internally (§3.2).** If the rep is the only one selling, the deal will stall.

# Part 4 — Offer Design

None of the preceding works if the offers themselves are vague. A rep cannot map a prospect to "consulting" — but they can map a prospect to "the 12-week Revenue Architecture Program." Off-the-shelf offers require explicit, sharp, named design.

## 4.1 $100M Offers — Hormozi

**Source:** *$100M Offers: How to Make Offers So Good People Feel Stupid Saying No* by Alex Hormozi (2021). Hormozi's voice is tactical, consumer-flavored, and loud; the principles translate to B2B if you can tolerate the tone.

### Core premise

*The offer is the product. A mediocre product with a brilliant offer beats a brilliant product with a mediocre offer — in sales, in marketing, in everything.* The offer is the specific bundle of value, guarantee, price, and terms the buyer considers. Sharpen the offer and selling becomes easier because the offer does more of the work.

### The Value Equation

Hormozi's central framework for designing offers:

$$
\text{Value} = \frac{\text{Dream Outcome} \times \text{Perceived Likelihood of Achievement}}{\text{Time Delay} \times \text{Effort \& Sacrifice}}
$$

Four levers to move:

1. **Dream outcome** — what the buyer actually wants, stated in their language. Maximize.
2. **Perceived likelihood of achievement** — proof, guarantees, case data, specificity. Maximize.
3. **Time delay** — how long until they get it. Minimize.
4. **Effort & sacrifice** — what they have to do, give up, or risk. Minimize.

An offer that scores well on all four is irresistible. Most B2B offers fail on #2 (weak proof) and #3 (long time-to-value).

### Problem inventory

Hormozi's tactical move for offer design: list every problem a buyer might have before, during, and after buying. Then design the offer to pre-empt each one. This is the source of bonuses, guarantees, onboarding accelerators, and scope clarifications. B2B application: each "yeah but what if..." question in a discovery call is a problem that should be pre-empted in the offer itself.

### Naming formula

Hormozi's formula for offer names: Magnetic Reason + Avatar + Result + Container. Example: "The 6-Week Revenue Audit for Series B SaaS CROs." You know who it's for, what they get, and how long it takes without reading further.

B2B translation: name your offers like SKUs, not like services. "Consulting engagement" is a category. "Revenue Architecture Diagnostic" is an offer. Buyers buy offers, not categories.

### Guarantees

Hormozi argues that a strong guarantee converts dramatically better than an equivalent discount — and costs the business less because most buyers don't invoke it. Guarantees reduce *perceived* risk, which is the dominant barrier to purchase once value is established.

B2B application: guarantees can be structural ("defined deliverables by defined date or fees refunded") or outcome-based ("if metric X doesn't move, we refund"). Most B2B services companies under-use guarantees.

### Junior-rep applicability

Indirect but important. Reps don't design offers, but reps *suffer* when offers are vague. A rep with sharp, Hormozi-style offers can quote pricing, scope, and timeline in the first call; a rep with fuzzy offers can't.

### Limits / critiques

- Tone is consumer-marketing-flavored and can feel hypey in B2B.
- Some tactics (urgency, scarcity, aggressive guarantees) don't translate to enterprise buying.
- The book underweights positioning — an offer without positioning still has no home.

---

## 4.2 The Business of Expertise — Baker

**Source:** *The Business of Expertise: How Entrepreneurial Experts Convert Insight to Impact + Wealth* by David C. Baker (2017); also *Secret Tradecraft of Elite Advisors* (2022). Baker runs Punctuation and is the canonical advisor for consultancies, agencies, and firms. Co-hosts the *2Bobs* podcast with Blair Enns.

### Core premise

*Expertise is created through deliberate positioning, not accumulated through experience.* A generalist with 20 years of experience is a commodity; a specialist with 3 years in one vertical/service is an expert. The business case for specialization is not preference — it's economics. Specialists command higher fees, shorter sales cycles, better referrals, and stronger authority.

### Positioning = Vertical × Horizontal

Baker's core model: positioning is the intersection of a vertical (industry you serve) and a horizontal (service you deliver). Examples:

- Vertical only: "We serve SaaS companies." (Too broad — any service.)
- Horizontal only: "We do positioning consulting." (Too broad — any industry.)
- Both: "We do positioning consulting for Series B fintech companies." (Specific — searchable, referable, priceable.)

A firm can specialize on one axis heavily and the other axis loosely, but complete lack of specialization on both is commodity.

### How authority is built

Baker's formula: **Positioning × Expertise × Demonstration = Authority.** All three must be present. Positioning without demonstration is a claim. Expertise without positioning is invisible. Demonstration without expertise is showmanship.

Demonstration means:

- **Writing** — books, articles, long-form content with a point of view.
- **Speaking** — at events where target buyers are present.
- **Proprietary research** — surveys, benchmarks, studies the firm conducts and publishes.
- **Published case studies** — named, quantified, specific.

Authority is built visibly over years. There is no shortcut.

### Pricing follows positioning

Specialists price higher than generalists for the same deliverable. A generalist's $10k engagement is a specialist's $40k engagement because the specialist can credibly claim faster time-to-value, higher probability of outcome, and pre-built methodology. Junior-rep implication: if your firm's offers are priced like a generalist's, your positioning isn't sharp enough yet.

### Junior-rep applicability

Indirect. Reps don't design positioning. But reps working for a sharply-positioned firm sell more easily because the firm's authority does pre-qualification for them. Buyers arrive aware; reps convert higher.

### Limits / critiques

- Baker writes for firms, not products. Translation to product-company positioning is possible but not automatic.
- Less prescriptive on execution than on principle.
- The "specialization" argument can be misread as "pick any narrow niche" — the niche must also have enough buyers to sustain a business.

---

## 4.3 Win Without Pitching Manifesto — Enns

**Source:** *The Win Without Pitching Manifesto* by Blair Enns (2010); follow-up *Pricing Creativity* (2018). Enns runs Win Without Pitching, a training organization for agencies and consultancies. Co-hosts *2Bobs* with David C. Baker.

### Core premise

*Experts don't pitch. They're consulted.* The pitching posture — preparing a formal response, presenting in competition, hoping to win — is a commodity posture. Experts engage as advisors from the first interaction, with the authority to ask questions, set terms, and qualify the prospect as much as the prospect qualifies them.

Written as a twelve-proclamation manifesto, each one a stake in the ground against common industry practices.

### The twelve proclamations (compressed)

1. **We will specialize.**
2. **We will replace presentations with conversations.**
3. **We will diagnose before we prescribe.**
4. **We will rethink what we sell.**
5. **We will not solve the client's problem in the sale.**
6. **We will address issues of money early.**
7. **We will be selective.**
8. **We will build expertise rapidly through focus.**
9. **We will balance ego — we are neither desperate nor arrogant.**
10. **We will charge more — and be worth it.**
11. **We will refuse to work at a loss.**
12. **We will free ourselves from clients who do not value our expertise.**

Each is a refusal of a bad industry practice and a commitment to an alternative.

### Key concept — "diagnose before prescribe"

Aligns directly with Gap Selling and Solution Selling. Enns's formulation is sharper on the sale dynamic: *you are not qualified to recommend a solution until you have thoroughly diagnosed the problem, and the prospect knows you have.* A vendor who recommends before diagnosing signals commodity; an expert who diagnoses before recommending signals authority.

Practical move: have an offer specifically for diagnosis — a paid diagnostic engagement that precedes any larger engagement. This does three things: (1) qualifies the prospect by their willingness to invest in understanding the problem, (2) generates insight that makes the larger engagement far more effective, and (3) positions the firm as an expert, not a vendor.

### Key concept — "address money early"

Enns argues that money discussions delayed to the end of the process waste everyone's time. Pricing should be addressed conversationally in the first discovery call — not a formal quote, but a range, a reaction, and a test of whether the prospect can afford the firm. Prospects who can't should be disqualified or re-routed to a smaller offer. Reps who hide pricing until proposal waste weeks on deals that were unaffordable from minute one.

### Junior-rep applicability

Medium. The philosophy is more useful for founders and senior sellers than for junior reps, but junior reps benefit from the posture — refusing to pitch, qualifying on affordability early, leading with diagnosis. The manifesto is 40 pages; every new rep should read it.

### Limits / critiques

- Written for agencies and creative firms; adaptation to B2B SaaS is possible but requires translation.
- The "don't pitch" posture can be misapplied: enterprise procurement *requires* formal responses to RFPs. Enns's answer is to be selective about which RFPs you respond to, not to refuse all formal processes.
- The manifesto style can read as absolutist. The principles hold; the applications need judgment.

---

## 4.4 Synthesis — offer architecture for productized services

For a firm like Cambrian Catalyst to have junior-rep-usable offers, the offers must pass four tests drawn from these three authors:

1. **Specialized** (Baker) — named vertical, named service, named buyer.
2. **Sharp in the Value Equation** (Hormozi) — clear dream outcome, high proof of achievement, short time delay, low effort.
3. **Positioned as expert, not vendor** (Enns) — diagnosis precedes recommendation; pricing addressed early; selectivity maintained.
4. **Visibly demonstrated** (Baker) — writing, speaking, case studies, proprietary research backing the offer's credibility.

Offers that fail any of these tests cannot be reliably sold by junior reps — they will default to pitching features because there's nothing sharper to reference.

# Part 5 — Customer Experience as a Sales Principle

The goal stated up front — *being sold to can be exhausting and full of disappointment* — is the heart of these three books. They're all CEB / Gartner research–based and they converge on a single counterintuitive finding: **buyers don't want delight in the buying process. They want less effort, clearer recommendations, and help deciding.**

## 5.1 The Challenger Customer

**Source:** *The Challenger Customer: Selling to the Hidden Influencer Who Can Multiply Your Results* by Brent Adamson, Matthew Dixon, Pat Spenner, and Nick Toman (2015). The sequel to *The Challenger Sale* (2011). Based on CEB (now Gartner) research across thousands of B2B buying processes.

### Core premise (research finding)

*Enterprise B2B buying is decided by groups, not individuals, and those groups are structurally bad at reaching good decisions.* The research found an average of 5.4 people involved in a typical enterprise purchase (updated in later Gartner research to 6–10+). The dynamics of committee buying — consensus-seeking, lowest-common-denominator compromise, political risk-aversion — produce decisions that are worse for the buying organization than an individual decision would be.

The corollary for sellers: a rep who "sells" individual stakeholders and tries to aggregate support ends up with a committee that is enthusiastic-in-parts but won't converge. Deals stall at the committee level even after individual stakeholders are convinced.

### Mobilizers vs. Talkers

CEB's research identified seven stakeholder archetypes within buying committees. Three are "Mobilizers" (drive change, build consensus, close deals); four are "Talkers" (engage with reps but don't drive decisions internally). Most reps spend their time with Talkers — who are more accessible and enthusiastic — while Mobilizers are the ones who actually move deals.

**Mobilizer archetypes:**

- **Go-Getters** — outcome-driven, push for change when they see opportunity.
- **Teachers** — persuasive internal communicators, shape others' thinking.
- **Skeptics** — demand rigor; slow to commit, powerful when they do.

**Talker archetypes** (useful for information, not for closing):

- **Guides** — give useful information but don't advocate.
- **Friends** — accessible, agreeable, but low-influence.
- **Climbers** — self-interested, unreliable.
- **Blockers** — actively oppose change.

Practical implication: identify the Mobilizers early and invest disproportionately in them. Do not mistake enthusiastic Talkers for Mobilizers.

### Commercial Insight

The Challenger methodology's central teaching tool. A "commercial insight" is a point of view the seller teaches the buyer about the buyer's own business — specifically, a fact or framing the buyer didn't know that reframes the problem in a way favorable to the seller's solution. Example: "Most companies in your segment assume their onboarding costs are in their CAC; our research shows they're actually in their churn numbers — and they can be addressed differently."

Effective commercial insight has three features: (1) it's surprising to the buyer, (2) it's defensible with data, and (3) it naturally leads toward the seller's differentiated capability. It is not a product pitch disguised as insight.

### The 5.4-buyer problem and "consensus selling"

Key finding: consensus-based selling (trying to get every stakeholder to say yes) produces deals that are structurally fragile because *any one no* kills the consensus. The counterintuitive alternative: help a Mobilizer drive the decision through the committee with enough authority and air-cover that a minority objection doesn't derail it.

This is why Champion Enablement (Part 3.2) matters so much. The champion is the Mobilizer inside the account; their job is not to aggregate consensus but to drive a decision through the group.

### Junior-rep applicability

High at the concept level, medium at execution. Junior reps benefit from the Mobilizer/Talker distinction because it prevents them from wasting time with enthusiastic Talkers who can't close. The Commercial Insight practice requires senior-level content development (research, frameworks, data); junior reps consume insights rather than produce them.

### Limits / critiques

- The Mobilizer archetypes can oversimplify individual buyers — real humans don't fit cleanly into 7 boxes.
- "Challenger" as a selling style can be misapplied as abrasive, contrarian, or dismissive. Done well, it's pedagogical; done poorly, it's obnoxious.
- The underlying research is proprietary and not fully replicable; some findings have been challenged.

## 5.2 The JOLT Effect

**Source:** *The JOLT Effect: How High Performers Overcome Customer Indecision* by Matthew Dixon and Ted McKenna (2022). Based on analysis of ~2.5 million recorded sales conversations using machine learning to identify patterns distinguishing high-performing reps from average ones — specifically around deals that ended in "no decision" rather than "lost to competitor."

### Core premise (research finding)

*40–60% of qualified B2B pipeline ends in no-decision.* Most sales methodology has optimized for "win vs. lose to competitor." The actual dominant failure mode is "buyer stalls and doesn't buy anything." Status quo bias is real, but this research identifies the deeper cause: buyer **indecision**, which is a different phenomenon.

The indecision problem is under-addressed because reps interpret stalls as status-quo-bias (buyer prefers current state) when they're actually *decision paralysis* (buyer can't make a confident choice). Different cause → different remedy.

### The three sources of indecision

1. **Valuation problem** — the buyer faces too many options and can't determine which is best. More options *lowers* close rates above a certain point.
2. **Lack of information** — the buyer feels they don't have enough information to decide and keeps asking for more. Usually a dodge for uncertainty rather than a real information gap.
3. **Outcome uncertainty** — the buyer fears making the wrong choice and being blamed. Career risk, political risk, financial risk.

Each source requires a different intervention. A rep who responds to every stall with "more information" makes valuation problems and outcome uncertainty worse.

### The JOLT framework

Dixon and McKenna's four-step model for helping an indecisive buyer decide:

**J — Judge the level of indecision.** Assess which of the three sources is dominant. Signals: buyer asks for excessive information (lack of info), buyer circles between options without progress (valuation), buyer expresses fear of being wrong (outcome uncertainty).

**O — Offer your recommendation.** Be directive. High performers make specific recommendations ("for your situation, Package B is the right choice, and here's why") rather than presenting balanced options. Buyers appreciate being told, not asked to self-select from a menu.

**L — Limit the exploration.** Cap the options explicitly. "We offer three configurations, but only two of them make sense for your situation. Let's focus on those and skip the third." This counters the valuation problem directly.

**T — Take risk off the table.** Reduce outcome uncertainty with guarantees, pilots, phased rollouts, opt-outs, or anything else that lowers the buyer's perceived risk of being wrong.

### Counterintuitive findings

The JOLT research produced several findings that contradict conventional selling wisdom:

- **Offering more options hurts close rates.** The "choice is good" assumption is wrong above ~3 options.
- **Giving more information often hurts.** Buyers in outcome-uncertainty mode use "more information" as an avoidance tactic; feeding them more doesn't help.
- **Being directive correlates with winning.** High performers *tell* more than average performers. "I recommend X" closes more than "here are your options, what do you think?"
- **Guarantees and de-risking mechanisms close deals.** Not through their economic value — through their psychological risk reduction.

### Junior-rep applicability

Extremely high. JOLT gives reps permission to do what their instincts already suggest (be directive, narrow options, de-risk) but which most sales training discourages (as "pushy" or "unprofessional"). A junior rep who internalizes JOLT produces a *lower-effort buying experience* for the prospect — directly serving the CX goal.

The second-order benefit: JOLT mitigates the common junior-rep failure of "being too helpful." Over-helpfulness manifests as excess information, too many options, reluctance to recommend — all of which worsen indecision and tank close rates. JOLT gives juniors a framework for being helpful *in the way buyers actually want*.

### Limits / critiques

- The research is from recorded conversations, which may over-sample certain deal types.
- "Be directive" requires judgment; done badly it becomes pushy or paternalistic.
- The findings challenge the "consultative selling" orthodoxy and will feel counterintuitive to some sales leaders.

## 5.3 The Effortless Experience

**Source:** *The Effortless Experience: Conquering the New Battleground for Customer Loyalty* by Matthew Dixon, Nick Toman, and Rick DeLisi (2013). Also CEB/Gartner research. Originally about customer service; the principles translate directly to sales.

### Core premise (research finding)

*Delight doesn't drive loyalty; effort does.* Counterintuitive finding from multi-year research: customers who experience "delight" (above-and-beyond service) aren't more loyal than customers who experience adequate service. Customers who experience *high effort* are dramatically less loyal. The variable to optimize is not delight upward; it's effort downward.

### Customer Effort Score (CES)

Dixon's team proposed CES as a better loyalty predictor than NPS. A single question: "How much effort did you personally have to put forth to handle your request?" (1–7 scale). CES correlates with retention and word-of-mouth more strongly than NPS in CEB's data.

For sales: *how much effort did the prospect have to expend to buy from you?* Friction in the buying process — lengthy forms, excessive follow-ups, opaque pricing, hidden requirements, bouncing between reps — generates effort that reduces conversion and damages long-term relationships even when the deal closes.

### Effort-reduction principles applied to sales

1. **Anticipate next issues.** Instead of answering the question and stopping, answer the question and pre-empt the next three related questions. "You asked about X; you'll probably also want to know Y and Z, so here's that too."
2. **Don't bounce buyers between reps.** Hand-offs from SDR to AE to SE to Customer Success each add effort. Minimize and smooth handoffs; provide warm introductions, not cold re-starts.
3. **Acknowledge and validate effort.** When a buyer has done work (filled out a form, attended a demo, reviewed a proposal), acknowledge it. This is cheap and dramatically improves experience.
4. **Make the next step obvious.** Never leave a buyer wondering what happens next. Every interaction ends with a concrete next action, owner, and date.
5. **Engineer the buying process, don't improvise it.** Repeatable buying experiences reduce effort; snowflake experiences increase it.

### Junior-rep applicability

High. Effort-reduction is a discipline juniors can apply immediately without needing experience. It pairs perfectly with JOLT (being directive, limiting options) — both reduce buyer effort and both counter the industry's obsession with "delighting" prospects through over-service.

### Limits / critiques

- Original book focused on customer service; the sales translation requires some interpretation.
- Over-applied, effort-reduction can produce cold, transactional buying experiences. Pair with relational touches (Part 5.1 Mobilizer work, Part 4.3 Enns's expert posture).

---

## 5.4 Synthesis — CX-first selling for junior reps

The three books converge on an operating principle for the CX-first rep: **be directive, be selective, reduce effort, help the buyer decide.** The opposite of the "smooth salesperson" archetype — and the opposite of what overloaded buyers dread.

Concretely, a junior rep executing CX-first selling:

- Diagnoses before recommending (Part 1).
- Qualifies with discipline and disqualifies visibly (Part 2 + Enns's selectivity).
- Maps to a specific offer and says so directly ("I think Offer B is the fit here, not Offer A or C") (Part 3 + JOLT).
- Enables the champion to decide through the committee rather than trying to aggregate consensus (Challenger Customer).
- Reduces the effort of the buying process — clear next steps, pre-empted questions, warm handoffs, no information dumps (Effortless Experience).
- When the buyer hesitates, diagnoses *why* (valuation / info / outcome uncertainty) and responds appropriately rather than defaulting to "more information" (JOLT).

The experience from the buyer's side: the rep understood their situation, made a specific recommendation, helped them navigate committee politics, and made the process low-effort. Even if the buyer doesn't purchase, the experience was valuable. That is the CX-first posture operationalized.

## 6.2 The Discovery Question Library

Organized by framework layer. A rep pulls questions as needed, not as a script.

### Situation questions (use sparingly; do homework first)
- "Can you walk me through your current [relevant process]?"
- "Who owns [function/metric] today? How is the team structured?"
- "What tools are you using for [relevant workflow]?"
- "How long have you been in your role? What did you inherit?"

### Problem questions (surface implied needs)
- "Where in [process] does it feel like things break down?"
- "What's the part of [this workflow] that takes longer than it should?"
- "What about the current setup isn't working?"
- "What have you tried before that didn't stick?"

### Implication questions (expand pain to business impact)
- "If this continues through next quarter, what does that mean for [metric]?"
- "How does this affect [adjacent team or function]?"
- "What does this cost you in [revenue / time / headcount] over a year?"
- "How is this showing up in your board reporting?"

### Need-payoff questions (buyer articulates value)
- "If you could fix [X] by [date], what would that unlock?"
- "What would be different for your team if [problem] was resolved?"
- "How would you measure success if this was working?"
- "What does 'working' look like six months from now?"

### JTBD / timeline questions (Moesta)
- "When did you first start thinking this needed to change?"
- "Between then and now, what shifted that made you start looking actively?"
- "What have you evaluated? What did you rule out?"
- "What would need to happen for you to decide by [date]?"

### MEDDPICC qualification questions
- **Metrics:** "How will you measure success on this? What number moves?"
- **Economic Buyer:** "Who signs off on decisions like this? Would they be part of our next conversation?"
- **Decision Criteria:** "What criteria will you use to choose a vendor? Are those written down?"
- **Decision Process:** "Walk me through what happens from here to a signed contract."
- **Paper Process:** "Have you gone through security/legal/procurement with a similar vendor recently? How long did that take?"
- **Identify Pain:** "What's the cost of not solving this by [date]?"
- **Champion:** "Who else on your team feels this? Who would push this forward internally?"
- **Competition:** "What else are you considering, including doing nothing or building it yourself?"

### SPICED Critical Event question
- "What's forcing action on this by [timeline]? What happens if nothing changes?"

### JOLT — indecision diagnosis questions
- "What's the main thing making this hard to decide?"
- "Is the challenge picking among options, not having enough information, or something else?"
- "What would make you feel confident in a decision by [date]?"

| **Total (weighted, /5)** | | | **100%** | |

**Thresholds:**
- ≥ 4.0 → Commit. High confidence in close this quarter.
- 3.0–3.9 → Best case. Needs specific weak-letter intervention to become commit.
- 2.0–2.9 → Upside. Real but unlikely this quarter; plan for next quarter.
- < 2.0 → Disqualify or nurture. Don't forecast.

Review weekly in pipeline meeting. The weak letter(s) for each deal become the rep's action items.

## 6.4 The Offer Decision Tree

A one-page diagram (or text flow) that routes a diagnosed prospect to the right offer or a referral. Sample logic:

```
Q1: Is there a named Critical Event within 6 months?
  No → Nurture. Not in-market.
  Yes → continue

Q2: Does the diagnosed gap match any of our off-the-shelf offer types?
  No → Refer out (specific partners by gap type).
  Yes → continue

Q3: Which gap type?
  Gap A (e.g., pipeline generation) → Offer 1
  Gap B (e.g., CRO fractional support) → Offer 2
  Gap C (e.g., positioning/messaging rebuild) → Offer 3

Q4: Do they meet the ICP qualifiers for the selected offer?
  (firmographic + buying authority + compelling event)
  No → Adjust offer or refer out.
  Yes → continue

Q5: Is the Economic Buyer accessible in the next 2 meetings?
  No → Champion enablement phase (don't propose yet).
  Yes → proceed to proposal.

Q6: Is the buyer showing indecision signals (valuation / info / outcome)?
  No → proceed.
  Yes → apply JOLT: narrow options, make specific recommendation, de-risk.
```

Visual form recommended — junior reps absorb flowcharts faster than paragraphs.

## 6.5 Disqualification & Partner Referral Protocol

Disqualifying is a revenue strategy (Part 5 of the ICP core doc covers this at the account level; this covers it at the deal level).

**Three outcomes when a prospect isn't a fit:**

1. **Not right now** — the prospect is a good ICP fit but no Critical Event exists. Route to nurture with a specific re-engagement trigger.
2. **Not for us, but for someone** — gap doesn't match any offer, but you know who could help. Refer to a partner with a warm introduction; document the referral.
3. **Not anyone you know** — gap doesn't match any offer or partner. Honest exit with guidance.

**Script for an honest disqualification:**

> "Based on what you've shared, I don't think we're the right fit for this — specifically because [specific reason tied to their own diagnosis]. Here's what I'd suggest instead: [partner / alternative / revisit trigger]. If your situation changes — for example, if [specific trigger] — I'd love to reconnect. In the meantime, here's [something valuable]."

Ending with value (a named referral, a relevant resource, a specific piece of advice) is the CX move. The prospect leaves the conversation having gained something even though you didn't close.

**Partner referral protocol:**

- Keep a documented partner list: for each offer gap, 2–3 named partners you'd refer to.
- Warm-introduce, don't cold-refer. Email the partner with context before introducing.
- Track referrals out (and referrals in). Healthy partner ecosystems are reciprocal.
- Make the referral value-positive for the buyer: "I've known [person] for three years; they're sharper than we are on this specific problem."

## 6.6 Junior-Rep Enablement Path

A 90-day path for a junior rep to become offer-fit-capable.

**Week 1–2: Foundations.**
- Read: Gap Selling (short), ICP core knowledge base Parts 1–2, Solution-Fit Cards for all offers.
- Observe: 5 discovery calls from senior reps.
- Output: write one-page summaries of two offers in their own words.

**Week 3–4: Diagnosis practice.**
- Role-play discovery calls with peers. Record. Review.
- Fill out PICs (Keenan) for three real customer stories.
- Read: The Mom Test, Demand-Side Sales 101, Part 1 of this doc.

**Week 5–8: Supervised live discovery.**
- Run discovery calls with senior rep observing.
- Debrief with PIC + MEDDPICC scorecard.
- Learn the Discovery Question Library by tagging their own questions (S/P/I/N).

**Week 9–12: Qualification discipline.**
- Read: MEDDPICC book (Whyte), The JOLT Effect, The Challenger Customer.
- Run full qualification on 5 active deals. Present in pipeline review.
- Learn partner referral protocol; do one warm referral under supervision.

**End-of-90-days certification test.**
- Conduct a full discovery call (real or role-play) on a live deal.
- Produce: PIC, filled Deal-Level Fit Scorecard, Solution-Fit Card recommendation, next-step plan.
- Pass criteria: diagnose correctly, map to correct offer (or disqualify), articulate MEDDPICC letters.

After certification: rep can run discovery independently. Continue with monthly skill-reviews for the first year.

# Part 7 — Worked Example: Cambrian Catalyst's Revenue Architecture Program

An applied example showing how the frameworks cohere. (This is meant to be rough — the actual offer design work should be iterative.)

### The offer as designed

**Name:** The Revenue Architecture Diagnostic (6 weeks) + Revenue Architecture Program (12 weeks execution).

**Who it's for (ICP slice):** Series B B2B SaaS in fintech / payments / martech-adjacent verticals, $3M–$15M ARR, newly-hired CRO (or founder-run sales), visible pipeline or conversion problem, board pressure to professionalize GTM.

**Who it's not for:** Seed or very-early-stage companies without funded GTM motion; enterprise companies with existing mature RevOps functions; industries outside the named verticals.

### Value Equation (Hormozi)

- **Dream outcome:** predictable, repeatable GTM motion that the CRO can scale without the founder bottlenecked.
- **Perceived likelihood:** specific published frameworks, named case references, outcome-based diagnostic engagement.
- **Time delay:** first deliverable in week 2 (diagnosis), program output in 12 weeks.
- **Effort required:** 2 hours/week of CRO time; specific data pulls; 3 customer interviews.

### Positioning (Baker / Enns)

- **Vertical × Horizontal:** fintech/payments/martech × GTM architecture. Sharper than "B2B SaaS consulting."
- **Authority demonstration:** public writing on fintech GTM; case studies from Blackhawk/Tango adjacent work; the ICP knowledge base itself as a proof artifact.
- **Pricing posture:** discussed in first call (Enns); diagnostic engagement de-risks the larger program (Enns); value-priced at specialist rate (Baker).

### Discovery (Gap Selling / SPIN)

- **PIC:** mapped during the diagnostic; filled by week 2.
- **SPIN sequencing:** minimal Situation questions (most facts confirmable from public sources); heavy Problem and Implication; Need-payoff questions surface the CRO's own articulation of value.

### Qualification (MEDDPICC / SPICED)

- **Metrics:** pipeline coverage ratio, SQL-to-Opp rate, ramp time, win rate. Baseline captured in diagnostic.
- **Economic Buyer:** CEO for CRO-led purchases; CRO for mid-engagement scopes.
- **Decision Criteria:** fit to GTM stage, fintech/payments specialization, speed to value.
- **Decision Process:** diagnostic engagement is the decision process — it becomes the deciding moment for the larger engagement.
- **Paper Process:** simple services MSA + SOW; 2-week typical.
- **Identify Pain:** quantified via diagnostic.
- **Champion:** CRO (or CEO if pre-CRO). Enabled with frameworks and published assets.
- **Competition:** usually "do nothing," "hire a head of X," or "another fractional consultant." Address directly.

### CX-first (Challenger / JOLT / Effortless)

- **Mobilizer:** CRO is the Mobilizer in post-CRO companies; CEO in founder-led.
- **Commercial insight:** "Fintech GTM breaks differently than horizontal B2B SaaS because [specific insight from experience]; here's how."
- **JOLT:** explicit recommendation at end of diagnostic, limited options, risk-reduced via diagnostic-before-program structure.
- **Effortless:** every engagement phase has a defined deliverable, defined owner, and defined date. No open-ended scope.

### Solution-Fit Card abbreviated

- **Fit trigger:** new CRO, missed quarter, Series B close.
- **Disqualifier:** pre-Series-A, unclear economic buyer, industries outside named verticals, "we want a consultant who'll just execute what we say" (fails the Enns test).
- **Adjacent offers:** if they need only positioning work → positioning sprint (smaller offer). If they need interim execution → fractional CRO (different offer, potentially a partner referral).

# Part 8 — Common Failure Modes

The predictable ways offer-fit selling breaks down:

**Pitching before diagnosing.** Rep opens with product. No diagnosis possible. Buyer feels sold to. Converts poorly.

**Diagnosis theater.** Rep "does discovery" but doesn't actually change their recommendation based on what they learn. Goes through the motions. Buyers can tell.

**Framework over function.** Rep runs SPIN as a script, ticking off question types mechanically. Conversation feels robotic. Switch to letting the framework inform posture, not dictate dialogue.

**Feature-dumping the demo.** No discovery connection to demo content. Every feature shown. Buyer exhausted. "Death by demo" (Care & Bohlig).

**Confusing coach with champion.** Rep mistakes enthusiastic information-sharers for advocates. Deals stall when rep isn't in the room.

**Missing the Critical Event.** No time pressure. Deal slips quarter after quarter. Forecast is fiction.

**Over-helping.** Rep answers every question, presents all options, lets buyer self-select. Buyer stalls (valuation problem). JOLT would predict this.

**Hiding pricing until proposal.** Rep doesn't address money until weeks in. Buyer discovers the fit was wrong on affordability from minute one. Weeks wasted.

**Single-threading.** Rep sells one stakeholder, never expands. Committee kills the deal. Mobilizer work skipped.

**Soft disqualification.** Rep keeps bad-fit deals in pipeline because killing them feels like lost pipeline. Pipeline quality degrades; rep time wasted; buyer experience damaged.

**Off-the-shelf in name only.** Offers are categories, not specific SKUs. Rep can't cleanly recommend. Defaults to pitching. CX suffers.

**Champion without enablement.** Rep has a champion but doesn't arm them. Champion can't sell internally. Deal stalls.

**Hand-off chasms.** SDR → AE → SE → CS each a cold restart. Buyer re-tells their story 4 times. Effort compounds. NPS damaged even on won deals.

# Glossary

- **BANT** — Budget, Authority, Need, Timeline. IBM-era qualification framework. Lightweight.
- **CES (Customer Effort Score)** — single-question loyalty predictor focused on buyer effort.
- **Champion** — internal advocate with power, active selling behavior, and a personal win from the deal.
- **Coach** — internal contact who shares information but does not sell on your behalf.
- **Commercial Insight** — a point of view that reframes the buyer's problem in a way favorable to your solution.
- **Critical Event** — externally imposed or internally committed deadline that forces buying action by a date.
- **CS / FS / Gap** — Current State, Future State, Gap (Keenan).
- **Economic Buyer** — the single person with discretionary authority to sign the contract.
- **Effortless Experience** — CX principle that effort reduction matters more than delight.
- **Force Management** — enterprise sales methodology provider (Command of the Message).
- **Gap Selling** — Keenan's diagnostic-first methodology centered on CS-FS-Gap.
- **JOLT** — Judge, Offer, Limit, Take risk off (Dixon & McKenna).
- **JTBD** — Jobs To Be Done theory applied to purchase motivation.
- **MEDDPICC** — Metrics, Economic buyer, Decision criteria, Decision process, Paper process, Identify pain, Champion, Competition.
- **Mobilizer** — buyer archetype that drives decisions internally vs. merely shares information.
- **PBOs (Positive Business Outcomes)** — Force Management term for quantified business results.
- **PIC (Problem Identification Chart)** — Keenan's 4-column diagnostic worksheet.
- **POV / POC** — Proof of Value (measured against criteria) vs. Proof of Concept (technical test).
- **SPIN** — Situation, Problem, Implication, Need-payoff (Rackham's question hierarchy).
- **SPICED** — Situation, Pain, Impact, Critical Event, Decision (Winning by Design).
- **Solution Selling** — Bosworth/Eades pain-chain-and-vision methodology.
- **Supply-side / Demand-side selling** — Moesta's distinction between product-pitch and progress-helping postures.
- **Value Equation** — Hormozi's (Dream × Likelihood) / (Time × Effort) formula for offer strength.
- **Vision Creation / Vision Reengineering** — Solution Selling terms for first-in vs. follower competitive postures.
