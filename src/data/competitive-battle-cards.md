# Competitive Battle Cards — Template & Examples

> Battle cards exist because prospects will mention competitors, and reps will be tempted to either disparage them or cave ("yeah, they do have that"). Both lose deals. The goal of a battle card is to give the rep a specific, practiced response that reframes the comparison on an axis favorable to you, *without trashing the competitor*.

## How battle cards work

**The philosophy is Dunford, not warfare.** You do not "win" a competitive situation by being better on the buyer's existing evaluation criteria — those criteria were often shaped by the incumbent, so winning on them means winning on their home turf. You win by changing the buyer's evaluation criteria so that what you do well becomes what they're evaluating.

**Battle cards are for prepared responses, not scripts.** A rep who reads from a battle card sounds like a rep reading from a battle card. The goal is rehearsed fluency: the rep has internalized the content so that in the moment, their response sounds considered and conversational.

**"We don't disparage" is non-negotiable.** Trash-talking a competitor does three things, all bad: (1) signals insecurity, (2) teaches the buyer that all vendors talk this way, (3) turns the champion off because they have to defend that competitor or look stupid for considering them. Every battle card must pass the "would this be okay if the competitor's CEO heard it?" test.

**Update cadence:** quarterly review. Competitors change pricing, launch features, acquire/get acquired, pivot positioning. A battle card from 12 months ago is stale ammunition.

**Use cards for prep, not during calls.** Reps should review the relevant card before a call where the competitor is named or likely, not read from it during.

# Filled Example 1 — Large management consultancies (McKinsey, Bain, BCG, Deloitte)

```
---
# Battle Card: Large Management Consultancies
Last reviewed: 2026-04-20 | Owner: Joe
---
```

## Competitor overview

**Who they are:** McKinsey, Bain, BCG (MBB) at the top; Deloitte Digital, PwC, Accenture, EY-Parthenon in the broader tier. For GTM work specifically, Bain's Customer Strategy & Marketing practice, McKinsey's Marketing & Sales, and BCG's Go-To-Market practice are the direct comparables.

**How they'd describe themselves:** global strategic advisors, proprietary research, executive access, deep benchmarking data, trusted by boards and Fortune 500.

**How mid-market buyers describe them (honestly):** expensive, slow, PowerPoint-heavy, often staffed with junior consultants who've never operated a GTM motion, process-driven, stronger on strategic frameworks than operational execution.

**Pricing:** engagements typically start at $300k–$500k for focused work, $1M+ for multi-phase. Per-hour partner rates $800–$1,500+.

**Typical engagement shape:** 3–6 months, 2–5 person teams, executive-visible deliverables.

**Their strengths (honestly):**
- Brand legitimacy with boards and late-stage investors.
- Access to their research libraries and benchmark data.
- Strong facilitation of executive alignment.
- Useful for deeply complex organizational transformation.

**Their weaknesses (honestly):**
- Operating experience in early/mid-stage B2B SaaS: limited. Consultants have studied but often haven't run GTM motions.
- Speed: slow relative to mid-market company cadence.
- Cost efficiency for mid-market: poor — engagement scale often exceeds mid-market budgets.
- Vertical depth in specialized categories (fintech, payments, etc.): variable. Generalist frameworks over-applied.

## When we win against them

- Prospect is Series A–C (below their sweet spot).
- Prospect needs operational execution, not frameworks.
- Prospect has a fintech / payments / martech flavor where vertical depth matters.
- Prospect has been burned by "consultants who've never carried a bag."
- Budget is $25k–$120k (well below their engagement floor).
- Timeline is urgent (Q-on-Q cadence, not annual planning cycles).
- CEO/CRO wants a peer, not a practice — someone who can be in the room as an operator, not as a senior advisor to the practice.

## When they win against us

- Prospect is $500M+ revenue and needs board-level validation.
- Problem is enterprise-wide transformation, not GTM-specific.
- Incumbent relationship with the firm (existing MSA, M&A support, etc.).
- Prospect is a PE portfolio company with a PE firm that has MBB relationships.
- Prospect values brand legitimacy over operator experience (e.g., public-company prep, high-profile board).
- Problem scope genuinely exceeds a solo practitioner (e.g., 12-month multi-function transformation).

## Our reframe

**They want the buyer to evaluate on:** strategic sophistication, executive-level framework depth, research rigor, brand reputation.

**We want the buyer to evaluate on:** operator experience in your specific vertical, speed to operational outcomes, cost efficiency, senior-level attention for the duration of the engagement (vs. partner sells, associate delivers).

**The bridge:** the question isn't "who's better at consulting?" The question is "what does your business need in the next 90 days?" If you need frameworks presented to your board, hire MBB. If you need a GTM motion working by Q3, you need someone who has run one.

**Specific reframe language:**

*"MBB will do great work for you — they're legitimately excellent firms. The question I'd encourage you to ask is: who's actually doing the work once the partner is off-ramp? If it's a senior associate three years out of business school, and your problem is operational GTM in fintech, that's a mismatch with the problem. My entire practice is this one thing, in this one space, and I'm the person in every room from kickoff to close."*

## Trap questions to ask the buyer

1. **"Who specifically will be on your engagement team, at what time commitment?"** — exposes the partner-sells-associate-delivers pattern.
2. **"How many of their consultants have operated a GTM motion at a company your size?"** — exposes the framework-vs-operator gap.
3. **"What's their engagement price, and what does that pay for relative to what you need?"** — exposes the cost mismatch.
4. **"What's their standard engagement length? How does that compare to your timeline?"** — exposes speed mismatch.
5. **"If the problem turns out to be different than you thought mid-engagement, how flexible is their scope?"** — exposes process rigidity.

## Proof points

- Peer reference: [similar-stage company that chose us over MBB, outcome].
- Operator credibility: [specific prior role with named company at similar stage].
- Speed: our Diagnostic delivers in 6 weeks what equivalent MBB scope takes 3+ months.
- Cost: our Program delivers in $60–120k what MBB delivers in $400–800k.
- Continuity: same senior practitioner start to finish; their team rotates.

## Messaging DO's

- Acknowledge their legitimacy genuinely — they are real firms doing real work.
- Frame the choice as "which shape of help fits this problem," not "who's better."
- Lean on operator experience in vertical.
- Offer to stay useful if they go another direction ("I'm happy to be a sounding board even if you go with them").

## Messaging DON'Ts (landmines)

- Don't mock MBB or make jokes about "deck-making." Signals insecurity and alienates buyers who have MBB alumni on their team.
- Don't claim parity ("we do what they do, cheaper"). That's commodity framing. We do something *different*.
- Don't over-sell the operator-vs-consultant divide. Some MBB consultants genuinely have operating experience; the critique is about typical staffing, not about them personally.
- Don't trash-talk their research or data. It's real and valuable for certain uses.

## If you hear...

- **"We've worked with [MBB firm] before and they're great."** → *"That's helpful context — what worked well, and what did you wish was different? My practice is specifically designed around the 'wish was different' parts most mid-market clients raise."*
- **"The board wants us to use a recognized firm."** → *"Understood. Worth asking the board: recognized for what? If the signal is 'we're being rigorous,' that's one thing; if the signal is 'we need operational GTM to work,' that's a different question and a different answer."*
- **"They're offering us a fixed-fee engagement at [price]."** → *"That's a real offer. If price is comparable, the question is what you're getting for it — specifically, whose hours and for what deliverable. Let me compare scope-for-scope if you'll share theirs; I'm confident you'll see a meaningful difference in what senior time you're actually getting."*

## Escalation triggers

- Prospect signals they want MBB for board-legitimacy reasons → probably can't win; offer to be a sounding board instead.
- Prospect asks for a bake-off format (side-by-side proposal comparison) → respond selectively per Enns; decline pure bake-offs, propose a paid diagnostic as a better way to evaluate.

# Filled Example 2 — Solo fractional CROs / online marketplaces

```
---
# Battle Card: Solo Fractional CROs (via Pavilion, Chief Outsiders, Catalant, LinkedIn)
Last reviewed: 2026-04-20 | Owner: Joe
---
```

## Competitor overview

**Who they are:** individual fractional CROs or CMOs, often ex-VPs/SVPs from mid-market SaaS companies, now doing 2–4 concurrent engagements. Sourced through:
- **Pavilion** — the fractional executive marketplace within the GTM community.
- **Chief Outsiders** — fractional CMO placements, often for mid-market.
- **Catalant** — project-based marketplace, quality varies.
- **LinkedIn / referral networks** — direct sourcing of known operators.
- **Operators Guild / RevGenius / other communities** — peer-network sourcing.

**How they'd describe themselves:** "I've done this before; I'll come run your GTM function 2 days a week."

**How buyers describe them (honestly):** mixed. Great when the match is right; patchy when matched on firmographics instead of real fit.

**Pricing:** typically $8k–$20k/month depending on seniority and time commitment. Sometimes project-based ($25k–$75k projects).

**Typical engagement shape:** 2–3 days/week as fractional operator; 6-to-18-month relationships.

**Their strengths:**
- Genuine operating experience.
- Often cheaper than a full-time hire at the same seniority.
- Can execute, not just advise.
- Close peer-to-peer relationships with CEO.

**Their weaknesses:**
- Concurrent engagements mean limited attention per client.
- Single-point-of-failure: when the fractional is sick, vacationing, or burned out, client gets no service.
- No firm methodology — each engagement is a snowflake based on one person's experience.
- Knowledge transfer at end is often weak; client is back where they started when fractional leaves.
- Most have horizontal B2B SaaS experience; vertical depth (fintech, payments) is rarer.

## When we win against them

- Prospect needs architectural / diagnostic work, not interim operational leadership.
- Prospect needs repeatable methodology and documentation, not "a smart operator."
- Prospect has been through a fractional-CRO engagement that flamed out.
- Prospect needs vertical-specific (fintech, payments) depth.
- Prospect wants published IP and frameworks, not a person's personal playbook.
- Prospect is at a stage where architectural work is needed before operational leadership (Diagnostic + Program come before Fractional).

## When they win against us

- Prospect genuinely needs interim operational execution — managing reps day-to-day, running pipeline meetings, taking customer calls.
- Prospect wants 2+ days/week of presence, not a weekly advisory session.
- Prospect is happy with "smart operator's opinion" and doesn't need documented architecture.
- Prospect has found a fractional with exactly the right vertical match and track record.
- Prospect is cost-constrained and comparing monthly retainers 1:1 where the fractional's $10k/month for 2 days/week looks cheaper than our $12k/month Fractional Advisor.

## Our reframe

**They want the buyer to evaluate on:** operator seniority, hours committed, interim-leadership capacity.

**We want the buyer to evaluate on:** whether the problem is *operational* (run the team for a quarter) or *architectural* (design the motion that the team runs for years), and whether the engagement leaves behind systems or just time spent.

**The bridge:** the question isn't "who's a better operator?" The question is "what's the role you're filling?" If you need someone to run your team in place of a VP Sales, hire a fractional operator. If you need to *design* the GTM motion that a VP Sales would then run, that's different work — and it's what I do.

**Specific reframe language:**

*"Fractional CROs can be great operators. The question I'd ask is: what happens when they leave? If the value is 'someone runs our team for 6 months,' that's real but temporary. If the value is 'we built systems and documented playbooks that outlast any single person,' that's what my practice is designed to produce. Which do you need?"*

## Trap questions to ask the buyer

1. **"What do you want to exist in your business 6 months after the engagement ends?"** — exposes the system-vs-operator distinction.
2. **"How many other engagements does the fractional have concurrent with yours?"** — surfaces attention dilution.
3. **"What methodology do they use, and can you see a prior version of what they'll produce?"** — exposes the "snowflake-per-client" pattern.
4. **"What's their specific experience in [fintech/payments/your vertical]?"** — exposes horizontal-vs-vertical depth.
5. **"If they get sick for a month or take a new gig, what's your fallback?"** — exposes single-point-of-failure.

## Proof points

- Published IP — this knowledge base itself; frameworks and playbooks available to clients.
- Vertical depth — Blackhawk/Tango operating experience in incentives/payments; fintech engagement track record.
- Methodology — named engagements (Diagnostic, Program, Fractional Advisor) with documented deliverables, not one-off custom work.
- Continuity model — when we engage, deliverables and systems outlast the engagement.

## Messaging DO's

- Acknowledge fractional operators are real and valuable for real problems.
- Be specific about when we're the right fit and when we're not.
- Lean on the system-vs-operator distinction.
- Highlight vertical depth if the prospect is in fintech/payments/martech.

## Messaging DON'Ts

- Don't dismiss fractional CROs as unserious. Many are excellent; we want their referrals.
- Don't claim we're better operators — we may or may not be; that's not the point.
- Don't badmouth Pavilion or similar marketplaces. We participate in those networks; they refer prospects.
- Don't pretend we do what they do. If the prospect genuinely needs 2 days/week of operator presence, we're not the fit.

## If you hear...

- **"We're thinking about hiring a fractional CRO instead."** → *"Good instinct, depending on what you actually need. If you need someone running your team 2 days a week, that's the right shape. If you need to figure out what motion the team should be running in the first place, that's a different problem. Which one is it?"*
- **"A fractional is $10k/month. You're more expensive."** → *"That's true on monthly rate. Worth comparing on total value: a fractional gives you 6–8 days of a person per month; I give you a weekly strategic session plus published frameworks, systems we build together, and a structured 6-month arc. Different product, different economics. Happy to scope which fits best."*
- **"We tried a fractional CRO; it didn't work out."** → *"What specifically didn't work? Often the issue isn't the person — it's that the problem was architectural and they were trying to solve it operationally. That's a pattern I see often, and it shapes how I engage."*

## Escalation triggers

- Prospect genuinely needs interim operational leadership → refer to a fractional CRO in our network (value-positive for everyone), don't force-fit.
- Prospect is cost-shopping and sees us as interchangeable with a fractional operator → pricing conversation needed; if they can't see the difference, we may not have positioned clearly enough yet.

# Brief treatments — other competitor types

For competitors worth knowing about but not worth a full card yet. Write the full card the first time a deal is actively contested against one of these.

**Force Management / Winning by Design consulting:**
- Strength: rigorous, well-known methodologies, training-heavy.
- Weakness: generalist across verticals; methodologies can feel heavy for mid-market.
- Reframe: methodology vs. operator — they train your team on a framework; we implement architecture in your specific vertical context.
- When they win: large enterprise looking for training programs, not architectural consulting.

**MKT1 Collective / specialty fractional marketing firms:**
- Strength: excellent at B2B marketing specifically, community-based.
- Weakness: marketing-only; don't cover sales architecture or RevOps.
- Reframe: complementary, not competitive. If prospect needs only marketing work, refer. If GTM is the problem, we handle the sales/marketing/CS architecture as one system.
- When they win: prospect's problem is genuinely marketing-isolated.

**GTM Partners / Pavilion Advisors / mid-tier GTM firms:**
- Strength: recognized, community-adjacent, quality practitioners.
- Weakness: scale and process overhead; may not have vertical depth in fintech.
- Reframe: the specific practitioner matters more than the firm. Ask who's on your engagement and what their vertical experience is.
- When they win: prospect wants firm infrastructure (proposals, MSAs, team backup); we're solo by design.

**"Hire a full-time CRO / VP Sales" (the internal alternative):**
- Strength: permanence, 40 hours/week, culture-building.
- Weakness: 3–6 months to hire well; 6–12 months to ramp; expensive ($300k+ total comp).
- Reframe: this is the right answer for some situations; we're the right answer for others. Diagnostic can help decide which.
- When they win: prospect has budget, time, and clarity on role profile; our offer is bridge or advisory.

**"Do nothing / figure it out internally":**
- The most common competitor.
- Strength: zero vendor cost; doesn't add dependencies.
- Weakness: opportunity cost of doing nothing; internal team often lacks external perspective and specialist expertise.
- Reframe: doing nothing is a decision with quantified cost. Surface that cost during diagnosis.
- When they win: budget is genuinely unavailable; CEO doesn't believe in outside help; or problem is genuinely not important enough to address now. Each of these is a legitimate reason.

# Common landmines (across all competitive situations)

Behaviors that will damage *any* competitive deal regardless of the specific competitor:

- **Disparaging language.** "They're really just a bunch of..." / "I've heard bad things about..." / "They're way more expensive for what you get." Every one of these loses credibility.
- **Feature-list bake-offs.** "We have X, Y, Z; they don't have Z." The buyer will weight according to their priorities, not yours — and feature lists invite commodity comparison.
- **Price-cutting under competitive pressure.** Signals you were overpriced, erodes margin, teaches the buyer to negotiate. Hold price; defend on value.
- **Over-promising to beat a competitor.** Promising capabilities or outcomes you can't reliably deliver generates churn and reference damage later.
- **FOMO tactics.** "Other clients are taking this slot; you need to decide now." Cheap urgency that sophisticated buyers see through.
- **"We do everything they do, but better."** Commodity framing. We don't do the same thing; we do *different* work.
- **Getting defensive.** When a buyer says "your competitor has X," the reflex to justify makes us small. The reframe makes us considered.

# Update cadence

Every quarter:

1. Review each active battle card against what actually happened in deals.
2. Add new competitors that have shown up in 2+ deals.
3. Remove competitors that haven't shown up in 6+ months (archive, don't delete).
4. Update pricing ranges based on what buyers reported.
5. Refresh proof points with new reference stories.

Every time a competitor comes up in a deal:

- Log the encounter: which competitor, which deal, what the buyer said, what we said, what happened.
- After the deal (win or lose), update the relevant battle card with what we learned.

The battle cards get smarter with every deal. Without this feedback loop, they become stale.
