# Quarterly Business Review (QBR) Template

> A structured template for running a meaningful GTM QBR — not the ritualized PowerPoint theater that QBRs often become. Produces honest signal, surfaces what's working and what isn't, and generates a prioritized plan for the next quarter. Designed to be usable by a CRO presenting to a CEO or board, a CEO presenting to the board, or Cambrian Catalyst presenting to a client in a Program or Fractional engagement.

## What a QBR is (and isn't)

**A QBR is:** a disciplined, evidence-based review of the quarter that just ended, with honest analysis of what drove the numbers, clear patterns identified, and specific priorities for the next quarter. It exists to drive better decisions going forward, not to celebrate or apologize for the past.

**A QBR is not:** a status dashboard, a revenue recitation, a commercial for the sales team, or a rationalization of missed numbers. The most common QBR failure is that it reports outcomes without diagnosing causes, which means the next quarter's plan is untethered from the last quarter's reality.

**The test for a good QBR:** at the end, the audience can answer three questions specifically — *What happened this quarter and why? What did we learn? What are we doing differently next quarter?* If the QBR doesn't answer these, it's theater.

## How to use this template

**Adapt, don't blindly copy.** Every organization has its own rhythm, data availability, and audience. The structure below is a starting frame; remove sections irrelevant to your situation, expand sections that matter most.

**Prep owns the outcome.** A good QBR is 80% prep, 20% presentation. Budget 2 weeks of prep time for a real QBR. Anything less produces a report that reads well but doesn't surface real insight.

**One narrative, not ten dashboards.** The QBR should tell a coherent story about the quarter, supported by data. Reports that are just collections of charts lose the audience and bury the insight. Every chart should support a claim the presenter is making.

**Leadership sits in the meeting, not behind it.** The most valuable QBRs happen when the CEO, CRO, CFO, and VP Marketing are all in the room, engaged with the same data, and challenging each other's interpretations.

# Pre-QBR prep (2 weeks out)

Work backward from the QBR date. Two weeks is the minimum budget for a substantive review.

## T-minus 14 days: Data pull

Pull the underlying data. Owner: RevOps or dedicated analyst.

Required data sets:

- **Closed-won deals this quarter** — with ACV, close date, source, rep, stage duration at each step, Deal-Level Fit Scorecard at close, ICP-fit classification.
- **Closed-lost deals this quarter** — with reason, stage at loss, competitor (if any), source, rep, Deal-Level Fit Scorecard at entry.
- **Pipeline entering the quarter vs. exiting** — open opportunities, stage-by-stage movement, aging analysis.
- **Pipeline generation by source** — inbound, outbound, partner, referral, customer expansion.
- **Rep-level performance** — quota attainment, pipeline coverage, win rate, ramp metrics for new hires, scorecard trend from Discovery Call Scorecards.
- **ICP fit of closed-won vs. closed-lost vs. active pipeline** — using the ICP Scorecard from the core knowledge base.
- **Retention / expansion for existing customers** — churn, gross and net revenue retention, expansion by segment.

## T-minus 10 days: Analysis

Turn data into patterns. Owner: CRO + RevOps.

Questions to answer:

1. Did we hit the revenue number? If yes, what drove it? If no, what was the miss?
2. Did we generate the pipeline we needed? If no, where was the shortfall (source, segment, rep)?
3. Did our ICP fit improve, stay flat, or degrade? Are closed-won deals tier-1 ICP or tier-2?
4. Where is the motion working? Where is it not?
5. What patterns changed from last quarter?
6. Which reps are growing, plateauing, regressing?
7. Which offers are landing? Which are struggling?

## T-minus 7 days: Draft narrative

Write the story the QBR tells before making slides. Owner: CRO (or Joe if in Program/Fractional engagement).

- One paragraph: headline of the quarter.
- Three bullets: key findings.
- Three bullets: what we're doing about them.

If you can't write this cleanly, the analysis isn't done.

## T-minus 3 days: Build deck

Slides support the narrative, not the other way around. Owner: CRO + RevOps.

Every slide should:
- Have a one-sentence takeaway as the title (not "Q3 Pipeline" — try "Q3 pipeline grew 20%, but Enterprise-segment pipeline shrank 15%").
- Support one claim.
- Source data transparently (what slice, what period, what caveats).

## T-minus 1 day: Dry run

Present the deck to a smaller group (e.g., direct reports). Test the narrative. Where do people get confused? What questions come up? Refine.

# The QBR structure — slide by slide

A ~25-slide deck for a 60–90 minute QBR. Add or cut sections based on audience and time.

## Section 1 — Executive summary (1 slide)

**Format:** one slide, dense.

**Contents:**
- Revenue vs. target (number, % attainment).
- Pipeline health (coverage ratio, entering-exit pipeline change).
- One-line headline characterization of the quarter ("The quarter we proved SMB works and enterprise stalled").
- Three key findings, bulleted.
- Three priorities for next quarter, bulleted.

**Purpose:** if the audience reads only this slide, they know the story.

## Section 2 — Revenue & pipeline results (3–5 slides)

### Slide 2.1: Quarterly revenue vs. target
- Actual vs. target vs. forecast, by month.
- YoY comparison for context.
- Takeaway: did we hit, miss, or exceed, and by how much?

### Slide 2.2: Pipeline generation and movement
- Pipeline generated in quarter by source (inbound / outbound / partner / referral / expansion).
- Stage-by-stage progression: how much moved, how much stalled, how much regressed.
- Aging analysis: how long are deals sitting in each stage vs. expected cycle time?

### Slide 2.3: Win rate and cycle time
- Win rate this quarter vs. last 2 quarters (control for deal mix).
- Average sales cycle by segment or offer.
- Takeaway: is the motion getting faster or slower? Winning or losing more?

### Slide 2.4: Closed-won analysis
- Deals won: count, total ACV, ICP-fit distribution (tier-1 vs. tier-2 vs. tier-3).
- Top 3 wins: quick narrative on what made them close.
- Source analysis: where did winning deals come from?

### Slide 2.5: Closed-lost analysis
- Deals lost: count, total pipeline value lost, primary reasons.
- Loss-to-competitor vs. loss-to-no-decision (JOLT lens — indecision-driven losses).
- Patterns: are we losing on the same criteria repeatedly?

## Section 3 — ICP fit analysis (2–3 slides)

This is often the most insight-rich section and the most skipped. It's also where a Cambrian Catalyst engagement adds distinct value.

### Slide 3.1: ICP fit distribution across the funnel
- Breakdown of pipeline, opportunities, closed-won, closed-lost by ICP tier (1 / 2 / 3 / out-of-ICP).
- Win rate by ICP tier — the ultimate test of ICP rigor.

### Slide 3.2: ICP drift
- Compare ICP distribution this quarter vs. last quarter vs. 4 quarters ago.
- Is the pipeline shifting toward or away from stated ICP?
- Takeaway: are we staying disciplined or drifting toward easier-to-sell but worse-fit accounts?

### Slide 3.3: Retention / expansion by ICP tier
- Gross and net retention by ICP tier, where data allows.
- Expansion rate by ICP tier.
- Takeaway: are tier-1 ICP customers also tier-1 in long-term value? (If not, the ICP definition is wrong.)

## Section 4 — Rep performance (2–3 slides)

Handle with care. QBRs aren't individual-performance reviews, but rep-level patterns are real and worth surfacing.

### Slide 4.1: Quota attainment distribution
- How many reps hit quota, missed, or exceeded?
- Concentration of revenue: is the quarter carried by 1–2 reps, or distributed?

### Slide 4.2: Scorecard trend
- Average Discovery Call Scorecard score across reps, this quarter vs. trend.
- Top 3 dimensions improving / degrading.
- Ramp progress for new reps against plan.

### Slide 4.3: Coaching priorities for next quarter
- Which skills are we doubling down on?
- Who needs specific intervention, and what's the plan?
- Note: anonymize individual reps if audience is external; name them internally.

## Section 5 — Offer mix analysis (1–2 slides)

### Slide 5.1: Performance by offer
- Revenue by offer / product / package.
- Win rate by offer.
- Average deal size by offer.
- Time-to-close by offer.

### Slide 5.2: Offer-fit signals
- For each offer: is it landing well (high win rate, good fit, happy customers) or struggling (low win rate, wrong fit, high churn)?
- Any offer candidates for pricing adjustment, repositioning, retirement, or expansion?

## Section 6 — Win/loss insight (2 slides)

Pull patterns from the deal-level data into insights.

### Slide 6.1: Why we won (patterns across wins)
- What do winning deals have in common?
- Which reps, sources, offers, ICP tiers, trigger events show up disproportionately?

### Slide 6.2: Why we lost (patterns across losses)
- Breakdown of loss reasons: lost to competitor, lost to no-decision, disqualified late, budget pulled, other.
- JOLT lens: what % of losses were indecision (indicating coaching opportunity) vs. genuine competitive loss (indicating positioning / product opportunity)?
- Top 3 patterns to address.

## Section 7 — Customer signals (1–2 slides)

For client engagements or sales orgs with CS data.

### Slide 7.1: Customer health
- Retention trend.
- Expansion rate.
- NPS / CES / customer health score direction.
- Top 3 at-risk accounts and mitigation.

### Slide 7.2: Voice of customer themes
- What are customers saying about the product / service?
- Sales-relevant themes — pain points they mention, reasons they bought, reasons they'd leave.
- Connection to next-quarter priorities.

## Section 8 — Critical insights (1 slide)

The 3–5 most important things we learned this quarter. Not every data point — the ones that change how we operate.

Each insight stated as:
- **What we learned.**
- **Evidence.**
- **What it changes.**

Example: "We learned that our mid-market segment win rate is 2x our enterprise win rate at half the cycle time. Evidence: 28 deals closed, segmentation table attached. What it changes: we're re-tiering our ICP to prioritize mid-market explicitly in Q4 outbound and content."

## Section 9 — Next quarter priorities (2–3 slides)

### Slide 9.1: Priorities for the quarter ahead
- 3–5 priorities, in order.
- For each: what it is, why it matters, who owns it, what changes operationally.
- Each priority should be traceable to an insight in Section 8.

### Slide 9.2: Targets for next quarter
- Revenue target, pipeline target, key metrics.
- What's different from this quarter's plan? Why?

### Slide 9.3: Resource asks
- What the team needs from leadership / the board to hit the plan.
- Specific asks: headcount, budget, tooling, executive engagement, customer-reference time, etc.

## Section 10 — Discussion (open time)

The QBR should end in discussion, not presentation. Reserve 20–30 minutes.

**Good questions to seed discussion:**
- Where do you disagree with our analysis?
- What's a risk we're not addressing?
- What would you prioritize differently?
- What context do you have that we're missing?

# Facilitation guide

## Before the meeting

- **Pre-read required.** Send the deck 48 hours in advance. Attendees who haven't read shouldn't participate in discussion — they're catching up, not thinking.
- **Set the frame.** In the calendar invite, name the purpose: "The goal of this QBR is to understand what happened in Q[n], align on what it means, and commit to Q[n+1] priorities. It is not a status update."
- **Clear the calendar.** QBRs need 60–90 minutes minimum; shorter QBRs produce shallow QBRs. Block additional time for follow-up.

## During the meeting

- **Don't read slides.** Anyone who attended the pre-read already saw them. Use slides as evidence; spend the spoken time on insight and discussion.
- **Watch the time.** 45 minutes presentation, 15–30 minutes discussion, 5 minutes commitments. Cut mid-deck if needed; it's better to get to commitments than finish slides.
- **Capture commitments.** Every priority needs an owner, a date, a success criterion. Write them down in real time in a commitments document. Send within 24 hours.
- **Be honest about what you don't know.** Questions you can't answer honestly ("we don't have data on that yet") are better than answers you're making up.
- **Invite disagreement.** If the CEO, CFO, or CRO nods and approves without friction, either the analysis was bulletproof or — more commonly — no one is engaging. Press for disagreement.

## After the meeting

- **Send the commitments document within 24 hours.** Every commitment with owner, date, success criterion.
- **Schedule follow-up check-ins.** Every commitment gets a check-in cadence. Weekly for this-quarter items; monthly for longer-horizon items.
- **Update the next-quarter dashboard.** The priorities named in the QBR become the dashboard for the following quarter. Track progress visibly.
- **Distribute the final deck.** To attendees plus stakeholders who weren't in the room (board, investors, extended team). With executive summary at the top.

# Variations by company stage

The template above is calibrated for a Series A–C company with existing pipeline and defined ICP. Adjust for other stages:

## Very early stage (pre-Series A, <$2M ARR)

- Cut: rep performance section (often 1–2 sellers, not a team), offer mix (usually one offer), detailed ICP analysis (ICP is still being defined).
- Expand: customer signals (qualitative insights from the few customers you have carry more weight), learning/pivots (what did we learn about the market and the product this quarter?), resource asks (what will change in the next quarter to get closer to repeatability?).
- Cadence: consider moving from quarterly to monthly — the pace of learning is too fast for 90-day intervals.

## Mid-scale (Series B/C, $10M–$50M ARR)

- The template above is calibrated for this stage. Use as-is.
- Pay particular attention to ICP drift (are we losing discipline as we scale?) and offer mix (are new offers landing or cannibalizing?).

## Scale-up (Series D+, $50M+ ARR)

- Add: segment breakdowns (enterprise, mid-market, SMB run as separate sub-reviews), geographic analysis, channel/partner breakdown, vertical analysis.
- Consider separate QBRs per motion (direct sales, PLG, partner) reconciled at a higher-level meta-QBR.
- Board-level QBRs become annual or semi-annual; quarterly reviews move to operating-committee level.

## PE-backed

- Add: explicit tracking against investment thesis metrics (the PE firm's deal model).
- Add: value-creation plan (VCP) tracking — the specific initiatives the PE firm identified at acquisition.
- Consider: reverse-time-order — start with what the PE firm will ask, then work backward to evidence.

## Cambrian Catalyst in a Program or Fractional engagement

When Joe presents a QBR as part of a client engagement:

- **Framing:** "This is the QBR I'd want to see if I were on your board. It's not an activity report; it's an honest diagnosis and plan."
- **Add:** engagement-specific section (what Cambrian Catalyst did this quarter, what we learned, what we'd do differently).
- **Add:** recommendations section (3–5 specific things we'd change going forward, with rationale).
- **Add:** "what we're watching" — early signals that could become next quarter's story, positive or negative.
- **Position:** the QBR is a demonstration of our work — it's evidence for Fractional renewal conversations or case-study material with permission.

# Common QBR failure modes

Watch for these. Several of them are universal failure patterns across organizations.

**Report mode, not review mode.** Deck reads like a status update — things happened, here's what they were. No insight, no diagnosis, no causation. Remedy: force a "why" on every major finding. If you can't answer why, it's not a finding.

**Vanity metric narration.** Celebrating metrics that move without connecting them to business outcomes. "Pipeline grew 30%!" Great — is that the pipeline we wanted (ICP-fit, likely-to-close)? Remedy: every metric gets a quality adjective.

**Excuse-heavy loss analysis.** "We lost because [reason outside our control]." Occasionally true; usually a dodge. Real loss analysis owns what we could have done differently. Remedy: for every loss reason, ask "what changes in our process would reduce this?"

**Plan-heavy, learning-light.** The deck spends 80% on next quarter and 20% on last quarter. Plans are easier than honest diagnosis. Remedy: inverse the ratio — spend the majority of the QBR on what happened and what we learned.

**All-reps-are-above-average syndrome.** Rep performance section carefully avoids surfacing who's underperforming. Result: no coaching happens. Remedy: distribution charts, not averages. Names where appropriate; anonymized patterns where sensitive.

**One-way presentation.** CRO presents; leadership nods; meeting ends. No challenge, no disagreement, no insight. Remedy: explicitly invite dissent; schedule pre-reads; structure discussion time with prompts.

**Commitment-free.** The meeting ends with "great discussion" and no specific ownership, dates, or success criteria. Next quarter, nothing has changed because nothing was committed. Remedy: commitments document, same-day distribution, check-in cadence.

**ICP drift unexamined.** Pipeline is evaluated without reference to fit quality. The sales team hits quota by selling to bad-fit customers; CS lives with the consequences next year. Remedy: Section 3 of this template, every quarter, without exception.

**Data without story.** Fifteen charts in a row with no narrative arc. Attendees remember nothing; decisions don't get made. Remedy: every chart supports a claim; the QBR tells one story, not a collection of facts.

**Story without data.** The opposite: compelling narrative unsupported by evidence. Tempting during tough quarters. Remedy: every claim needs a supporting data slice. "We improved in Q3" is weak; "Win rate in mid-market grew from 18% to 26% in Q3 with N=43 deals" is strong.

# Appendix — Templates & checklists

## QBR pre-meeting checklist (CRO or presenter)

- [ ] Data pulls completed and verified (T-minus 14 days).
- [ ] Analysis completed; insights documented (T-minus 10 days).
- [ ] Narrative drafted in plain English; dry-run partner identified (T-minus 7 days).
- [ ] Deck built; every slide has a takeaway title (T-minus 3 days).
- [ ] Dry run completed; feedback incorporated (T-minus 1 day).
- [ ] Pre-read sent 48 hours in advance.
- [ ] Discussion questions prepared.
- [ ] Commitments template ready.

## QBR follow-up checklist

- [ ] Commitments document distributed within 24 hours.
- [ ] Calendar invites created for every commitment check-in.
- [ ] Final deck distributed to full stakeholder list.
- [ ] Actions assigned in project management tool.
- [ ] Next-quarter dashboard updated with new priorities.
- [ ] Date set for next QBR (90 days out).
