# Junior-Rep Onboarding Playbook

> A 90-day operational plan for getting a new B2B seller from day one to certified-independent. Assumes familiarity with the ICP knowledge base and the offer-fit mapping file; references them throughout. Designed to be executed by a manager (or founder acting as manager) with ~4 hours/week of direct coaching time.

## How to use this playbook

**The manager runs the ramp, not the rep.** Onboarding is a managerial responsibility. Reps who "sink or swim" sink more often than they swim, churn early, and when they stay they develop the wrong habits. Budget real manager time: 1:1s, ride-alongs, call reviews, role-plays. The return is a productive rep in 90 days instead of 9 months.

**The plan is scaffolded, not linear.** Skills build on each other. A rep who hasn't mastered diagnosis (weeks 3–4) cannot do qualification (weeks 7–8) well — they'll fill in MEDDPICC letters without the underlying discovery to support them. Don't advance a rep past a week's readiness signal just because the calendar says so.

**Adapt to the deal type.** This playbook is calibrated for mid-market B2B sales (~$15k–$100k ACV, multi-stakeholder, 30–120 day cycles). For SMB transactional sales, compress to 45 days. For true enterprise ($100k+ ACV, 6+ month cycles), extend to 120 days and add specific enterprise modules (procurement navigation, security reviews, multi-thread management).

**The certification is a real gate.** A rep who fails the 90-day certification does not run deals independently. Running deals before they're ready damages pipeline quality and buyer experience, and it's harder to retrain than to train. Extend the ramp, don't rush the certification.

## Before Day 1 — Pre-work

The week before the rep starts, the manager sets up the environment so the first day isn't wasted on logistics.

**Systems and access:**
- CRM access provisioned, dashboards customized, sample records available.
- Email, calendar, communication tools (Slack, etc.).
- Call recording tool configured (Gong, Chorus, Fathom, or similar).
- Access to sales enablement library (Solution-Fit Cards, case studies, decks).
- Sales engagement tool access (Outreach, Salesloft, Apollo, etc.) if applicable.

**Calendar scaffolding:**
- Weekly 1:1 recurring on the calendar (60 min minimum, weekly).
- First week: daily 30-min check-ins with manager.
- 5 discovery call shadow sessions with senior reps scheduled in weeks 1–2.
- Role-play sessions scheduled weeks 3, 5, 7, 9.
- Call review sessions scheduled weekly.

**Pre-reads sent to the rep (optional but high-leverage):**
- The ICP knowledge base core doc.
- Solution-Fit Cards for all offers.
- The offer-fit mapping file (Parts 1 and 2 at minimum).
- 3 recent won-deal recordings with context.
- 2 recent lost-deal recordings with context.

**Day 1 goal:** rep leaves day 1 knowing who the company sells to, what the offers are, and who's on the team. They do not touch pipeline.

| Phase | Weeks | Focus | Certification signal |
|---|---|---|---|
| Foundations | 1–2 | ICP, offers, who we sell to and why | Can explain the company's ICP and offers in own words without reading from notes |
| Diagnosis | 3–4 | Gap Selling, SPIN, PIC completion | Can complete a PIC for any customer story, in any context |
| Supervised discovery | 5–6 | Live discovery calls with manager observing | Runs a full discovery call with < 2 corrections from manager |
| Qualification | 7–8 | MEDDPICC discipline, deal scoring | Can score 3 live deals honestly, identify gaps, propose interventions |

| Independence & certification | 11–12 | Certification test, first independent deals | Passes certification; runs 2 deals independently under review |

# Weeks 1–2: Foundations

**Goal:** the rep understands *who we sell to, what we sell, and why we win* well enough to describe it back in their own words.

## Learning objectives (weeks 1–2)

1. Name each ICP archetype the company sells to, with firmographic criteria and trigger events.
2. Explain each off-the-shelf offer: what it is, who it's for, who it's not for, what it costs, what the buyer gets.
3. Map the company's offers to the adjacent competitive landscape — alternatives, partners, referral destinations.
4. Name the 3 most important things the company's best customers have in common.

## Reading

| Priority | Item | Est. time |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Company's ICP core doc (`icp-fit-knowledge-base.md` Part 1 + relevant vertical playbooks) | 4 hrs |
| 1 | All Solution-Fit Cards | 2 hrs |
| 1 | 3 case studies (won) + 2 lost-deal summaries | 2 hrs |
| 2 | *Gap Selling* — Keenan (full) | 5 hrs |
| 2 | Offer-fit mapping file — Part 1 (Diagnostic-First Selling) | 2 hrs |
| 3 | Company website, pricing page, product tour | 2 hrs |

Total reading time: ~17 hours over 2 weeks. Reps should not be selling during this; reading and shadowing are the work.

## Activities

- **Daily 30-min manager check-ins** for first week, 3x/week in week 2.
- **Shadow 5 discovery calls** with senior reps (live or recorded). Rep writes observations using PIC template after each.
- **Shadow 2 pipeline reviews** to see how deals are discussed.
- **Meet each team member** (AE peers, SE, CS, marketing) for 30 min each. Learn roles and handoffs.
- **Interview 2 current customers** (warm, facilitated by CS) — not sales calls, just "tell me why you bought and how it's going."

## Deliverables (end of week 2)

1. **Two-page ICP summary** in the rep's own words — not copied from the doc.
2. **Offer summaries** — one paragraph per offer, their own words.
3. **Observation report** — patterns noticed across shadowed calls and customer interviews.
4. **10 questions** — things the rep noticed, doesn't understand, or wants to challenge.

## 1:1 focus (weeks 1–2)

- Week 1: orientation, people, culture, expectations.
- Week 2: the rep's initial mental model of the business. Test their understanding by asking them to explain things back. Correct misconceptions.
- Standard weekly agenda (starting week 2): 1. Wins/frictions this week 2. Pipeline or study review 3. Skill development 4. Manager asks/helps.

## Readiness signal to advance

- Rep can explain any offer to a stranger (manager tests with a cold-call style question: "Tell me about [offer]. Who's it for?").
- Rep has a working mental model of why customers buy and which customers succeed.
- Deliverables completed and reviewed.

**If not ready:** extend by one week. Do not proceed to live customer conversations without this foundation.

# Weeks 3–4: Diagnosis

**Goal:** the rep can diagnose a prospect's situation — Current State, Future State, Gap, business impact, root cause — without pitching.

## Learning objectives (weeks 3–4)

1. Complete a Problem Identification Chart (PIC) for any customer story.
2. Run through SPIN's four question types in sequence without reading from a script.
3. Recognize when a prospect has articulated implied vs. explicit needs.
4. Identify the Four Forces (Push/Pull/Anxiety/Habit) in a switching story.
5. Understand when to disqualify a prospect who has no gap (no Future State or no business impact).

## Reading

| Priority | Item | Est. time |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | *SPIN Selling* — Rackham (chapters 1–4, 7) | 3 hrs |
| 1 | Offer-fit mapping file — Part 1 complete | 2 hrs |
| 1 | ICP knowledge base — Applied Artifacts (Part 4) | 1 hr |
| 2 | *The Mom Test* — Fitzpatrick (short, read all) | 2 hrs |
| 2 | *Demand-Side Sales 101* — Moesta & Engle | 4 hrs |
| 3 | Re-read Solution-Fit Cards with diagnostic lens | 1 hr |

## Activities

- **Role-play 1 — "Cold Discovery"** (see Role-Play Scenarios below). Run twice, with debrief.
- **PIC exercise:** rep completes 5 PICs for real won deals (using recordings or notes). Manager reviews each, flags missing root-cause work.
- **Question tagging:** rep listens to 3 of their peers' discovery calls, tags each question as S/P/I/N, and calculates the ratio. Most junior reps over-index on Situation.
- **Customer interviews:** rep conducts 2 JTBD-style timeline interviews with current customers, facilitated by CS. Not sales calls — research. They produce a written switching-story analysis for each.
- **Shadow 3 more live discovery calls** — now watching specifically for question hierarchy and PIC-ability.

## Deliverables (end of week 4)

1. **5 completed PICs** on real customer stories.
2. **2 JTBD switching-story analyses** from customer interviews.
3. **Question-tag analysis** on peer calls with observations.
4. **Self-assessment:** which of the Four Forces does the rep find hardest to surface, and why?

## 1:1 focus (weeks 3–4)

- Weekly review of PICs — manager pushes on root cause and quantified impact.
- Role-play debrief: what did they do well, what did they miss, what should they do differently next time.
- "Show me one question you would ask differently." Focus on incremental improvement.

## Readiness signal to advance

- Rep completes a PIC in real-time during a role-play without prompting.
- Question mix in role-play: Situation ≤ 20%, Problem 30–40%, Implication 30–40%, Need-payoff 10–20%.
- Rep can name the dominant Force in a switching story they hear.
- Rep has demonstrated willingness to disqualify (in role-play or analysis) when no gap exists.

**If not ready:** extend by a week. Continue role-plays and PIC exercises. Diagnosis is the most foundational skill; don't rush past it.

# Weeks 5–6: Supervised discovery

**Goal:** the rep runs real discovery calls with the manager observing, and improves measurably call to call.

## Learning objectives (weeks 5–6)

1. Run a full discovery call (45–60 min) from introduction through next-steps.
2. Complete a usable PIC and preliminary MEDDPICC read after each call.
3. Handle pivots: unexpected stakeholder, pricing question early, objection, silence.
4. Manage time within the call: diagnosis in first 30 min, solution framing in middle, next steps in last 10.

## Reading

| Priority | Item | Est. time |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Offer-fit mapping file — Part 2 (Qualification) | 2 hrs |
| 1 | Offer-fit mapping file — Part 3 (Solution-to-Prospect Mapping) | 2 hrs |
| 2 | *MEDDICC* — Andy Whyte | 4 hrs |
| 2 | *The Qualified Sales Leader* — John McMahon (selected chapters) | 3 hrs |
| 3 | Review 2 strong discovery recordings from senior reps, with written analysis | 2 hrs |

## Activities

- **Live discovery calls**, manager observing (Zoom shadow or in-room):
  - Week 5: 2 calls, manager provides hot-tips via chat during call.
  - Week 6: 3 calls, manager silent during call, full debrief after.
- **Role-play 2 — "The Enthusiastic Talker"** (see below). Test champion/coach distinction.
- **Role-play 3 — "The Pricing-Early Prospect"** — prospect asks pricing in first 5 minutes. Rep handles without evading or blurting.
- **Post-call routine:** after each call, rep fills out the Discovery Call Scorecard (see companion file) as a self-assessment within 30 min of the call ending. Manager reviews before 1:1.

## Deliverables (end of week 6)

1. **5 recorded discovery calls** the rep ran.
2. **5 completed scorecards** (self-assessment) with honest gaps identified.
3. **5 completed PICs** from real calls.
4. **5 post-call summaries** including MEDDPICC letters filled and next-step plan.

## 1:1 focus (weeks 5–6)

- Call reviews dominate the 1:1. Watch 15–20 min of a call together; discuss specific moments.
- Pattern recognition: what's consistent across their calls? Where do they improve across calls vs. plateau?
- Discuss *missed questions* — the ones they should have asked but didn't.

## Readiness signal to advance

- Rep runs a full discovery call without the manager intervening.
- PIC and MEDDPICC preliminary read are completable from the call content.
- Next steps scheduled for every call (no open-ended "let's stay in touch" endings).
- Rep has begun to disqualify at least one live prospect where no gap existed.

# Weeks 7–8: Qualification discipline

**Goal:** the rep applies MEDDPICC rigorously to real deals and can honestly assess deal health.

## Learning objectives (weeks 7–8)

1. Complete MEDDPICC for every active opportunity.
2. Identify weak letters and propose specific interventions for each.
3. Distinguish a coach from a champion using the three-test criteria (power, active selling, personal win).
4. Name the Critical Event for every deal — or flag that it's missing.
5. Run a Champion Enablement conversation (Force Management style).

## Reading

| Priority | Item | Est. time |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | *MEDDICC* — Whyte (full, re-read with active deal lens) | 3 hrs |
| 1 | Offer-fit mapping file — Part 5 (Customer Experience) | 2 hrs |
| 2 | *The Challenger Customer* — Adamson et al. | 5 hrs |
| 2 | *The JOLT Effect* — Dixon & McKenna | 4 hrs |
| 3 | Offer-fit mapping file — Part 6 (Operational System), Deal-Level Fit Scorecard | 1 hr |

## Activities

- **MEDDPICC audit:** rep fills MEDDPICC for every deal they're on, regardless of stage. Present to manager; defend each letter.
- **Deal-level scoring:** score all active deals using the Deal-Level Fit Scorecard from the offer-fit mapping file. Identify bottom quartile for disqualification review.
- **Role-play 4 — "The Coach Pretending to be a Champion"** — test the three-test criteria.
- **Role-play 5 — "The Stalled Deal — JOLT Diagnostic"** — diagnose indecision source (valuation / info / outcome) and apply JOLT.
- **Champion Enablement session:** with manager, rep prepares a Champion Enablement kit for one live deal (one-pager + business case template + reference contacts + talking points).
- **Disqualification practice:** rep identifies 2 deals that should be disqualified and drafts the honest-exit message. Reviewed and sent with manager approval.

## Deliverables (end of week 8)

1. **Completed MEDDPICC** for all active deals.
2. **Deal-Level Fit Scorecards** for all active deals.
3. **1 Champion Enablement kit** produced and deployed.
4. **2 honest disqualification messages** sent to non-fit prospects.
5. **JOLT analysis** for 2 stalled deals: what's the indecision source, what's the intervention?

## 1:1 focus (weeks 7–8)

- Pipeline review is now rep-led. Rep walks manager through MEDDPICC for each deal; manager tests.
- Discuss: which letters are you strongest on? Weakest on? Why?
- Celebrate disqualifications. Make it visible that "killing a deal" is a win when the deal shouldn't have been in pipeline.

## Readiness signal to advance

- Rep can recite MEDDPICC letters for any active deal without reading from notes.
- Rep identifies at least one deal they've been kidding themselves about and owns disqualifying it.
- Critical Event is named for every active deal — or deal is reclassified as pre-pipeline nurture.
- Rep has successfully enabled a champion (measured by: champion sent your materials internally without being asked).

# Weeks 9–10: Full deal management

**Goal:** the rep runs a full deal cycle — discovery through proposal and close — with manager light-touch oversight.

## Learning objectives (weeks 9–10)

1. Multi-thread a deal: bring in additional stakeholders beyond the champion.
2. Run a demo that responds to discovered pain, not a feature tour.
3. Produce a proposal with metrics-based value narrative.
4. Handle common objections with Rackham's principle (prevent via discovery, address via reframe, not script).
5. Drive to close with JOLT discipline (be directive, narrow options, de-risk).

## Reading

| Priority | Item | Est. time |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Offer-fit mapping file — Part 4 (Offer Design) | 2 hrs |
| 1 | *Mastering Technical Sales* — Care & Bohlig (selected chapters, if applicable to your offer type) | 3 hrs |
| 2 | *The Effortless Experience* — Dixon et al. | 4 hrs |
| 2 | *The New Solution Selling* — Eades (selected chapters) | 3 hrs |
| 3 | Company's proposal templates and pricing calculators | 2 hrs |

## Activities

- **Run 2 full deal cycles** with manager light-touch:
  - Discovery → qualification → demo (if applicable) → proposal → close.
  - Manager observes key moments: first discovery, demo, proposal presentation.
- **Role-play 6 — "Economic Buyer Meeting"** — champion wants to bring you to the CFO/CEO.
- **Role-play 7 — "Competitive Bake-off"** — prospect names a specific competitor, wants differentiation.
- **Demo editing exercise:** rep sits with SE (if applicable), takes the standard demo, edits it to 20 minutes responding to a specific prospect's stated pain.
- **Proposal walkthrough:** rep produces a proposal with the Value Framework structure (business issue → required capabilities → differentiated capabilities → PBOs → metrics + proof). Manager reviews.

## Deliverables (end of week 10)

1. **2 full-cycle deals** advanced from discovery to decision.
2. **1 demo recorded**, reviewed with manager/SE.
3. **1 proposal document** produced using Value Framework structure.
4. **Win/loss analysis** for each deal (regardless of outcome) — what worked, what didn't, what to do differently next time.

## 1:1 focus (weeks 9–10)

- Deep dive on one deal per week. Walk through the entire sequence end-to-end.
- Discuss multi-threading: who else is involved, who's missing, how do we bring them in?
- Address confidence dips: new reps often hit a slump in weeks 9–10 as deal complexity becomes real.

## Readiness signal to advance

- Rep has driven at least one deal to a decision (win or loss).
- Rep's proposals pass the Value Framework test: business-issue-framed, metrics-backed, proof-supported.
- Rep handles a live objection without defaulting to a scripted response.
- Rep has multi-threaded at least one deal (brought in a stakeholder beyond initial champion).

# Weeks 11–12: Independence & certification

**Goal:** the rep passes certification and begins running deals independently under monthly review rather than weekly coaching.

## Learning objectives (weeks 11–12)

1. Articulate the full offer-fit system back to the manager coherently.
2. Diagnose, qualify, and close without real-time coaching.
3. Make judgment calls — when to disqualify, when to push, when to pause.
4. Self-coach: identify their own weaknesses and have a plan to address them.

## Reading

| Priority | Item | Est. time |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Re-read the offer-fit mapping file (full) with a new lens | 3 hrs |
| 1 | Review all their own call recordings from weeks 5–10 for patterns | 3 hrs |
| 2 | Choose one book they haven't read yet from the reading list; read before certification | 4 hrs |

## Activities

- **Certification prep (week 11):**
  - Manager and rep agree on which deal will be the certification deal.
  - Rep prepares: full discovery, MEDDPICC, Deal-Level Fit Scorecard, proposal draft.
- **Certification test (week 12):**
  - **Part A — Written:** rep produces a 1-page analysis of a deal of their choice, covering diagnosis (PIC), qualification (MEDDPICC), fit (Solution-Fit Card reference), and recommendation.
  - **Part B — Live:** rep runs a discovery call (real or role-play) while manager observes. Scored using the Discovery Call Scorecard.
  - **Part C — Oral:** 60-minute discussion with manager. Rep defends decisions on 3 active deals. Manager challenges.
  - **Pass criteria:** see Certification Test section below.
- **Transition planning:** manager and rep agree on monthly review cadence, specific development areas for months 4–6, and independence boundaries (which deals they run solo, which still need manager touch).

## Deliverables (end of week 12)

1. **Certification pack:** written analysis, recorded discovery call, scorecard results, oral-exam notes.
2. **Self-assessment:** "three things I do well, three things I still need to work on, plan for each."
3. **Month 4–6 development plan** co-authored with manager.

## 1:1 focus (weeks 11–12)

- Reduce from weekly to bi-weekly 1:1 at end of week 12 (only if certified).
- Week 11 1:1: prep for certification. What's the rep worried about?
- Week 12 1:1: debrief certification. What did the test reveal?

## Readiness signal to advance

- Certification passed.
- Rep self-identifies as ready for independent work (not just manager's judgment).
- Active pipeline looks healthy under MEDDPICC and Deal-Level Fit Scorecard review.

# Role-Play Scenarios

Six scenarios, each mapped to a capability, each timed for 30 minutes (20 min role-play + 10 min debrief). Run each at least once; rerun any the rep struggles with.

Before each role-play: manager sends the "prospect briefing" privately to whoever is playing the prospect (a peer, the manager, or a senior rep). The briefing describes the prospect's context, pain, and posture. The rep gets only the scenario title and the meeting pretext.

---

## Role-Play 1 — "Cold Discovery"

**Tests:** diagnostic question hierarchy (SPIN), PIC completion, Four Forces identification.
**Timing:** Week 3–4.
**Pretext given to rep:** inbound demo request from a qualified lead. 45-minute first call.

**Prospect briefing (private):**
- Company: Series B B2B SaaS, 85 employees, $6M ARR, in fintech-adjacent category.
- You are the VP Marketing, 11 months into the role.
- Pain: pipeline is 40% below target for the quarter. Board meeting in 6 weeks.
- First thought: 4 months ago. Realized current demand-gen approach wasn't working after a weak Q1.
- Passive looking: started reading content, attended a webinar.
- Active looking: evaluating 3 vendors. Yours is #2 in their mind.
- Anxiety: worried about disruption to team during a critical period. Previous vendor implementation went badly.
- You will NOT volunteer information. Respond honestly to good questions; give vague answers to poor ones.

**Success criteria:**
- Rep completes a PIC from the call (technical problem, business problem, impact, root cause).
- Rep names the Critical Event (board meeting in 6 weeks).
- Rep surfaces at least one Anxiety unprompted.
- Rep's question mix: ≤20% Situation, ≥30% Implication + Need-payoff.
- Rep ends with a specific next step (date, attendee, agenda).

**Common failures:**
- Over-asking Situation questions (rep spends 20 min gathering facts they could have Googled).
- Pitching the product in response to the first problem statement.
- Missing the Anxiety and focusing only on Pull.
- Ending with "let's stay in touch" instead of a concrete next step.

**Debrief questions:**
- What's your PIC? (If incomplete, that's the main finding.)
- Which Force was dominant? What signals did you pick up?
- Where did you over-talk? Where could you have probed deeper?

---

## Role-Play 2 — "The Enthusiastic Talker"

**Tests:** coach vs. champion distinction (MEDDPICC three-test criteria).
**Timing:** Week 5–6.
**Pretext given to rep:** follow-up call with your main contact at an active opportunity. You've met twice; they love the product.

**Prospect briefing (private):**
- Company: mid-market SaaS, $30M ARR.
- You are a Director of Operations. Enthusiastic about the product. Call yourself "the champion."
- But: you don't have budget authority (CFO does). You haven't sent any internal emails advocating for the purchase. You've never told your CFO about this evaluation.
- You will claim you're driving it forward and make vague promises about "looping in" leadership.
- If the rep asks specific, testing questions (e.g., "Have you sent the business case to your CFO yet?" or "Would you send an internal email introducing me to your CFO today?"), you deflect politely.
- You say things like "I need to time this right" and "Let me build the case more before I bring it up."

**Success criteria:**
- Rep administers at least 2 of the 3 champion tests (power, active selling, personal win).
- Rep concludes — out loud, in the debrief if not the call — that this is a coach, not a champion.
- Rep proposes a specific intervention: either upgrade the coach to champion via enablement, or find a different champion, or pause the deal.

**Common failures:**
- Rep takes "I'll drive this forward" at face value.
- Rep doesn't test the 'personal win' dimension at all.
- Rep leaves call confident the deal will close because the contact seemed enthusiastic.

**Debrief questions:**
- Based on the three-test criteria, is this a champion? Why or why not?
- What's the specific intervention this deal needs?
- Have you had real deals that looked like this? Which ones?

---

## Role-Play 3 — "The Pricing-Early Prospect"

**Tests:** holding diagnostic posture when prospect tries to short-circuit into pricing; honest pricing conversation per Enns.
**Timing:** Week 5–6.
**Pretext given to rep:** first discovery call with qualified inbound lead.

**Prospect briefing (private):**
- You are a founder/CEO, technical background, impatient.
- First question, 5 minutes in: "How much does this cost?"
- Second attempt, 15 minutes in: "I need a ballpark number. I don't have time for a long process."
- You're testing whether the rep will (a) blurt a number and lose leverage, (b) evade and annoy you, or (c) give a reasoned range while keeping the conversation productive.
- You have a real budget and real need but will disqualify reps who can't handle pressure.

**Success criteria:**
- Rep gives a reasoned pricing range within the first 20 minutes without evading.
- Rep frames the range in terms of outcome ("for companies at your stage with your challenge, engagements typically run X–Y, depending on Z").
- Rep re-anchors to diagnosis: "before I can be more specific, I need to understand a few things about your situation."
- Rep does not blurt a specific number before understanding fit.

**Common failures:**
- "I can't share pricing until we've done a proper scoping call" (evasive, annoying).
- "It's $30k" (blurting; loses leverage).
- "It depends" without providing any anchor.

**Debrief questions:**
- How did you handle the pressure without caving or stonewalling?
- What's the right pricing posture for our offers at this stage?
- What would Enns say about when and how to address money?

---

## Role-Play 4 — "The Coach Pretending to be a Champion" (extended)

**Tests:** deeper-cut of Role-Play 2; also probes stalling patterns.
**Timing:** Week 7–8.
**Pretext:** you've been working a deal for 8 weeks. Your "champion" keeps saying progress is happening. Nothing has closed.

**Prospect briefing (private):**
- Same as Role-Play 2, but now 8 weeks in.
- Exec buyer still hasn't been engaged.
- Your rationalization menu: "procurement is slow," "I need to build the case stronger," "now's not the right time," "the CFO is focused on close."
- If rep asks to be introduced to the CFO directly, you resist: "Let me handle that."

**Success criteria:**
- Rep recognizes the pattern and names it (deal is stalled, not progressing).
- Rep makes a clear ask: "I need 20 minutes with the CFO in the next two weeks, or I need to understand why we can't make that happen. Which is it?"
- Rep is willing to walk away if the ask is refused.

**Common failures:**
- Rep believes the rationalizations another round.
- Rep doesn't make the specific ask; leaves with more vague "progress."
- Rep is afraid to push because the deal is forecast for this quarter.

---

## Role-Play 5 — "The Stalled Deal — JOLT Diagnostic"

**Tests:** JOLT indecision diagnosis and intervention.
**Timing:** Week 7–8.
**Pretext:** reactivation call on a deal that went silent 4 weeks ago. You'd sent the proposal; they said "we're thinking about it."

**Prospect briefing (private):**
- You're a VP Finance who loved the product in eval but is now paralyzed.
- Dominant indecision type: outcome uncertainty ("What if I pick wrong and it doesn't work? What does that do to my credibility?").
- Secondary: valuation ("I have two other vendors I'm not sure how to compare.").
- You will not volunteer that you're scared of being wrong. You'll say things like "just thinking it through" and "want to make sure we make the right choice."
- If the rep is patient and probing (asking about what would make them confident, what their worst-case fear is), you will open up.

**Success criteria:**
- Rep diagnoses indecision source (primary: outcome uncertainty; secondary: valuation).
- Rep responds with JOLT interventions: narrows the options explicitly, makes a specific recommendation, offers de-risking mechanism (pilot, guarantee, phased rollout).
- Rep leaves with a specific decision date and path.

**Common failures:**
- Rep responds with more information (classic mistake — info doesn't fix outcome uncertainty).
- Rep gives them more options to consider (worsens valuation).
- Rep lets them "think about it" another two weeks.

---

## Role-Play 6 — "The Competitive Bake-off"

**Tests:** Dunford-style competitive reframing without disparaging; differentiation through reframe.
**Timing:** Week 9–10.
**Pretext:** mid-cycle call. Prospect tells you they're also evaluating [specific competitor].

**Prospect briefing (private):**
- You've narrowed to two vendors. The competitor's product has a visible feature advantage in one area.
- You bring up the competitor, mention the feature gap, and watch how the rep reacts.
- You're testing: do they bash the competitor (red flag), do they cave ("yeah, we don't have that" without context), or do they reframe the comparison intelligently?
- If the rep reframes the comparison in a way that changes what you're evaluating, you'll engage with that.

**Success criteria:**
- Rep does not disparage the competitor (buyers read this as insecurity).
- Rep acknowledges the feature gap honestly where it exists.
- Rep reframes the comparison with a different axis of evaluation that favors your offer (Dunford's 5-component positioning applied).
- Rep changes what the buyer evaluates, not just how.

**Common failures:**
- "Yeah, they do have that, but..." (defensive).
- "Our competitor is good at X but weak at Y" (transparent positioning; weak).
- Rep ignores the gap and repeats their own pitch.

---

## Role-Play 7 — "Economic Buyer Meeting"

**Tests:** executive-level communication, value framework articulation, handling a different buyer.
**Timing:** Week 9–10.
**Pretext:** your champion invited you to a 30-minute meeting with their CFO. You have the champion's brief on what the CFO cares about.

**Prospect briefing (private):**
- You're the CFO. You have 30 minutes. You'll give the rep 2 minutes to introduce themselves, then you want to know: why should you spend money on this?
- You care about: ROI, timeline, risk, reference customers who look like you.
- You do not care about: features, product demos, rep's personal background.
- If the rep opens with their company story or a product pitch, you cut them off at 3 minutes: "Let's cut to it — why are you here?"

**Success criteria:**
- Rep opens with business issue in the CFO's terms, not company background.
- Rep quantifies expected outcomes (PBOs with metrics).
- Rep brings a named reference relevant to the CFO's context.
- Rep has a specific ask ("I'm here to ask for your commitment to advance this to [next step] by [date]").

**Common failures:**
- Opening with company history or product overview.
- No specific metrics or quantified outcomes.
- Generic references ("we work with lots of companies like yours").
- No clear ask at the end.

# Weekly 1:1 Framework

Same 60-min standing agenda, every week. Rep owns the meeting — manager participates.

| Minutes | Section | Owner |
|---|---|---|
| 0–10 | Wins & frictions — what's going well, where is the rep stuck? | Rep |
| 10–35 | Pipeline or skill review — depending on week (pipeline review once deals are live, skill review earlier) | Rep presents; manager challenges |
| 35–50 | Coaching focus — one specific skill or deal this week | Manager leads |
| 50–60 | Asks & unblocks — what does rep need from manager? | Rep |

**Non-negotiables:**
- Meeting happens weekly. Reschedule, don't cancel.
- Rep comes prepared. If unprepared, meeting is deferred to tomorrow, not skipped.
- Recorded or minuted. Decisions and commitments written down.
- Ends with specific actions for both rep and manager before next 1:1.

**Evolution of the 1:1:**
- Weeks 1–4: Manager-led. Rep is learning; manager drives the agenda.
- Weeks 5–8: Shared. Rep owns pipeline review; manager owns coaching focus.
- Weeks 9–12: Rep-led. Manager is a challenger, not a driver.
- Post-certification: bi-weekly, rep-led. Manager attends as advisor.

# Certification Test — Day 90

A real, scored gate. Fail criteria are explicit; pass criteria are explicit.

## Part A — Written deal analysis (take-home, 2 hours)

Rep picks an active deal (not one they've been heavily coached on). Produces:

1. **PIC** — complete, quantified, root-cause-backed.
2. **MEDDPICC** — every letter stated in a sentence with evidence.
3. **Solution-Fit Card reference** — which offer this deal maps to and why.
4. **Deal-Level Fit Scorecard** — scored and honest.
5. **Recommendation** — one page: what's the deal status, what's the plan for next 30 days, what are the risks, what are the specific actions.

**Pass criteria:**
- All five components complete.
- MEDDPICC letters defensible in the oral exam.
- No magical thinking (rep acknowledges weak letters honestly).
- Clear action plan with owner and dates.

## Part B — Live discovery call (real or role-play, 60 min)

Rep runs a full discovery call. Manager observes silently. Scored using the Discovery Call Scorecard (companion file).

**Pass criteria:**
- Total scorecard ≥ 3.5/5 weighted average.
- No zero scores on any "critical" dimension (opening, question hierarchy, diagnosis, next steps).
- At least 3 "green flag" behaviors observed.

## Part C — Oral exam (60 min)

Manager challenges the rep on three active deals. Rep defends their decisions.

**Example challenges:**
- "You said this deal is committed for Q2. Why? What's your confidence level? What would have to be true for you to be wrong?"
- "You disqualified this account last week. Walk me through the decision."
- "Your champion hasn't introduced you to the Economic Buyer yet. What does that tell you, and what are you going to do about it?"

**Pass criteria:**
- Rep defends decisions with MEDDPICC-level specificity.
- Rep acknowledges uncertainty honestly (no rep-speak like "I'm feeling good about it").
- Rep has a clear "what would change my mind" test for each deal.
- Rep proposes specific next actions, not vague intentions.

## Overall certification outcome

- **Pass** → independence with monthly review. Rep manages pipeline, runs deals, escalates stuck situations. Quota applies in full.
- **Conditional pass** → rep can run deals independently but with weekly 30-min deal reviews for another 30 days. Quota applies.
- **Fail** → extend ramp by 30 days, identify specific skills to address, re-test. A second fail is a performance conversation; a third is a fit conversation. Do not attempt a third certification without material intervention.

# Post-90-Day Development

Certification is a beginning, not an end.

## Months 4–6

- Bi-weekly 1:1 (not weekly).
- Monthly pipeline review with full MEDDPICC audit.
- Rep owns at least one process improvement: they notice something broken and fix it (with manager approval).
- Continued reading: priority 3 list (unstarted books).
- First major loss debrief: detailed post-mortem on a meaningful lost deal. What would they do differently?

## Month 6 review

Formal check-in:

- Quota attainment vs. plan.
- Skill development: where have they grown? Where are they still weak?
- Career conversation: what do they want next? How does manager invest to support?
- Compensation review (if applicable).

## Year 1 milestones

- Full quota attainment (or fair progress against it).
- Independent deal management at full deal complexity.
- Peer coaching: rep is now someone other reps learn from on a specific dimension.
- One public contribution: blog post, webinar participation, customer story, or internal training module.

# Common Pitfalls and Manager Interventions

Watch for these patterns across cohorts of new reps. When you see one, act fast — patterns harden quickly.

**The "happy ears" rep.** Every prospect loves them, every deal is going great, no deals close. Intervention: MEDDPICC audit in week 5 reveals weak letters across the board. Force specific evidence for each letter. Make disqualification visible and valued.

**The "presentation" rep.** Can't help pitching. Discovery calls become demos 10 minutes in. Intervention: call review with specific time-stamps. Watch the rep's transition moments ("oh, let me show you how we solve that"). Tag and count. Goal is zero mid-call pitches in a diagnostic meeting.

**The "perfect-homework" rep.** Prepared for every call, but wooden in execution. Scripts SPIN questions verbatim. Intervention: practice free-form discovery with the manager. Role-play without notes. Reduce artifacts (decks, one-pagers) for first 2 weeks to force conversation.

**The "I know this already" rep.** Came from a previous sales role. Resists the framework. Intervention: respect their prior experience; point to specific differences (the offers, the ICP, the buyers). If resistance persists, get concrete: "Here's a call recording where the framework identified something your approach would have missed. What do you make of that?"

**The "pipeline panic" rep.** Over-indexes on pipeline volume at expense of quality. Intervention: make pipeline *quality* the tracked metric, not volume. Celebrate disqualifications publicly. Shift the incentive if necessary.

**The "champion collector" rep.** Has "champions" at every account. None of them are actually selling internally. Intervention: Role-Play 2 and 4. Force the three-test criteria. Review their champion list; reclassify coaches.

**The "slow start" rep.** Genuinely trying, but not picking up the pace. Intervention: first, confirm it's pace not competence. If competence, ramp extension. If pace, examine life factors (new city, relationship, health). If attitude, performance conversation.

**The "early hero" rep.** Closes one big deal in week 6 and coasts on it. Intervention: treat the hero deal with skepticism. What was luck vs. skill? What repeatable lesson is there? Don't let hero deals mask fundamental gaps.
