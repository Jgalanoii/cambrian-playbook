# ICP — Professional Services, Consulting & Agencies Playbook

> Vertical playbook for consultancies, agencies, advisory firms, fractional executives, and solo practitioners. Unusually meta — this is the vertical Cambrian Catalyst itself lives in, so the examples skew toward our own category.

## 1. What makes professional services distinct

Five structural features that make ICP work different here than in product businesses:

**You sell trust, and trust sells you.** The buyer can't fully evaluate the service before purchase. They're buying the practitioner's judgment, reputation, and track record. This makes reference and referral flow the dominant acquisition channel at every price point — more than marketing, more than outbound. ICP work must account for the *referral network topology*, not just account characteristics.

**The service itself is the product — and that's a trap.** Pure time-for-money arrangements don't scale. The durable firms productize: named methodologies, packaged engagements, diagnostic-to-roadmap-to-execution sequences. ICP work is downstream of this productization: the sharper the product, the sharper the ICP.

**Leverage ratios define economics.** Solo practitioners have 1:0 leverage (all founder time). Boutique firms have 1:1–1:3 (partner plus associates). Traditional firms have 1:5+ (partner plus team pyramid). Each leverage ratio implies a different ICP, pricing model, and margin structure. Changing leverage = changing the business.

**Case studies are the marketing.** In most B2B, case studies are one asset among many. In services, they are the asset — the proof that a buyer like this succeeded with a practitioner like you. A firm without case studies has no ICP. A firm with case studies in specific verticals/sizes/situations has an ICP implied by its body of work, whether it articulates one or not.

**Commoditization pressure is relentless.** "Consulting" and "agency" are generic categories; the buyer's default assumption is that vendors are interchangeable. Positioning either specializes (vertical, function, outcome) or competes on price. There is no third path that lasts.

## 2. Sub-category matters

"Professional services" spans meaningfully different businesses. Name which you are:

- **Management consulting (strategy)** — McKinsey/Bain/BCG at the top; boutiques below; sells strategic frameworks to executives.
- **Management consulting (operations / implementation)** — Deloitte/Accenture/PwC and mid-tier; sells delivery alongside strategy.
- **Functional advisory / fractional executive** — Fractional CFO/CMO/CRO/CPO; sells capability into gaps.
- **GTM / RevOps consultancies** *(Cambrian Catalyst's primary category)* — sells GTM architecture, RevOps, positioning, demand gen systems, often to funded startups and scale-ups.
- **Agencies (creative, digital, brand, PR)** — sells campaign execution; project- or retainer-based.
- **SI / implementation partners** — tied to specific technology vendors; sells platform deployment.
- **Accounting / tax / legal advisory** — license-gated professions.
- **Engineering / architecture / design firms** — technical professional services.
- **Research / insights / analyst firms** — sells research outputs and access.
- **Staffing / contract** — sells named individuals on assignment; often commoditized.
- **Executive search / recruiting** — sells placements and network access.
- **Investment banking / M&A advisory** — sells transaction expertise and relationships.
- **Private equity / VC operating partners** — hybrid capital and advisory.

Each has different buyer psychology, pricing mechanics, and ICP. The rest of this doc speaks primarily to **GTM/functional consulting, fractional executive work, and boutique advisory** — Cambrian Catalyst's neighborhood.

## 3. ICP patterns for boutique consulting / fractional / advisory

Slicing dimensions:

- **Firmographic**: company stage, funding, revenue, headcount.
- **Functional gap / trigger**: what capability is missing? (No CRO yet, weak marketing function, no RevOps, etc.)
- **Readiness to engage outside help**: has the executive team worked with consultants before? Successfully?
- **Decision authority**: founder-led vs. professional CEO vs. PE-appointed management vs. corporate. Buying dynamics differ dramatically.
- **Board / investor influence**: is the board pushing for outside help? PE firms often mandate operating partner engagements.
- **Budget posture**: is there a services line item, or will this be carved out? Carved-out budgets close slower.
- **Cultural fit**: some organizations are allergic to consultants and will fight implementation. Filter for receptivity.
- **Engagement shape fit**: project vs. retainer vs. fractional vs. interim — which matches their need?
- **Adjacent vendor ecosystem**: do they already work with complementary vendors/partners who could refer you?

Test: a working consulting ICP can be expressed as "companies with [specific situation X], led by [specific role Y], experiencing [specific compelling event Z], with [specific budget/authority posture]." If the ICP statement is "growth-stage B2B SaaS," it's not operational.

## 4. The buying committee

Smaller than enterprise software but politically intricate:

- **Economic buyer** — CEO/founder (most common for fractional/advisory); CRO/CMO/CFO (for functional engagements); PE operating partner (for PE-backed companies); Board Chair (for board-driven mandates).
- **Champion** — typically same as economic buyer; sometimes a functional leader who advocated for outside help.
- **Executive team influence** — other C-level peers can veto on politics, scope overlap, or personal preference.
- **Procurement / legal** — MSA, SOW, rate card, conflict-of-interest, IP ownership, indemnification.
- **Finance** — approves scope, burn rate, and committed hours.
- **Internal function being "helped"** — critical; if they feel threatened or bypassed, engagement fails in delivery even if it closes.
- **Board members** — for strategic engagements, often involved or at least informed.
- **Investors** — particularly active in VC/PE portfolio companies.
- **Existing advisors / board observers** — can refer in, endorse, or block.

Pattern: consulting deals close on *relationship* and deliver on *function*. A buyer who bought you for the relationship but whose team resents the engagement has bought a failed project. ICP must include "organizational receptivity," not just "decision-maker readiness."

## 5. Trigger events

Compelling events that put a buyer in-market for outside help:

- **New executive joining (CEO, CRO, CMO, CFO)** — new leader wants an outside assessment or augmentation in first 6 months. Top trigger in this category.
- **Funding round closed** — Series A/B/C/D creates both budget and scaling pressure. Consulting spend spikes in the 3–6 months post-close.
- **Missed quarter / miss number** — creates urgency; CEO wants an outside voice.
- **Board mandate** — "hire someone to help us with X." Often results in a consultant introduction from the board.
- **PE platform acquisition or add-on** — operating partner playbook often includes GTM consultants, fractional execs, or specialized boutiques.
- **Executive departure** — CRO/CMO leaves; fractional or interim fills the gap until permanent hire (often 6–12 months).
- **Go-to-market motion change** — product expansion, segment expansion, geography expansion, channel launch.
- **Pre-raise preparation** — founder preparing for a round wants GTM story, metrics, and positioning cleaned up.
- **M&A / exit preparation** — due diligence readiness, revenue quality improvement.
- **Strategic inflection** — entering new category, responding to competitive threat, considering a pivot.
- **Board-observed performance gap** — CAC ballooning, conversion plateau, pipeline coverage issue.

Rule: consulting engagements rarely close without a named trigger. "We probably should think about X" rarely converts. "We just hired a new CRO who wants a GTM assessment" closes.

## 6. Positioning — unique-attribute categories

Durable differentiation for consultancies and fractional executives:

- **Named principal / operator credibility** — real operating experience at credible companies, ideally in the vertical or stage the buyer is in. "Did what you're trying to do" beats "studied what you're trying to do."
- **Vertical depth** — fintech, payments, healthtech, marketplaces, etc. Named logos served.
- **Functional depth** — GTM, RevOps, product marketing, demand gen, sales enablement, CS, etc.
- **Stage depth** — seed-to-Series-A, Series-B-to-C, scale-up, PE-backed growth, pre-exit. Each stage requires different muscle.
- **Methodology / frameworks** — named, repeatable approach visible in public content. Buyers want to see *how you think* before they engage.
- **Body of work** — case studies, published content, talks, interviews.
- **Network access** — some buyers pay for the practitioner's rolodex as much as their brain.
- **Engagement shape** — fractional, project, retainer, interim, advisory — match what the buyer needs.
- **Outcome orientation** — quantified results from prior work; willingness to price against outcomes.
- **Productized offering** — named engagements ("Revenue Architecture Program," "GTM Diagnostic," "Fractional CRO Sprint") that the buyer can visualize.

Positioning that reads "strategic GTM advisor helping B2B companies grow revenue" loses to positioning that reads "GTM operator who built the ABM motion at [named company] — now running a 12-week Revenue Architecture Program for Series B/C fintech companies." Specificity is the wedge.

## 7. Pricing & model patterns

Common structures and their ICP implications:

- **Hourly** — least scalable; signals junior practitioner or commodity work. Only works for short, specified tasks.
- **Project / fixed-fee** — defined scope, defined deliverables, defined outcome. Requires tight scoping discipline.
- **Monthly retainer** — defined hours or defined outcomes per month; predictable revenue. Common for fractional exec work.
- **Value-based / outcome-based** — priced on results delivered (revenue lift, cost saved, placement made, equity upside). Harder to sell upfront but highest margin when it works.
- **Productized services** — fixed scope + fixed fee + fixed timeline offerings, repeated across clients. Highest leverage.
- **Subscription advisory** — monthly access, limited hours, bundle of services. Ascendant model (Dan Martell's *Coaching*, Alan Weiss's *Million Dollar Consulting*, newer models from MKT1, GTM Partners).
- **Equity / revenue share** — for fractional execs and advisors; used as deal-closer or primary comp.
- **Hybrid** — common: cash retainer + outcome bonus + equity kicker.

Pricing strategy *is* ICP strategy: hourly pricing attracts small, tactical engagements; project pricing attracts defined-problem buyers; retainers attract ongoing relationship buyers; value pricing attracts outcome-focused buyers. You can't serve all four with the same offer and positioning.

## 8. The "referral economy" — a dimension most ICP frameworks miss

In professional services, the referral graph *is* the ICP machine:

- **Who refers you?** — Former colleagues, former clients, adjacent service providers (lawyers, accountants, bankers), investors, board members.
- **Who do they refer to you?** — Their network, which has high demographic/firmographic correlation with their own profile. If your referrers are Series B SaaS CROs, your referrals will mostly be Series B SaaS CROs.
- **What do they refer for?** — The problem they've seen you solve. Referrers don't refer generically; they refer for specific situations.
- **How do you maintain the graph?** — Keeping-in-touch cadence, public work that keeps you salient, collaborative engagements with adjacent providers.

Practical implication: your ICP is *also* a statement of what your referrers think you do. If your best clients are "Series B SaaS CROs," your referrers must know that. If you try to broaden your ICP without first broadening your referrers' mental model of you, you'll get low-quality referrals outside your real sweet spot.

Corollary for early-stage consultants: your first 3–5 clients *define* your ICP for the next 2–3 years because they'll be your primary referrers. Choose them with this in mind.

## 9. Archetypal ICP slices

Tailored to the GTM consulting / fractional / boutique neighborhood:

**A — "Series B B2B SaaS, GTM scale-up moment" *(Cambrian Catalyst home ICP)***
US-based B2B SaaS (fintech-adjacent, payments, martech, or similar), Series B with $10M–$30M raised, $3M–$15M ARR, CEO still founder or recently-hired CEO, no CRO yet (or newly-hired CRO with <6 months tenure), running growth via founder sales + 1–3 AEs, has named pipeline or conversion problem, board pressure to professionalize GTM. Trigger: Series B close, new CRO hire, board-mandated assessment, or missed quarter.

**B — "PE-backed mid-market GTM transformation"**
PE platform company, $25M–$100M revenue, 2–5 years post-acquisition, PE firm is push-oriented (not sleepy hold), CEO or CRO under pressure to hit EBITDA or revenue targets, prior consulting engagements have occurred, operating partner involved. Trigger: PE operating partner mandate, CEO replacement, add-on acquisition, or exit prep.

**C — "Founder-led, pre-CRO, pipeline crisis"**
Bootstrapped or seed/A SaaS, $1M–$5M ARR, founder still running sales, just-hired 1–2 AEs who are struggling, no marketing leader, no RevOps, no SDR motion, founder is bottleneck and burnt out, considering either "hire a head of sales" or "get help figuring out what to build first." Trigger: founder burnout, failed sales hire, or funding preparation.

**D — "Newly-hired CMO/CRO, first-90-day assessment"**
B2B company, $20M–$200M revenue, new functional leader (CMO, CRO, CPO) in first 90 days, needs an outside voice to validate or challenge their initial assessment, has discretionary budget for a short engagement, wants deliverables for their CEO/board. Trigger: executive hire, quarterly business review, or annual planning.

**E — "Fintech/payments vertical specialist engagement"**
Fintech or payments company at any stage, B2B-focused (BaaS, embedded finance, vertical SaaS with payments, processor, etc.), needs vertical-native GTM expertise that pure horizontal consultants lack, has specific domain problem (ICP definition in fintech, payments pricing strategy, sponsor bank narrative, etc.). Trigger: vertical expansion, pricing crisis, or differentiation initiative.

## 10. Common failure modes

- **Undifferentiated positioning.** "Growth advisor helping B2B companies" is invisible. Specialization is mandatory.
- **No productization.** Every engagement a snowflake = no leverage, no referrals, no scale. Name your engagements.
- **Pricing by the hour.** Signals commodity; caps economics; penalizes efficiency.
- **No case studies / body of work.** Without visible proof, sales cycles drag or die.
- **Ignoring the referral graph.** Marketing spend beats referral investment in most advisors' plans; this is almost always wrong.
- **Broadening ICP to fill the pipeline.** Chasing deals outside your sweet spot attracts wrong-fit clients, produces weak outcomes, damages referrals, and confuses positioning.
- **Over-delivering into unpaid scope.** Scope creep is the silent margin killer. Discipline required.
- **Not firing bad-fit clients.** Bad-fit clients drag delivery and damage referrals (Murphy's rule applies here too).
- **No repeatable methodology.** Each engagement reinventing the wheel = no IP, no leverage, no productization.
- **Weak founder-market fit.** The practitioner has to credibly match the buyer. A fintech GTM consultant with no fintech history sells harder than one with 10 years in the space.
- **Late to monetize the asset.** Consultants often under-price until forced by demand. By the time they raise rates, they've trained their market to expect the old price.

## 11. Applied to Cambrian Catalyst specifically

Meta-notes for the firm using this doc against itself:

- **Primary ICP**: Archetype A (Series B B2B SaaS, fintech/payments/martech-adjacent, pre- or early-CRO, GTM scale-up moment). Archetypes C and E are viable secondaries.
- **Referral graph priority**: Blackhawk/Tango alumni network, former colleagues in fintech/payments, fintech VC partner networks, fractional CRO/CMO peer network, SaaS operator peers. Investment of time here likely outperforms paid marketing.
- **Productization next step**: "Revenue Architecture Program" is a good name for the diagnostic → roadmap → execution sequence; productize each phase with fixed scope, fixed price, fixed timeline. Build visible IP (frameworks, public writing, talks) around it.
- **Positioning specificity**: "GTM operator for fintech / payments / B2B SaaS scale-ups" is sharper than "GTM consultant." Further sharpen with a named insight: "Fintech GTM is broken because [specific insight]; here's how we fix it." That insight *is* the Dunford sales-pitch setup.
- **Pricing posture**: Avoid hourly. Use project pricing for diagnostics, monthly retainer for ongoing, outcome kicker for scale-up execution. Consider equity-plus-cash for the right early anchor clients.
- **Case study discipline**: Every engagement should produce a publishable (even if anonymized) case study asset. Budget time for this in every SOW.
- **Disqualification is protection**: Early-stage founders without compelling events, first-time-consultant-buyers who seem likely to resent the engagement, and companies without executive sponsorship will consume disproportionate time and produce weak outcomes. Decline them or deflect to a less-intensive offer.
